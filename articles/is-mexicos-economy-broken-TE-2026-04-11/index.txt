3:T2ae0,<p><span><span><span>A selection of correspondence</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Is Mexico’s economy broken?</span></span></p><p><span><span>Also this week, costs of the Iran war, sex workers, types of tourists, boredom at work, fizzy water</span></span></p><p><span><span>Apr 09, 2026 05:09 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-04-11-00025.jpg"/></p><p><span>Letters are welcome via email to </span><a href="mailto:letters@economist.com"><span>letters@economist.com</span></a><br/><a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2025/03/26/a-note-for-readers"><span>Find out more</span></a><span> about how we process your letter</span></p><hr/><p><span>Mexico is making progress</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/the-americas/2026/03/26/mexicos-broken-economy">You were right to focus on weak growth and entrenched informality in the labour market as factors behind “Mexico’s broken economy”</a> (“Becalmed”, March 28th). These remain central constraints on the economy and help explain the country’s underperformance over time.</p><p>Concurrently, over roughly the past decade Mexico has combined falling poverty with rising real wages and a noticeable reduction in informal labour. The minimum wage is part of that story. During successive governments under the Morena party the minimum wage has risen sharply, now sitting high relative to the median wage. That has lifted incomes at the bottom and appears to have influenced pay even beyond the formal sector. </p><p>Here the narrative is less clear. You noted that a higher minimum wage could put a brake on formal labour. But these trends have evolved. Rather than rising, informality has edged down, suggesting a more complex set of dynamics at work. Several forces are moving at once. Changes in labour regulation, some tightening of enforcement and the expansion of social programmes may all have shifted incentives at the margin. None of these on their own explains the outcome. Taken together, they begin to.</p><p>The question is how far this can go. With the minimum wage now elevated by <span>OECD</span> standards, the space for further increases without side-effects is probably narrower. </p><p><span>WILL WISEMAN</span><br/>Practice manager for social policy, Latin AmericaWorld Bank<br/><span>Washington,</span>
<span><span>DC</span></span></p><p>You hit on a fundamental truth that the current administration in Mexico seems to overlook: the power of incentives. The government’s approach is like trying to squeeze more juice out of the same handful of lemons rather than planting more trees. By constantly raising mandated salaries and increasing the burden on existing taxpayers, they are inadvertently making “formality” a luxury many cannot afford.</p><p>In simple terms, we have created a system where it is expensive to follow the rules and free to ignore them. For a small business owner or an entrepreneur, the maths is devastatingly simple. Why would someone choose to become “formal”—paying taxes, social security and meeting high-wage mandates—when they can stay “informal” and keep that overhead as profit? When the cost of being legal rises, the incentive to stay in the shadows grows.</p><p>If the government truly wants to unleash the private sector, the policy should not be about extraction, but attraction. We must make it cheaper and easier to be formal than it is to be informal.</p><p><span>PABLO EMILIO MUÑOZ </span><br/><span>Ciudad Juárez, Mexico</span></p><p>The Mexican government’s formal response to the article can be found at <a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2026/04/02/mexicos-ministry-of-finance-responds-to-our-article-on-the-economy">www.economist.com/mexicoletter</a></p><hr/><p><span>The view from Mauritius</span></p><p>“After shock” (March 14th) catalogued <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/03/10/donald-trumps-options-to-cool-oil-prices-are-sorely-limited">the constraints facing America, the world’s most powerful oil consumer, as it tried to cool the oil price amid the Iran conflict</a>. Permit me to describe the view from a rather different position.</p><p>Mauritius imports all of its fuel. It holds no strategic petroleum reserve. It has no pipeline alternatives, no bilateral waiver from Washington, and no seat at the International Energy Agency. Since the closure of the Strait of Hormuz in early March, pump prices have risen sharply and freight costs on Indian Ocean routes have roughly tripled. The government has no meaningful policy lever beyond absorbing the shock or passing it to consumers who can least afford it.</p><p>The energy-security debate triggered by the war has been framed almost entirely around the options available to major importers, diversification, reserve releases and accelerated renewables build-out. These are legitimate responses for economies with the institutional capacity and fiscal space to pursue them. For small island states, they describe a future that is structurally inaccessible in the near term.</p><p>Energy security is increasingly determined by the ability to pay for it. The war has made that asymmetry visible. It has not made it a policy priority.</p><p><span>DR ZAHEER ALLAM</span><br/><span>Vacoas-Phoenix, Mauritius</span></p><hr/><p><span>Researching sex workers</span></p><p>I have been managing a high-volume brothel in Europe since 2016, and I am also a researcher focusing on <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/03/19/the-new-economics-of-sex-work">sex work as a form of labour</a>. I read your column on the economics of the industry with interest (Free Exchange, March 21st). One point that stood out in the article is how difficult sex work is to study. What is often missing in research is how this looks from the other side. Sex workers are used to valuing their time highly. When a researcher approaches them, often without real experience in the industry, and asks detailed questions about income it creates a barrier. Income is very private information, especially when a large part of it is in cash.</p><p>Because of this, a lot of research focuses on the most accessible groups, which are often at the lower end of the income range. A large group of higher-earning workers is mostly missing from the data, because they are difficult to reach and have little reason to take part.</p><p>The economic framing of much of the research is also too narrow. Sex work is not always just money for sex. It can also involve housing, services, goods, or access to opportunities. This makes the economic reality more complex than it may seem. You only touched on legal frameworks and new technologies, but these areas have their own dynamics that shape how the market works.</p><p><span>CATHERINE DE NOIRE</span><br/><span>Amsterdam</span></p><hr/><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-04-11-00064.jpg"/></p><p><span>Roam if you want to</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2026/03/19/war-is-only-the-starkest-way-that-politics-is-disrupting-tourism">Your taxonomy of modern travellers (“Wanderlust and lost”, March 21st) has a distinguished precursor</a>. Around 1860 Hippolyte Taine, a French historian and philosopher, divided tourists into six types. These were: people obsessed by the number of miles they covered; devotees of the details given in guidebooks; group travellers wary of foreign food; gastronomic pilgrims with the sole purpose of eating; antique hunters; and those who “see the Pyrenees” from a hotel chair. Were he writing today, Taine might have added a seventh type: the algorithmic tourist, dutifully shepherded by apps into the same “hidden gems” as everyone else.</p><p>Astonishingly, the species has changed little. Travellers then mostly had to endure bad inns and worse roads. As your article recounted, today’s tourists must also reckon with visa bans, closed airspace and wars.<br/><span>ZUBIN AIBARA</span><br/><span>Bülach, Switzerland</span></p><hr/><p><span>Dare to be bored</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/03/12/in-praise-of-grunt-work">The real risk of getting rid of grunt work</a> (Bartleby, March 14th) is the loss of the cognitive “lulls” that drudgery once provided. As Sandi Mann, an occupational psychologist, has noted, boredom is often the precursor to divergent thinking. If <span>AI</span> eliminates the natural toil of legacy processes, we may have to design deliberate periods of low-stimulation downtime into our workflows. This would ensure that the next generation has the mental space to be truly innovative, rather than just efficiently supervised.</p><p><span>PETER BALCHIUS</span><br/><span>Stäfa, Switzerland</span></p><p>In his praise of grunt work, Bartleby argues that we should be wary of an over reliance on <span>AI</span> to eliminate tedious work. There is, however, an important difference between work that builds skill and work that simply drains energy. Some tedious tasks may teach patience or train us in attention to detail, but much office drudgery consists of unnecessary busywork: fixing formats, hunting down files and repeating mechanical steps that software easily handles. In that context, <span>AI</span> is not replacing meaningful labour so much as removing needless obstacles. The real question is what do we do with the gains? More output is one answer; fewer working hours may be the better one.</p><p><span>ISAAC SHAMIE</span><br/><span>San Francisco</span></p><p>It is true that mind-numbing tasks can unlock creativity and satisfaction. This explains why many people in management or professional services were remarkably happier working from home during the pandemic. Sitting through endless PowerPoint meetings, where colleagues toss around concepts to hit personal airtime targets, achieving nothing beyond a vague sense of “alignment”, is deeply frustrating to the sane mind. Interspersing these torturous “intellectual” exercises with washing the dishes or making the bed offered a necessary respite. These simple chores provided what modern office life rarely does: an actual problem solved to completion, a genuine sense of agency, and the mental space for an occasional eureka moment.</p><p><span>ELMAR GANS</span><br/><span>Rio de Janeiro</span></p><hr/><p><span>The biz of fizz</span></p><p>I enjoyed your <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2026/03/19/which-is-the-best-sparkling-water">World In a Dish column on sparkling water</a> (March 21st). You featured Topo Chico, which is excellent and has the angriest bubbles on the market. But I find it inexplicable that you neglected to mention Vichy Catalan. Silky, elegant, a touch of salinity, it is without question the best sparkling water on earth. I will die on this hill.</p><p><span>DR JULIO FRIEDMANN</span><br/>Carbon Direct<br/><span>Livermore, California</span></p>6:["slug","is-mexicos-economy-broken-TE-2026-04-11","d"]
