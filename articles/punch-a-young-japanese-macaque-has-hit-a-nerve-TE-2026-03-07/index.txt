He’s a knockout<h1>Punch, a young Japanese macaque, has hit a nerve</h1>A lonely monkey is beloved by millions <p>3月 05, 2026 07:15 上午</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00068.jpg" title=" FEBRUARY 20 : Baby monkey named 'Punch' is seen with a stuffed animal at a zoo"/>
<p><b>H</b><span>IS TROUBLES </span>began the moment he was born. His mother rejected him. He struggled to fit in with his peers: they bullied him and dragged him around by his hair. His only source of comfort was a plushy orangutan toy.</p>
<p>This is the story of Punch, a seven-month-old Japanese macaque at Ichikawa City Zoo, who has become a global sensation. Posts about him have appeared 630m times on Reddit, YouTube and X, finds Sprout Social, an analytics firm. Some want to adopt Punch; others to wreak vengeance on his tormentors. An influencer even flew to Japan to check up on him. <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/07/23/ikeas-prints-have-transformed-how-homes-everywhere-look"><span>IKEA</span></a> has sold out of his <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/01/07/millennials-and-gen-z-are-falling-hard-for-stuffed-animals">toy</a> in several countries. As one fan put it: “Punch is the most loved creature on the Earth right now.”</p>
<p>Why has this little guy provoked such a big reaction? One reason is that humans simply cannot help finding him <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2024/02/02/small-but-mighty-how-cuteness-has-taken-over-the-world">cute</a>, says Morten Kringelbach, a neuroscientist at Oxford University. Look at something adorable and “within a seventh of a second, you get activity in the orbitofrontal cortex”, a part of the brain which processes pleasure. This has an evolutionary benefit, motivating humans to protect their young. And Punch, Professor Kringelbach points out, “looks so like a baby, walking around with that soft toy”.</p>
<p>Another reason is cultural: Punch’s story is a classic underdog tale. Life has placed obstacles in his path, this narrative goes, and he is much smaller than his foes. But Punch is resilient and determined to make it in macaque society. When others knock him down, he gets back up. Viewers urge him to #HangInTherePunch.</p>
<p>Is this anthropomorphism gone mad? No, argues Michael Bond, the author of “Animate”, a forthcoming book (and no relation of the creator of Paddington Bear). Punch is a reminder of other species’ emotional intelligence: watch a video of him and you can see that his suffering and sense of rejection are “very similar” to what a human might feel. “It really does change people’s perception of what animals are,” Mr Bond says, and may encourage more people to care about animal welfare. For a tiny monkey, he certainly packs a Punch. ■</p>
