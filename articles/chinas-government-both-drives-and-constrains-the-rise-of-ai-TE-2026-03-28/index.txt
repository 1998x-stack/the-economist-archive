3:T1cd6,<p><span><span><span>Chaguan</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>China’s government both drives and constrains the rise of AI</span></span></p><p><span><span>Officials want to lead the world in the new technology, but are still wary of disruption</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00004.jpg"/></p><p><span>I</span><span>T CAN SEEM</span> as if artificial intelligence is suddenly everywhere in China. From banking to shopping and maps to ride-hailing, almost all major Chinese mobile apps now offer <span>AI</span> chatbots. Universities are creating courses in <span>AI</span>. To shop for a toilet seat is to learn of <span>AI</span>-enhanced options that monitor urine quality. To order yoghurt from an upscale chain involves scanning your face so that its <span>AI</span> model can tailor its fermented product to your needs.</p><p>Of course, <span>AI</span> is not actually everywhere. But this being China, a country that loves presenting positive data, the government will tell you exactly how far it has got: by 2025, 42.8% of the country had “adopted” generative <span>AI</span>, double the share a year earlier. At this rate China will hit its target of a 90% <span>AI</span> “penetration rate” by 2030. Never mind the precise meanings of adoption or penetration. The questionable statistic itself is a small emblem of China’s <span>AI</span> story: the state’s hands-on role in pushing the technology.</p><p>Ever since China’s “<a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/01/27/deepseek-sends-a-shockwave-through-markets">DeepSeek moment</a>” last year, when an unknown startup shocked the world by exhibiting advanced capabilities at a fraction of the cost of Western competitors, attention has focused on the race to build the best <span>AI</span> models. But the more important question may be which country is better at applying <span>AI</span>, not just developing it. China has several things in its favour: open-source models that are cheap and customisable; a manufacturing base with a head start in weaving <span>AI</span> into physical products; and a deep pool of researchers working on the technology.</p><p>Ultimately, though, the factor that most differentiates China from America is its strong state. Much as DeepSeek alerted the world to China’s prowess, China had experienced its own awakening in 2017 when Google DeepMind’s <a href="https://www.economist.com/science-and-technology/2017/10/21/the-latest-ai-can-work-things-out-without-being-taught">AlphaGo beat a leading Go master</a> at the board game. A few months later China published its first national <span>AI-</span>development plan. It is now moving from research to application. Last year the government launched an “<span>AI</span> Plus” initiative to promote it in sectors from health care to energy. Of China’s 700bn yuan ($100bn) of capital spending on <span>AI</span> last year, estimates suggest the government accounted for about 60%.</p><p>But the state’s leading role is not an unambiguous advantage. As soon as the government creates targets, there is a showy element. Officials seek to vaunt their achievements. Chaguan recently visited Yizhuang, a tech hub in Beijing developing “embodied <span>AI</span>”—integrating <span>AI</span> into robots. An industrial park features a restaurant with automated waiters and robot performances. At one point a small robo-triceratops glided along the floor, coyly sidling up to patrons. All very impressive, until Chaguan spotted the man operating the creature with a video-game controller behind his back. Another staff member sheepishly admitted there were no robots in the kitchen. “They still can’t cook well,” he said.</p><p>These rough edges are a reality check about China’s deployment of embodied <span>AI</span>. The best-known examples are the humanoid robots that performed kung fu during the lunar-new-year gala on television, wowing hundreds of millions of viewers. But getting robots to perform a set routine, however elaborate, is not the same as getting them to move around unpredictable environments on their own. An executive with a big Chinese tech firm says that, at their core, the robots are finicky devices which are liable to overheat and wear down. China’s infamous “996” working culture—9am to 9pm, six days a week—might overwhelm them.</p><p>Though the government has embraced <span>AI</span>, it remains conservative in some areas—reassuringly so for those who fret about the technology. In recent weeks a mini-craze swept China as thousands installed OpenClaw, an <span>AI</span> assistant, on their laptops. Unlike <span>AI</span> software that requires prompts, OpenClaw runs autonomously, handling tasks such as replying to emails. But officials swiftly restricted its use in state-owned firms and government work, fearful that it could expose sensitive information—a conclusion that some large Western firms have also reached.</p><p>Chinese officials are worried, too, about jobs. When the government launched its <span>AI</span> Plus initiative it specified that it wanted to steer resources to where labour is scarce and working conditions hazardous. In a meeting with tech executives a senior leader had reacted with alarm to suggestions that <span>AI</span> might lead to mass layoffs, according to someone briefed on the interaction. His message was to avoid disruption in the labour market.</p><p><span>Visible hand</span></p><p>In another way China’s strong state may also impede <span>AI</span>’s spread. A fifth of the country’s workforce is employed in the public sector and state-owned enterprises, which are notoriously slow to adopt new digital technologies. Again, hitting mandated targets can overwhelm the business aim of seeking profits. Following the release of the DeepSeek model, state firms and government agencies were instructed to harness its power. Jeffrey Ding of George Washington University says that many opted for an easy solution: they installed stand-alone machines, separate from their operations. “People used it for a quick <span>PR</span> win. It wasn’t sustainable integration,” he says.</p><p>Indeed, the bigger contribution of the state may be in less glamorous policies, not in unleashing humanoids on the world. On March 13th the Communist Party announced its latest five-year plan, making <span>AI</span> the centrepiece. The plan calls for building high-quality datasets for sectors such as transport and health care. That is a “big deal”, says Kendra Schaefer of Trivium China, a consultancy, because the Chinese state sits on a huge reservoir of data. Granting access to domestic <span>AI</span> firms could give them an edge over foreign competitors. Yet that is a far cry from a country going full-bore on <span>AI</span> in all directions at once. Like most of the world, China believes that its future is bound to <span>AI</span>. But the state’s caution may matter as much as its enthusiasm. ■</p><p><span>Subscribers to The Economist can sign up to our </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/opinion"><span>Opinion newsletter</span></a><span>, which brings together the best of our leaders, columns, guest essays and reader correspondence.</span></p>6:["slug","chinas-government-both-drives-and-constrains-the-rise-of-ai-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
