The world this week<h1>Business</h1><p>6月 18, 2026 03:16 上午</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00004.jpg" title="Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh speaks to reporters"/>
<p>The <b>Federal Reserve</b> left interest rates on hold, its first decision under Kevin Warsh as chairman. With inflationary pressures on the rise, the Fed abandoned the trajectory it maintained at previous meetings towards an easing of monetary policy. No one on the policy committee voted for a rate cut. In an indication of the start of the Warsh era, the central bank’s statement was very short, asserting that it would deliver price stability.</p>
<p>In a busy week for central banks the <b>Bank of England </b>also made no change to interest rates. Two of its nine ratesetters voted for an increase. Before their meeting the latest update to the consumer-price index for Britain showed that the annual rate of inflation was unchanged in May, at 2.8%.</p>
<p>The <b>Bank of Japan</b> lifted its key interest rate from 0.75% to 1%, taking it to its highest level since 1995. After two decades of deflation consumer prices in Japan rose steadily after the pandemic. The annual inflation rate has been well under 2% this year, but higher energy prices as a result of the Iran conflict are driving up costs. Wholesale inflation soared in April and in May, to 6.3%.</p>
<p>The Trump administration <a href="../../feed_4/article_0/index_u28.html">imposed export controls·</a> on <b>Anthropic’s</b> most advanced <span>AI</span>, ordering the firm to block non-Americans from accessing its latest Fable and Mythos models.  The government issued the decree apparently after Andy Jassy, the boss of Amazon, alerted officials to a “jailbreak” his researchers had found in Fable that bypasses safety protocols. Lacking the ability to distinguish between citizen and non-citizen users, Anthropic suspended access to Fable everywhere, even internally. The government’s decree underlined America’s control of <span>AI</span> frontier technology, and heightened angst among America’s allies about how far they lag behind the <span>US</span>.</p>
<p>The European Parliament approved the <b>trade deal with America</b> that was agreed to in principle last year. European legislators postponed previous votes on the pact because of rows with the Trump administration over Greenland and the uncertainty caused by the Supreme Court’s reversal of Donald Trump’s tariffs. Mr Trump had threatened to increase duties on imports of European cars unless the parliament approved the agreement by July 4th.</p>
<p><b><a href="../../feed_13/article_1/index_u6.html">Fox Corporation·</a></b>, an entertainment and news company, advanced its ambitions in streaming by agreeing to buy <b>Roku</b>, which sells streaming hardware and software and operates streaming channels, for $22bn. In 2020 Fox bought Tubi, a free ad-supported streaming service. Last year it launched Fox One, its own app.</p><h4>We have lift-off</h4>
<p>The share price of <b>SpaceX</b> soared by 50% in the days following its initial public offering, lifting it to a market valuation of almost $2.7trn and challenging Amazon for the spot of world’s fifth-biggest listed company (behind Nvidia, Alphabet, Apple and Microsoft). The company raised $85.7bn on its first day of trading once underwriters bought additional shares, smashing the record for the world’s largest-ever <span>IPO</span>. Elon Musk’s personal wealth has jumped to $1.3trn according to Bloomberg. Even if that was reduced by $1trn he would still be the world’s richest person. </p>
<p>Flushed with success, SpaceX agreed to buy <b>Cursor</b>, which uses <span>AI</span> to automate computer coding, for $60bn. The acquisition will allow x<span>AI</span>, Mr Musk’s <span>AI</span> business, which is now owned by SpaceX, to boost its presence in the lucrative business of <span>AI </span>coding for company systems.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00005.png" title=""/>
<p>The <b>oil and gas industry</b> in the Gulf should see a gradual recovery if the  peace deal between America and Iran holds, according to the International Energy Agency. Mines still have to be removed from shipping lanes and supply chains will take time to normalise, it said. The <span>IEA</span> doesn’t expect the market to heal fully until next year, when supplies will surge, lowering the price of oil considerably and allowing countries to restock their depleted reserves.</p>
<p>For years <b>Pizza Hut’s</b> sales have struggled as consumers have become more health conscious, and now its owner, Yum Brands!, has decided to shift some weight by selling the franchise. In two deals Pizza Hut’s 15,000 restaurants outside China were sold to a private-equity company, and the outlets inside China were acquired by Yum’s Chinese holding company. It plans to expand Pizza Hut  in China, where it is already the largest casual-dining brand.</p><h4>When marketing goes wrong</h4>
<p><b>Starbucks Korea</b>, which operates the coffee-chain franchise in South Korea, said all its staff would receive training in historical awareness. The firm faces a backlash after a “Tank Day” marketing gimmick was seen to be making light of a pro-democracy protest in 1980, at which hundreds  were killed. <b>Lululemon</b> also found itself mired in controversy for ignoring local sensitivities, after it held a promotional event on the Great Wall of China that featured a Japanese drum. Amid renewed tensions between China and Japan the yoga-gear-maker apologised, blaming “limitations in our professional knowledge”.</p>
