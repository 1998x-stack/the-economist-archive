3:T16a0,<p><span><span><span>The beat goes on</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The risky plan to take the “K” out of K-pop </span></span></p><p><span><span>Music executives reckon the genre’s future lies outside its home country</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 15, 2026 05:53 AM | SINGAPORE</span></span></p><p><img src="https://www.economist.com/cdn-cgi/image/width=600,quality=80,format=auto/content-assets/images/20260117_CUP505.jpg"/></p><p><span>F</span><span>OR A COOL</span> S$6,500 ($5,050), you can attend a professional course at <span>SM</span> Universe, a new <span>K</span>-pop training academy, opened in Singapore last year. <span>SM</span> Universe is the first facility in South-East Asia with backing from a major music label (<span>SM</span> Entertainment). Its goal is to train the next generation of hitmakers to follow in the choreographed footsteps of some of <span>K</span>-pop’s biggest stars. Students include aspiring performers from France, Germany, Indonesia, Malaysia and the Philippines.</p><p>The academy points to a change in the <a href="https://www.economist.com/the-world-ahead/2025/11/12/tracking-the-worlds-most-popular-music-genres">popular music genre</a>: South Korean pop is becoming less Korean. With demand for <span>K</span>-pop more muted at home, labels are eyeing markets abroad. The musical acts need to reflect their adoring audiences. <span>HYBE, </span>another label, now runs training programmes in Los Angeles and Mumbai. (<span>HYBE</span> is behind <span>BTS</span>, one of the world’s biggest bands, which is reuniting in 2026 for a world tour. <span>BTS</span>’s seven male members had to trade their mics for fatigues and spent nearly four years completing South Korea’s required military service.)</p><p>Going abroad “is a natural progression” for the <span>K</span>-pop industry, says Andy Lim of <span>SM</span> Universe. Artists hoping to widen their appeal already collaborate with American stars. For example, Blackpink, a girl group, has collaborated with artists such as Cardi B, Lady Gaga and Selena Gomez. Many now sing in English rather than Korean. (One of the biggest hits of the past year was “Golden” by Huntrix, the fictional group in <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/09/02/girl-band-v-demons-the-secrets-of-netflixs-most-popular-film">“<span>K</span>Pop Demon Hunters”</a>, the most popular film to ever stream on Netflix.) <span>HYBE</span>’s founder, Bang Si-hyuk, has said the company wants to “get the<span> K</span> out of <span>K</span>-pop”.</p><p>But if <span>K</span>-pop is not pop music from Korea, is it just regular old pop music? Moa “Cazzi Opeia” Carlebecker, a Swedish songwriter who has written <span>K</span>-pop tunes, says the term no longer implies a country of origin or language but “a really fun music style”. It is ebullient, with <a href="https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2023/02/03/max-martin-knows-how-to-create-a-number-one-hit">catchy hooks</a> and polished production. Melodies and transitions are written to be danced to, which is why academies treat choreography with the seriousness of North Korean army drills.</p><p>Each track includes a variety of sounds: <span>K</span>-pop songs often shift from ballad to rap or from pop to <a href="https://www.economist.com/prospero/2019/02/08/the-changing-face-of-electronic-music">electronica</a>. <span>K</span>-pop groups tend to have lots of members, so songs are often structured in a way that gives each a moment in the spotlight. That way, if a fan likes only one member of the band, there is still something for her (<span>K</span>-pop fans are overwhelmingly female).</p><p>Labels are not only exporting that musical style, but also the specific system by which <span>K</span>-pop artists are coached. Training is long, demanding and tightly structured. Hopefuls can spend six to ten hours a day, for years, preparing for their big break. They are starting at ever younger ages—some have made their stage debuts at the age of 13—which some industry observers find concerning. “To be honest, it’s not really healthy,” says Yeonsoo Do, a former star. “But you have to understand how the industry works.”</p><p>For Jason Jaesang Lee, the boss of <span>HYBE</span>, what a chart-topper does offstage is almost as important as what happens under the spotlights. Another crucial aspect of <span>K</span>-pop is “idol” culture and the intense devotion performers inspire in their admirers. Wannabes are judged not just on their voice or their dancing, but also on their discipline, teamwork and public presentation. <span>K</span>-pop training aims to develop talented kids into charismatic stars who “can connect deeply with their fans”, Mr Lee says. Students are taught the art of the selfie and how to win fans and influence them.</p><p>Early attempts at casting a wider net for talent have been successful. Katseye—a girl group formed in 2023 that has accrued hundreds of millions of streams and has a new popular single, “Internet Girl”—includes American, Indian, Filipina and Swiss members. They trained in Los Angeles under <span>HYBE </span>x Geffen Records, a joint venture between the Korean label and Universal Music Group. Katseye was formed during a televised talent show that let fans watch the trainees compete and vote on who would make the final cut. Only one of the six winners is Korean. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","the-risky-plan-to-take-the-k-out-of-k-pop-TE-2026-01-17","d"]
