3:T164b,<p><span><span><span>The world this week</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Business</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00024.jpg"/></p><p><span>Oil prices</span> swung wildly in response to developments in the Iran war, rising sharply on fears that it would take months for energy markets to stabilise after the conflict, and falling amid speculation about peace talks. Prices for natural gas, especially in Europe, similarly whipsawed. With investors worried that <a href="index_split_081.html#filepos453629">higher energy prices will push up inflation·</a>, possibly causing central banks to increase interest rates, the yields on government bonds continued to rise. Gold, typically a haven in times of chaos and high inflation, has fallen from recent highs; its spot price is down by nearly 20% since the war began in late February.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00117.jpg"/></p><p>Christine Lagarde, the president of the <span>European Central Bank</span>, said in a speech that the <span>ECB</span> was “prepared, if appropriate, to make changes to our policy at any meeting”, which markets interpreted as paving the way for interest-rate rises this year. Ms Lagarde told <a href="index_split_084.html#filepos476563"><span>The Economist</span></a> that “too much has already been damaged” in the Iran war and the disruption to energy could last for “years”.</p><p><span>TotalEnergies</span> struck an agreement with the American government to “renounce” its offshore-wind licences in America and instead invest in fossil-fuel production. The French energy giant will invest $928m in a liquefied-natural-gas plant in Texas as well as in conventional oil and shale. In return the government will reimburse Total $928m for cancelling two wind-power projects. Patrick Pouyanné, Total’s chief executive, remarked that “the development of offshore-wind projects is not in the country’s interest.”</p><p>In a <a href="index_split_078.html#filepos440162">landmark trial a jury in Los Angeles·</a> sided with a 20-year-old woman who claims that <span>Meta</span> and <span>YouTube</span> contributed to her depression during childhood. The jury found that the companies had been negligent and that their platforms were designed to be addictive to young people. A separate jury in New Mexico found Meta liable for not protecting children from the harmful effects of social media. </p><p>Open<span>AI</span> abruptly dropped <span>Sora</span>, its <span>AI</span> video-generation tool, cancelling a $1bn deal with Disney that would have allowed users to create content using Disney characters. The startup had only just published a set of guidelines for publishing safely on Sora, including safeguards for teens.</p><p><span>A nutritious deal</span></p><p><span>Danone</span>, a French foods company, buffed up its range of fitness products by agreeing to buy <span>Huel</span> for €1bn ($1.2bn). Huel makes meal-replacement drinks, snacks and food supplements that are popular with gym fanatics, or “Hueligans” as the firm describes them.</p><p><span>Arm</span>, a British chip designer in which SoftBank holds a majority stake, unveiled its first chip, as it branches out from selling chip designs. Its <span>AGI</span>
<span>CPU</span> is purpose built to co-ordinate large numbers of tasks among data-centre <span>AI</span> agents, using less power and promising to reduce costs for customers compared with traditional <span>CPU</span>s. Its lead partner and first customer is Meta; other partners include Open<span>AI</span>.</p><p>After eight years of negotiations Australia and the <span>EU</span> signed a <span>free-trade pact </span>that removes tariffs on most goods, with the notable exception of Australian beef and lamb, which still face quotas. Farmers’ groups in Australia criticised the deal. Meanwhile, the <span>EU</span> announced that a trade agreement with Mercosur countries will come into provisional effect on May 1st.</p><p><span>Argentina’s</span> president, Javier Milei, was able to claim that his tough economic reforms were bearing fruit when data showed the economy had expanded by 4.4% in 2025, after contracting in 2023 and 2024. A survey of economists by the central bank forecasts that <span>GDP</span> will grow by 3.4% this year.</p><p><span>Elon Musk</span> unveiled an ambitious plan to build two chip factories near Austin, Texas, one to produce chips for cars and robots and the other to supply <span>AI</span> data centres in space. The “Terafab” complex will be built by Tesla and SpaceX, though no timeline was given for its development. Earlier, a federal jury found that Mr Musk had misled investors in Twitter when he implied in the spring of 2022 that he was no longer interested in buying the social-media platform, causing its share price to fall. He subsequently bought it in October that year and renamed it X.</p><p><span>Leaving orbit</span></p><p><span><span>NASA</span></span>
<a href="index_split_090.html#filepos513821">overhauled its Artemis Moon programme·</a>, ditching a plan to build a space station in lunar orbit and instead repurposing its components to build a base on the Moon, which will eventually use nuclear power. We are “focusing on infrastructure that supports sustained operations on the lunar surface”, said Jared Isaacman, <span>NASA</span>’s administrator. It still hopes to land astronauts on the Moon in 2028, with SpaceX and Blue Origin developing the landers.
