<p><i>Letters are welcome via email to </i><i><a href="mailto:letters@economist.com">letters@economist.com</a><a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2025/03/26/a-note-for-readers"><br/></a><a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2025/03/26/a-note-for-readers">F</a></i><i><a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2025/03/26/a-note-for-readers">ind out more</a></i><i> about how we process your letter</i></p><hr/><h4>Britain’s labour laws</h4>
<p>You are right to note that <a href="https://www.economist.com/britain/2026/02/05/britains-trade-union-laws-are-returning-to-the-1970s">Britain’s trade-union laws are returning to the 1970s</a>, highlighting the scale of change and the risks of moving too far and too fast in a labour market already under strain (“Ready, steady, unionise!”, February 7th). Strong employment standards and constructive workforce representation matter, and retailers want to work with the government to ensure work pays and living standards rise. But the real test of reform is whether it expands job opportunities without unintentionally reducing them.</p>
<p>The Bank of England has been clear that the labour market is cooling, with hiring intentions softening as employment costs rise and unemployment increases. In this context, the rapid expansion of trade-union powers and union-access rights risks adding more costs and uncertainty for labour-intensive sectors that provide millions of flexible jobs, delaying recruitment and investment. Recent analysis suggests that these provisions alone could cost businesses more than £1bn ($1.3bn) a year. </p>
<p>The nature of work has also changed. Since the pandemic, flexibility has increasingly become a preference rather than a sign of insecurity. There is a real risk of regulating for a labour market that existed before covid, rather than the one workers operate in today. The Employment Rights Act can raise standards, but only if it is implemented with care and industry insight. Get the balance right and it can support inclusion and living standards. Get it wrong and it risks narrowing routes into work at precisely the moment the country needs them most.</p>
<p><span>Helen Dickinson</span><span><br/></span>Chief executive<br/>British Retail Consortium<i><br/></i><i>London</i></p><hr/><h4>Packing an aerial punch</h4>
<p>Your article on <a href="https://www.economist.com/china/2026/01/27/behind-chinas-race-to-build-aircraft-carriers">the trajectory of aircraft-carrier construction</a> missed the opportunity to discuss the development of smaller aircraft launch platforms (”Top gun”, January 31st). Particularly so for the America-class and its Chinese equivalent, the Type <span>076</span>. The American navy holds an advantage in these large-deck amphibious ships.</p>
<p>While the <i><span>USS</span></i> <i>America</i> and Wasp can launch manned <span>F-35B</span> Lightning II stealth fighters via short take-off and vertical landing (<span>STOVL</span>), the equivalent Chinese ships are limited to helicopters and drones. This distinction matters. Until China develops a <span>STOVL</span> fighter, their “amphibious” carriers will lack the air-to-air combat capacity of their American counterparts, forcing them to rely on unproven unmanned platforms for air support.</p>
<p><span>Jordan Fox</span><span><br/></span><i>New York</i></p><hr/><h4>Poverty background</h4>
<p>I was surprised by the absence of historical context in the conclusions of a recent study on <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/02/19/did-americas-war-on-poverty-fail">the merits of America’s welfare programmes</a> (Free Exchange, February 21st). The authors contend that the national poverty rate fell more dramatically (29 percentage points) in the decades before the Great Society programmes of the 1960s than in the 60 years that followed (16 points). They suggest that free-market forces of the 1940s, 1950s and early 1960s were more effective in alleviating poverty.</p>
<p>This analysis is historically flawed on two counts. First, the starting-point of the initial time period (1939–63) falls in the final years of the Great Depression, when the national unemployment rate was still a staggering 17.2%. It stands to reason that as the country emerged from the Depression and experienced the post-war economic boom, the rate of poverty reduction would exceed that which came later.</p>
<p>Second, it is difficult to characterise this early period as one of unfettered free-market capitalism. During the war years, federal outlays hit over 40% of <span>GDP</span>. Meanwhile, the top marginal tax rate peaked at an astronomical 94% in 1945 and was above 90% during the 1950s until, notably, 1963.</p>
<p>Failing to account for the redistributive effects of these fiscal policies strikes this history teacher as a glaring omission, one that ultimately obscures, rather than clarifies, the important question of how best to alleviate poverty.</p>
<p><span>Nate Bowen</span><span><br/></span>Social-studies department co-ordinator<br/>Mountain View High School<i><br/></i><i>Mountain View, California</i></p><hr/><h4>Trade winners</h4>
<p><a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/02/10/who-wrangled-the-best-trade-deal-from-donald-trump">Who wrangled the best trade deal from Donald Trump</a>, you asked (“Comparative advantage”, February 14th)? The American government recently released its trade statistics for 2025, which show that Mexico’s total exports to the United States reached a record high for a single year. This was unforeseeable a year ago, when <i>The Economist</i> wrote that Mr Trump’s tariffs were “his most extreme ever” (“Bad neighbour”, March 3rd 2025). And although you noted that the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement shielded Mexico and Canada “from trade oblivion” (“Trick or treaty”, October 4th 2025), the fact that Mexico increased its exports, despite the appreciation of the Mexican peso against the dollar, makes the case for considering Mexico to be a winner.</p>
<p><span>Rene Cabral</span><span><br/></span>Professor of economics and finance<span><br/></span><span>EGADE</span> Business School<i><br/></i><i>Monterrey, Mexico</i></p><hr/><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00077.jpg" title=""/><h4>Tater tales</h4>
<p>As you say, <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2026/02/16/the-worlds-most-common-vegetable-is-enjoying-a-great-year">the spud’s role in human history “is no small potatoes”</a> (“Playing the tuber”, February 21st). John Gerard, a barber-surgeon and head gardener at the home of William Cecil, Elizabeth I’s chief adviser, grew potatoes, among other rare and exotic plants. He did this in the garden of his home in Holborn, London. Gerard is best remembered for publishing in 1597, “The Herball, or Generall Historie of Plantes”, a dictionary of plants covering 1,392 pages illustrated with more than 2,000 woodcuts, including the first illustration of a potato.</p>
<p>The text explains how the vegetable could be eaten, “rosted…boyled…or dressed any other way by the hand of some cunning in cookerie.” “The Herball” was updated  by Thomas Johnson, an apothecary, who reminded readers that potatoes had once been banned in Burgundy for fear they could cause leprosy.</p>
<p><span>Robin Laurance</span><span><br/></span><i>Oxford</i></p>
<p>The most commonly credited figure for inventing French fries is Antoine-Augustin Parmentier (1737–1813), a French pharmacist and nutritionist who championed the potato in France. He is best remembered as a vocal promoter of the potato as a food source throughout Europe.</p>
<p>His many other contributions to nutrition and health included establishing the first mandatory smallpox vaccination campaign in France (under Napoleon, beginning in 1805) and pioneering the extraction of sugar from sugar beets. Parmentier also founded a school of breadmaking and studied methods of conserving food, including refrigeration.</p>
<p>Parmentier is interred at Père Lachaise Cemetery in Paris, and his grave is famously decorated with potato plants. Admirers to this day put potatoes on his grave.</p>
<p><span>Jacob Gopas</span><span><br/></span><i>Beer Sheva, Israel</i></p><hr/><h4>Sing it loud and proud</h4>
<p>It is indeed the case that the ever-changing social and cultural landscape is leading to a dearth of opportunities for children, and boys especially, to receive the kind of training their voice needs to develop the elasticity and control required to be a tenor (“The great tenor shortage”, February 14th). In England those church choirs that do still thrive have largely accepted girl choristers for many years, righting a gender injustice but inadvertently making it even less appealing for boys to join in. The weight of responsibility for filling the void has fallen on the shoulders of schools and provision is inconsistent. Success is largely down to the passion and expertise of individual teachers, with pockets of excellence in both maintained and independent schools.</p>
<p><span>Alex Osiatynski</span><span><br/></span>Director of music<br/>Stamford School<i><br/></i><i>Stamford, Lincolnshire</i></p>
<p>It’s heartening to see stories on issues specific to our humanity. The tenor shortage isn’t likely to be solved by <span>AI</span>’s promises of efficiency, productivity, or financial gains, but instead by giving greater focus to those areas, like music, that make us truly human. That is, unless we decide as a society that we are <span>OK</span> with robot singers.</p>
<p><span>Jeremy Eikenberry</span><span><br/></span><i>Gaithersburg, Maryland</i></p>
