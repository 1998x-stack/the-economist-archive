3:T1033,<p><span><span><span>Taking back control</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Indonesia’s erratic president grabs the country’s commodity exports </span></span></p><p><span><span>Facing a tumbling currency, Prabowo Subianto announces a vast new agency to sell resources to the world</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 04:55 AM | SINGAPORE</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00094.jpg"/></p><p><span>Now under state control</span></p><p><span>A</span><span>PRESENTATION</span>
<span>TO </span>parliament on fiscal policy might seem like a dry affair meant for bean-counters. Yet this year’s address in Indonesia generated high drama. In an unprecedented intervention, Prabowo Subianto, the president, took to the podium. But instead of reassuring markets that his administration had heard their concerns—especially over the rupiah, now at an all-time low, he launched into a defiant broadside. “We no longer want to be a victim and endure unfair treatment of other countries to our nation,” he bellowed. “I am here to say: it has to come to an end.”</p><p>Mr Prabowo’s main pronouncement was over the creation of Danantara Sumberdaya Indonesia (<span>DSI</span>), a new state-owned agency under Danantara, his sovereign-wealth fund. Citing Indonesia’s persistently low tax-to-<span>GDP</span> ratio, Mr Prabowo gave <span>DSI</span> the task of quashing practices that let Indonesian resource-extraction firms pay less in tax. These include selling underpriced commodities to affiliates in Singapore, which are then resold to the rest of the world at a mark-up. Mr Prabowo claimed that systematic under-invoicing had cost the country over $900bn since the 1990s. To stop this, <span>DSI</span> will act as the sole buyer from Indonesian commodity producers, selling their wares to global customers. It will initially cover coal, palm oil and certain nickel products—for all of which Indonesia is the world’s largest exporter. In addition, 100% of foreign-exchange proceeds must now be deposited into state-owned banks, in an effort to prop up the rupiah against the dollar.</p><p>It is a vast undertaking, though its precise scope is as yet unclear. A modest version of <span>DSI</span> could amount to merely an extra step added to Indonesia’s bureaucratic thicket. But at its most ambitious, <span>DSI</span> could transform Indonesia’s economy. Bureaucracy could slow and distort the flow of exports, on which Indonesia relies to raise revenue. Nor is it clear how <span>DSI</span> will handle existing long-term contracts. Rather than increasing Indonesia’s tax take, <span>DSI</span> could end up cutting it, as <span>S</span>&amp;<span>P</span>, a rating agency, warned last week.</p><p>The abrupt, seemingly half-baked scheme has raised hackles among Indonesia’s businesspeople. Stocks of resource companies have been sold off for fear that <span>DSI</span> will squeeze margins. Officials have tried to calm nerves. “We will respect all existing contracts,” said one. “We are listening to the market,” said another. There have also been early signs of backpedalling. American firms have already been exempted from the state-owned bank foreign-exchange retention rule.</p><p>But Mr Prabowo’s resource nationalism has grown ever more aggressive. He has already nationalised millions of hectares of forest land for alleged regulatory violations and compelled oligarchs to stump up $3bn to buy low-paying “patriot bonds”. In his speech he rejected “neoliberal capitalism”, which he said does not feature in the constitution. Such thinking could augur a deep shift in the relationship between the state and private business. Mr Prabowo may have hoped to show bold initiative. Instead he has deepened a sense of crisis in a country where the policymaking process is going off the rails. ■</p><p><span>For exclusive coverage of Asian politics, economics and security, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/asia-bulletin"><span>Asia Bulletin</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","indonesias-erratic-president-grabs-the-countrys-commodity-exports-TE-2026-05-30","d"]
