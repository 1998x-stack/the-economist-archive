Coding against the machine<h1>Why AI has not yet upset India’s IT industry </h1>Deploying the technology is the real world is proving tricky<p>3月 19, 2026 05:16 上午 | Bangalore</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-21-00053.jpg" title="Indian tech workers "/>
<p><b>T</b><span>his year’s</span> edition of the annual jamboree for Indian <span>IT </span>firms, held last month in Mumbai, was a study in contrasts. The president of Nasscom, the industry body, hailed a new sales record: it expects that its members will have enjoyed combined revenue of more than $315bn in the year to March, up by 6% on the year before. Yet delegates tearing their eyes from the stage and glancing at their phones would see share prices plunging. The Nifty <span>IT </span>index dropped by around a fifth following a viral blogpost that imagined new artificial-intelligence coding tools would wipe the industry out altogether (see chart).</p>
<p>The case for gloom is obvious. For decades the industry has profited from labour arbitrage: the cost of hiring a coder in Pune, a city in western India, is a fraction of hiring one in Pasadena, a Californian suburb. It wasn’t just software engineers: call-centre and data-entry jobs were outsourced to India, too. <span>IT </span>consulting firms such as Infosys and Tata Consultancy Services (<span>tcs</span>) derive much of their revenue by providing customers with armies of Indian coders who perform labour-intensive tasks such as maintaining software, answering support tickets and writing routine code.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-21-00054.png" title=""/>
<p>Now, however, there is an even cheaper alternative: an <span>AI </span>agent. Claude Code, a tool from Anthropic, can put together a prototype of a software application in minutes. Making it run efficiently and securely requires deeper technical knowledge. If one skilled developer armed with Claude can do the work of several, then businesses may find themselves with less need for the coders that Infosys and<span> tcs</span> provide.</p>
<p>So far, however, the predictions that the mass automation of coding will leave outsourcing firms obsolete seem overblown. Their clients often hope <span>AI </span>will create huge productivity gains by, for example, using the technology to quickly and cheaply build a new internal <span>HR </span>tool. But such improvements in productivity are only possible in “greenfield” environments with “clean architecture”, argues Atul Soneja, chief operating officer at Tech Mahindra, an <span>IT </span>firm. Deploying <span>AI </span>in “brownfield” environments—with legacy code, a lack of documentation and multiple systems that must all continue to operate in real time—is far trickier. In the end, clients often realise that their <span>AI </span>dreams were too ambitious and end up hiring as many outsourced coders as before, say executives.</p>
<p>What is more, the <span>AI </span>boom may present an opportunity for the consultancy arms of India’s outsourcers. They argue that they can now fulfil more of a strategic role for their clients: getting the most out of <span>AI </span>requires understanding all of the context around the problem, something that consultants with experience across businesses can offer. Nandan Nilekani, one of the founders of Infosys, reckons that such services related to <span>AI</span> could be worth $300bn-400bn by 2030.</p>
<p>The bulls have data on their side. The most recent results for the outsourcers were slightly better than expected. <span>tcs </span>reported that in the three months to January <span>AI</span>-related sales rose by 17% on the previous quarter, making up 6% of total revenue. Headcount has fallen at <span>tcs </span>but risen at its competitors in recent years, despite the possibility of automation. A report by Yogesh Aggarwal of <span>HSBC</span>, a bank, notes that there are few “tangible case studies to support claims of <span>AI </span>cannibalisation of traditional software”.</p>
<p>So-called global capability centres (<span>GCC</span>s) are another part of the picture. In essence, these are outsourcing arms dedicated to a single company, such as Lululemon or Wells Fargo, and employ far more of India’s tech workers than the <span>it </span>consultants do. Their rise reflects the fact that nearly every company now sees its technology as core to its business. If companies do more of their coding in-house, aided by agentic tools, that may still benefit India’s<span> it</span> industry. Many <span>IT </span>consultants offer services linked to <span>GCC</span>s, helping businesses set them up, for instance.</p>
<p>From the moment Chat<span>GPT</span> made its debut in November 2022, Indian outsourcers have been pegged as one of the sectors most exposed to displacement by <span>ai</span>. More than three years on, though, the promised disruption has not arrived. Revenues are growing, and hiring continues. Yet the sector nonetheless encapsulates the effect of <span>ai</span> on business. The technology may still upend the industry, but so far its effect is unclear and uneven. ■</p>
