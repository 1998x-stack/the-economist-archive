3:T1193,<p><span><span><span>Disfigured</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Bad government statistics can cost the economy billions</span></span></p><p><span><span>A new study tries to put a number on the value of reliable numbers</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 07, 2026 07:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-09-00037.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE LIFE</span> of American government beancounters is tough, and not just at cocktail parties. They have a hard time persuading people to talk to them at work, too. A decade ago nearly nine in ten Americans, when approached, agreed to fill out the Current Population Survey, which is administered to about 60,000 households each month and asks about, among other things, employment. Fewer than seven in ten do so now (see chart 1). For the Consumer Expenditure Survey, which tries to capture 3,700 households monthly, the response rate is down from 68% to 40%.</p><p>Low engagement is a problem because the surveys are much more than a way to satisfy Uncle Sam’s curiosity about the citizenry. Bosses and investors rely on accurate data about <span>GDP</span> growth, joblessness, inflation and much else besides to manage businesses and allocate capital. When the information becomes less reliable, investment decisions get more difficult to make. Some may be delayed, creating a potential drag on the economy.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-09-00111.jpg"/></p><p>But how much of a drag? A new working paper by Nicholas Bloom of Stanford University, Erica Groshen, former head of the Bureau of Labour Statistics (<span>BLS</span>) now at Cornell University, and Duncan Hobbs and Michael Strain of the American Enterprise Institute, a think-tank, hazards an estimate. Preserving trust in “the integrity and quality of official statistics”, the authors claim, generates economic benefits of about $25 for every $1 spent on the <span>BLS</span>, the agency with an annual budget of $700m that is responsible for many of these data.</p><p>To arrive at their conclusion the quartet analysed an ignoble episode in the <span>BLS</span>’s recent history. On August 1st 2025 <a href="https://www.economist.com/united-states/2025/08/04/donald-trump-escalates-his-war-on-numbers">Donald Trump sacked Erika McEntarfer</a>, appointed as the agency’s commissioner by his predecessor, Joe Biden. The president alleged, without evidence, that the <span>BLS</span>’s steep downward revision to recent jobs numbers had been “<span>RIGGED </span>in order to make the Republicans, and <span>ME</span>, look bad”.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-09-00003.jpg"/></p><p>In fact, it was the wanton dismissal that looked bad in the eyes of many observers. In the following seven days there was a 50% leap, relative to the week before, in the average value of the index of Economic Policy Uncertainty (<span>EPU</span>), which tracks the number of articles mentioning such uncertainty that are published daily in American newspapers. This was a discernible jump even when compared to the chaos caused by Mr Trump’s trade war in April 2025 and his real one in Iran in the past two months (see chart 2). Based on Mr Bloom’s earlier study with other colleagues of the <span>EPU</span>’s impact on business investment, industrial production and employment, the authors estimate that the jump reduced American <span>GDP </span>by over $100bn (0.3%) and non-farm payrolls by 168,000 (0.1%).</p><p>Some of this may not have been the direct result of Ms McEntarfer’s firing. A part was probably due to the big jobs revision itself. The unrelated resignation of a Federal Reserve governor on the same day may have played a role, too. But even after controlling for those variables, the researchers reckon that the McEntarfer affair trimmed <span>GDP </span>by at least $20bn and employment by 31,000 jobs. A brazen presidential attack on statisticians’ dependability may, admittedly, do more damage than a slow-motion crisis caused by unco-operative respondents. But the study shows that reliable statistics have real value. Forsaking them would be an incalculable loss. ■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in economics, finance and markets, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/money-talks"><span>Money Talks</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","bad-government-statistics-can-cost-the-economy-billions-TE-2026-05-09","d"]
