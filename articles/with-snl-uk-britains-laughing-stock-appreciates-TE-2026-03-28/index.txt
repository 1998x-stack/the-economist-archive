3:T1548,<p><span><span><span>The funny business</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>With “SNL UK”, Britain’s laughing stock appreciates</span></span></p><p><span><span>The television show offers a chance to make sketch comedy amusing again</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00127.jpg"/></p><p><span>M</span><span>OST OF THE </span>time, the aim of the opening monologue on <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/02/13/the-secret-to-the-success-of-saturday-night-live">“Saturday Night Live”</a> is simple: it sets the tone, gives the celebrity host a few easy gags and primes the audience to laugh at the sketches to follow. But on March 21st Tina Fey, the inaugural host of a new British version of the show, had a different job: to justify the programme’s existence. “Why do a <span>UK</span> version of ‘<span>SNL</span>’?” the American writer-performer wondered aloud, repeating a question that many have been asking since the project was announced. “Well, like so many large-scale American operations these days, no one really knows why.”</p><p>In fact, commissioners and producers at Sky, the broadcast network that is home to “<span>SNL UK</span>”, had been mulling it over for about five years. They reckoned there were good reasons to launch the show half a century after “<span>SNL</span>” made its debut across the Atlantic. The first was that, in dark times, people need light relief. Comedy is a release valve—and there is plenty of pent-up tension thanks to war, economic instability and <a href="https://www.economist.com/britain/2023/08/17/what-happens-to-comedy-when-british-politics-becomes-a-joke">political</a> polarisation. “We’re in a moment where we need to laugh more than ever,” says Philip Edgar-Jones, Sky’s executive director of unscripted originals.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00110.jpg"/></p><p>“Comedy can do a brilliant job—and they do it brilliantly in America—of [speaking] truth to power,” he adds. The American edition of “<span>SNL</span>” often opens with a bit about some foolhardy thing the president has said or done; the first sketch of “<span>SNL UK</span>” portrayed Sir Keir Starmer, Britain’s prime minister, as a feeble flip-flopper, terrified of talking to the man in the White House. (Donald Trump, delighted to be portrayed as a bully, shared it on social media.) The show will soon take on the royal family (a rich subject for satire) and British culture more broadly. One of the best sketches from the premiere imagined an immersive “Paddington” experience, featuring not a cuddly, marmalade-loving bear but a bloodthirsty beast.</p><p>Though “<span>SNL UK</span>” adapts an existing format, there is nothing like it on British television at present. <span>TV-</span>sketch comedy has keeled over like a dead parrot in the past decade: gone are the days when you could choose between “Fry and Laurie” and “French and Saunders” or “Harry and Paul” and “Armstrong and Miller”. (A notable exception is “Mitchell and Webb Are Not Helping”, which last year reunited two British comedy favourites and became Channel 4’s biggest new comedy show since 2018.) Whether panel shows or sitcoms, <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/04/16/the-success-of-lol-last-one-laughing-is-no-joke">comedy</a> is rarely broadcast live any more, meaning that “<span>SNL UK</span>” has a madcap energy all its own: the appeal of watching it on <span>TV</span> is the prospect of seeing things go awry in real time.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00062.jpg"/></p><p>And, after the broadcast, the show can reach a giddy <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2024/05/09/american-comedy-has-become-too-safe-on-tv">audience online</a>. Videos tagged #skit go viral on Instagram and TikTok. Posts related to sketch comedy had 4.5bn impressions on Reddit, YouTube and X between mid-February and mid-March, according to Sprout Social, an analytics firm. “<span>SNL UK</span>” counts a number of social-media stars among its writers and cast, including Omar Badawy, Lorna Rose Treen and Al Nash; sketches from the premiere, such as one which imagines famous Britons at a dinner party, have already racked up hundreds of thousands of views. The hope, Mr Edgar-Jones says, is to produce material that “sets the internet on fire the day afterwards” in Britain and beyond.</p><p>The first episode of “<span>SNL UK</span>” was patchy. But it has a chance to cultivate the next generation of British comedians, just as “<span>SNL</span>” has launched the careers of umpteen American actors and writers who are now globally famous. (The choice of Ms Fey as host underscored that point.) Audiences may remain sceptical for now; the show will need to land more of its punchlines. But if it spreads on social media and gains a loyal following live, Ms Fey’s question will have been answered. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","with-snl-uk-britains-laughing-stock-appreciates-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
