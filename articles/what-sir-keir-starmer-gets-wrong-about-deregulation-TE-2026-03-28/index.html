What Sir Keir Starmer gets wrong about deregulation

A tilt at a little-known auditor reflects a strategy without a theory

Mar 26, 2026 05:57 AM

WHEN IT COMES to deregulation, Sir Keir Starmer, Britain’s prime minister, at least has the phrasebook. He promises to “hack back the thicket of red tape”, and to shut down the “checkers and blockers”. Occasionally he even acts on it: the government intends to reform planning codes for nuclear power stations, for example. Yet his agenda is struggling, because it is a cause in need of a theory. To see why, consider the predicament of the Regulatory Policy Committee.

The RPC has a low profile, a small budget and an unsexy task. It studies the impact assessments that accompany draft new laws or regulations, and judges whether they are sufficiently thorough. Those which show a clear rationale for the policy, a consideration of alternatives, a proper cost-benefit analysis and a calculation of the effect on businesses are given a green rating. Those that fall short are marked red. The findings are public. The government is now considering scrapping it. The focus, the logic runs, should be on improving policies themselves, rather than on the accompanying analysis.

That would be a mistake. If Sir Keir were serious about deregulation, he would consider the RPC a vital ally in changing Whitehall’s ways. It does not judge policies’ merits: Herod’s slaughter of the first-born would get a green rating if he properly assessed the impact on Judea’s small businesses. But it is one of the few restraints ministers face against sloppy or rushed rulemaking.

“A government which didn’t have public scrutiny of regulatory cost-benefit analysis would find it impossible to control the quality of the regulation it created,” notes Joe Hill of Re:State, a reformist think-tank. The quality of analyses has fallen since 2022, according to the RPC’s data. The Employment Rights Act, a measure to increase trade-union powers, was among those marked red.

This state of affairs reflects two confusions at the heart of Sir Keir’s drive for deregulation. The first is whether the beneficiary of lighter regulation is the private sector, or government ministers. Sir Keir uses them interchangeably; yet often their interests are in conflict. If ministers are freed to “get things done”, as he insists, what will often get done is more poorly drafted regulation.

The second confusion is what deregulation is for. Sir Keir characterises it as a basic administrative task: a question of streamlining decisions and processes. But this misses the point. Regulation and regulators are ultimately instruments to achieve economic or social ends, which are for politicians to define and for which Sir Keir’s government lacks a coherent theory.

The pursuit of “growth” is not, by itself, a meaningful instruction for regulators, notes Stuart Hudson, a regulatory-policy expert, in a recent submission to Parliament. It could justify almost any regulatory stance: allowing bills to rise to increase investment, or forcing them lower to benefit consumers; softening merger control to facilitate dynamism, or tightening it to encourage competition. Working out which economic ends regulators pursue matters more than how onerously they do it.

On this score, the government is still working out what it believes. In March 2025 it declared that regulators were “too risk-averse”. Yet, ten months later, a report by the National Audit Office (another blocker, no doubt) found that government departments had failed to “articulate their risk appetite” to the regulators they sponsor. Nor was there a “high-level, structured analytical framework” for the relationship between regulation, growth and risk, which the audit office drily concluded makes it tricky to have “informed conversations about risk aversion”.

It is easy to blame the regulators. The real problem lay with a vacuum at the top of government. Moving against the RPC would look less like a government with a grand design for a dynamic economy than a fitful one swiping at a soft target. ■
