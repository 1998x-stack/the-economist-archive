The world this week<h1>Business</h1><p>4月 01, 2026 03:22 上午</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-04-04-00004.jpg" title="Man fills his car at a gas station as price of gas rises. "/><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-04-04-00005.png" title=""/>
<p><b>Oil prices</b> in March chalked up their biggest monthly increase ever. Brent crude hit $119 a barrel, a rise of 63% since Iran blockaded the Strait of Hormuz, but trading is volatile and the price plunged on the latest hopes for a peace deal.  The average price of <b>petrol</b> in America reached $4 a gallon this week. <b>Stockmarkets</b> had their worst quarter since 2022. In March alone the <span>S&amp;P 500</span>, the <span>NASDAQ</span> Composite and the Dow Jones Industrial Average all fell by around 5%. In Europe the Stoxx 600 was down by 8%. In Asia Hong Kong’s Hang Seng index dropped by 7% and Japan’s Topix by 11%. The <span>MSCI</span> Emerging Markets index is 13% lower. Markets rallied on March 31st on renewed hopes that the war would end soon. https://t.me/demagazinesharing</p>
<p>In <b>Japan</b> Mimura Atsushi, the government minister overseeing the country’s currency policy, warned that “decisive measures” may need to be taken to stop “speculative moves” against the yen. The currency has fallen to its weakest level against the dollar since July 2024, when Japan last intervened to prop it up.</p>
<p>The euro zone’s annual<b> inflation</b> rate surged in March, to 2.5% from 1.9% in February, smashing through the European Central Bank’s 2% target. Energy prices were 4.9% higher than a year ago; in February they had decreased by 3.1%.</p>
<p><b>Carnival </b>reduced its annual profit outlook because of higher fuel prices. It is the only big American cruise line that does not hedge its fuel costs; its share price has slumped over the past month.</p>
<p><b><span>BYD</span></b><b>’s</b> annual net profit fell by a fifth amid a savage price war in China’s electric-vehicle market. It was the Chinese company’s first drop in profit in four years. However, its share price has surged since the start of the Iran war, as investors bet that the conflict will result in a renewed shift in the car industry away from petrol towards electrification.</p><h4>Feeling slightly deflated</h4>
<p>The sell-off in <b>memory-chip stocks</b> intensified, after Google published research suggesting a new algorithm could crunch the amount of storage needed for <span>AI</span>. That could help alleviate the supply shortage of memory chips; a tight market has boosted the share prices of  companies that make them over the past year. After the research was published stocks in <span>SK</span> Hynix, SanDisk and Western Digital all tumbled. Micron’s share price fell by 10%, and is down by 30% since mid-March (though still up by 275% over the past 12 months).</p>
<p>A federal judge issued a temporary block on the Pentagon’s designation of <b>Anthropic</b> as a “supply-chain risk”. The Pentagon announced the designation after Anthropic sought safeguards for the use of its <span>AI</span> in certain situations; the judge found that the government was probably being “arbitrary and capricious”. The court of appeals in Washington, <span>DC</span>, is expected to rule soon on the matter in a separate case.</p>
<p><b>Unilever</b> announced a deal to merge its foods business, which includes the Hellmann’s, Marmite and Pot Noodle brands, with <b>McCormick</b>, best known for its line of herbs and French’s mustard. Unilever shareholders will own 65% of the $66bn newly combined company. The consumer-goods conglomerate spun off its ice-cream business last year, leaving it to focus on its personal-care and household-products businesses.  </p>
<p><b>Eli Lilly</b> struck a licensing and research agreement with <b>Insilico Medicine</b>, which works with <span>AI</span> to develop new drugs. Worth up to $2.75bn, it could be the biggest deal to date in the <span>AI</span>-drugs sector, which uses machine learning and automated labs to cut the costs of pharmaceutical <span>R&amp;D</span>. In a busy week for Eli Lilly it also agreed to buy <b>Centessa</b>, which specialises in sleeping disorders, for $7.8bn.</p>
<p>The authority of the <b>World Trade Organisation</b> took another knock when the latest round of talks ended in deadlock. The sticking-point was a disagreement between America and Brazil over extending a moratorium that prevents duties from being imposed on digital downloads and streaming (America wants the duties to be scrapped for ever). </p>
<p>Michael Rousseau said he would step down as <b>Air Canada’s</b> chief executive, following a furore over his condolence message to the families of the two pilots  killed in a collision at La Guardia airport. Apart from “bonjour” and “merci” Mr Rousseau’s remarks were delivered in English only, a grave sin in bilingual Canada.</p><h4>Stars in their eyes</h4>
<p><span>NASA</span> prepared to launch <b>Artemis II</b>, which it hopes will carry four astronauts around the Moon on a ten-day voyage. Earlier, <b>Virgin Galactic</b> confirmed it was on track to begin test flights of the first of its two new spacecraft this year, which will eventually resume the company’s commercial space flights. It has started selling tickets to potential space tourists for $750,000.</p>
