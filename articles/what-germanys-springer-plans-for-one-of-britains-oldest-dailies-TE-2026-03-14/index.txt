Springer into action<h1>What Germany’s Springer plans for one of Britain’s oldest dailies</h1>Its purchase of the Telegraph is part of an ambition to be the leading digital media company of the free world<p>3月 12, 2026 05:41 上午 | BERLIN</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-14-00031.jpg" title="Commuters from Margate in Kent read the Guardian, the Daily Telegraph"/>
<p><b>E</b><span>ver since</span> its parent company fell into receivership in 2023, the fate of the 170-year-old <i>Telegraph</i>, a newspaper favoured by Britain’s conservatives, has been in limbo. It was the subject of a failed bid involving the United Arab Emirates (which led to resistance by editorial staff and a change of legislation) and one from America. Its latest suitor was the Daily Mail and General Trust (<span>DMGT</span>), owned by Lord Rothermere, which bid £500m ($670m) for the main rival of its <i>Daily Mail</i>, Britain’s largest paid-circulation daily. The<span> DMGT </span>was set to close the deal when its offer was bested by an all-cash bid of £575m from Axel Springer, a German media group.</p>
<p>There is an irony that a paper known for its aversion to the European Union and Schadenfreude whenever Germany is doing poorly will be owned by Germans (pending regulatory approval). But owning the <i>Telegraph </i>is a long-held ambition of Mathias Döpfner, Springer’s boss, who paid a “trophy-asset multiple” for the paper he had already tried to buy in 2004, according to Enders Analysis, a research firm. The <i>Telegraph</i> was the “North Star” of Axel Cäsar<b><i> </i></b>Springer, who founded the group in 1946, exuded Mr Döpfner.</p>
<p>What are Mr Döpfner’s plans for the Telegraph Media Group (<span>TMG</span>), owner of the <i>Daily</i> and <i>Sunday Telegraph</i>? He sees “massive growth potential for <span>TMG</span>”; a “transformation with the best artificial-intelligence tools” is critical. Editorial independence is sacrosanct, he says. He wants to turbocharge <span>TMG</span>’s expansion into the American market via <i>Politico</i> and <i>Business Insider</i>, two of his American media outlets.</p>
<p>Brexiteers worry that Mr Döpfner will make the rabidly pro-Brexit <i>Telegraph</i> more European. <i>Bild</i>, his flagship tabloid, campaigned for Britain to remain in the <span>EU</span>. Staff of <i>Bild </i>and <i>Die Welt</i>, his other big German daily, have to sign up to “essentials”, the company’s credo, which includes support for Israel’s right to exist and the “fundamental” alliance between America and Europe. Peter Huth, a spokesman for Springer, says that <span>TMG</span> staff will not need to sign and insists that Chris Evans, the <i>Telegraph’</i>s editor-in-chief, will have free rein. Time will tell. In 2021 (the year of a German federal election) Mr Döpfner told the then editor-in-chief of <i>Bild, </i>in a (subsequently leaked) text message, that he should strengthen the pro-business Free Democratic Party.</p>
<p>Both the <span>TMG </span>and the Springer papers in Germany have a conservative bent. The unresolved issue for both is whether they still embrace mainstream conservatism or now incline towards the parties of the populist right such as the Alternative for Germany and Reform <span>UK</span>, says John Kampfner, author of “Why the Germans Do It Better”. Nigel Farage, the leader of Reform, frequently writes columns in the <i>Telegraph</i>.</p>
<p>Mr Döpfner’s goal is to double the value of Axel Springer over five years. He paid top dollar for the <i>Telegraph </i>because he believes it can help him reach his aim: with its 1.1m subscribers he will double the number of paid subscribers of his media brands. With the help of <span>ai</span> in particular he thinks he can make Springer the leading digital media company of the free world.■</p>
