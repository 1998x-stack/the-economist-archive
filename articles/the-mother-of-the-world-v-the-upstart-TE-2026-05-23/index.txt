Feeling surrounded<h1>The mother of the world v the upstart</h1>Why Egypt needs and fears the United Arab Emirates<p>5月 21, 2026 03:18 上午 | CAIRO</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-23-00122.jpg" title="Sheikh Mohamed bin Zayed Al Nahyan and Egypt's President Abdel Fattah al-Sisi at Yas Mall in Abu Dhabi"/>Retail diplomacy
<p><b>W</b><span>hen</span> <span>abdel-fattah al-sisi</span> arrived in Abu Dhabi on May 7th, he might have expected a fulsome display of gratitude. Egypt had, belatedly, offered warplanes to help defend the United Arab Emirates (<span>uae</span>) from Iran. But instead of a photo-op in one of his palaces, Muhammad bin Zayed, the <span>uae</span>’s ruler, took the Egyptian president for tea in a mall.</p>
<p>For Egyptians, the optics spoke volumes about their country’s sinking prestige. They still think of it as <i>umm al-dunya, </i>the mother of the world, whereas the <span>uae</span> is less a startup nation than an upstart one, newly arrived from the desert. They resent what they see as the <span>uae</span>’s encirclement of their larger, historically dominant country with separatist movements, militias and client rulers. They are bitter that Egypt’s plight has forced it to sell prized assets and vast tracts of land to Gulf investors.</p>
<p>The Emiratis see Egypt as ungrateful. For decades, they have bailed it out. Yet rather than rush to the <span>uae</span>’s aid when Iran struck, many Egyptians cheered Iran. Instead of contributing to the fight against Iran, Egypt called for de-escalation and sent ministers to negotiate. Pakistan has paid for similar policies; the <span>uae</span> demanded repayment of over $3bn in loans and expelled 15,000 nationals. Some in Abu Dhabi mutter that Egypt, with 400,000 nationals there, should be taught a similar lesson.</p>
<p>Both sides have grievances. Egypt is alarmed by the <span>uae</span>’s support for the Rapid Support Forces, a militia accused of genocide in Sudan. (The <span>uae</span> acknowledges providing some early support to the <span>rsf</span>, but denies it is still doing so.) Egypt also frets about Emirati-backed separatists in Libya and Somaliland and the<span> UAE</span>’s support for a separate administration in Gaza.</p>
<p>Most troubling for Egypt is the <span>uae</span>’s growing alignment with two menacing regional powers: Abiy Ahmed’s Ethiopia and Binyamin Netanyahu’s Israel. Ethiopia’s construction of the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam—with the <span>uae</span>’s encouragement, say some Egyptians—threatens Egypt’s life force, the Nile. Israel’s continued squeeze on Gaza might yet tip its population, Islamists included, into Egypt.</p>
<p>The Emiratis believe they are owed more loyalty. In 2023 Egypt seemed close to economic collapse. Years of overspending on Mr Sisi’s vanity projects, the army’s gutting of the economy and mounting debt had shaken investor confidence. Inflation surged. The currency tumbled. The <span>uae</span> provided billions of dollars, unlocking help from the <span>imf</span> and the European Union, and has since poured in billions more.</p>
<p>Still, neither side will let the quarrel spiral too far. Mr Sisi has offered the <span>uae</span> fighter pilots if war with Iran restarts and has told Egypt’s media to stop criticising the Gulf state. But Cairo is also bolstering ties with Qatar, Saudi Arabia and Turkey. Egypt may not want to alienate the <span>uae</span>. But nor can it afford to rely on it. ■</p>
