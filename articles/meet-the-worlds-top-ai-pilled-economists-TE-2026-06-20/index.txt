AI-Econ 101<h1>Meet the world’s top AI-pilled economists </h1>Most of them are not found in ivory towers<p>6月 18, 2026 03:54 上午 | Paris</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00040.jpg" title="Illustration of two people with binoculars, one is looking through theirs out into the distance, the other has put theirs down and is looking up at a giant robot stood directly in front of them"/>
<p><b>A</b><span>rtificial intelligence</span> has already created trillions of dollars of market value and turned a handful of tech nerds into celebrities. The public is excited but also terrified. Many brainy people, from Bill Gates to Elon Musk, say that the technology is just getting started. Talk to academic economists, though, and most seem oddly uninterested in studying the impact of the potentially world-changing technology. The centre of gravity in <span>ai</span> economics is instead shifting away from universities—and a gang of “<span>ai</span>-pilled economists” is leading the charge.</p>
<p>University researchers can move fast when they want to. After Lehman Brothers, an investment bank, collapsed in 2008 and sparked the global financial crisis, economists turned the study of bank runs and credit crunches from a niche pursuit into a mainstream interest. Two months into covid-19, in 2020, close to a third of working papers on economics published by America’s National Bureau of Economic Research (<span>nber</span>), a prestigious repository of economic thought, focused on the pandemic’s effects in some way. Some of this work burst into the mainstream, including that by Nicholas Bloom of Stanford University, an expert in working from home, and Emily Oster of Brown University, who studied school closures.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00041.png" title=""/>
<p>Three and a half years after the launch of Chat<span>GPT</span> ushered in the <span>ai</span> age, by contrast, economic analysis of the technology remains comparatively scarce. The share of <span>nber</span> papers which focus on <span>ai</span> is rising, but not especially fast (see chart 1). Even in 2024, after the covid emergency was over and the <span>ai</span> era had truly begun, the number of covid-related papers exceeded the number of <span>AI</span>-related ones. This year the <span>nber</span> is likely to host more conferences on health care than on <span>ai</span>.</p>
<p>Some academic economists have seized the <span>ai</span> opportunity (see chart 2). Susan Athey of Stanford is exploring what happens if <span>ai</span> puts people out of work. Basil Halperin of the University of Virginia has written lucidly about how financial markets price <span>ai</span> developments. Yet none is nearly as recognisable as Mr Bloom or Ms Oster. And few economists seem to recognise the research potential. “I’ve been shocked by how few of my colleagues have even tried to speak with Anthropic or Open<span>ai,</span>” says one superstar academic economist who insists he talks to <span>ai</span> labs all the time.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00042.png" title=""/>
<p>A lot of the research that exists is highly abstract. According to <span>ideas</span>/Re<span>pec</span>, a bibliographic database dedicated to economics, Daron Acemoglu of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology is the highest-ranked wonk in <span>ai</span> economics. His paper from 2024 featuring a complex model of economic growth under <span>ai</span> implies modest aggregate productivity gains. It has received more than 1,000 citations. But the model underestimates the potentially transformative effect of new <span>ai</span> products coming to market, argues Tyler Cowen of George Mason University. “The gains from <span>ai</span> measure as small because it is assumed <span>ai</span> will not be doing new things.”</p>
<p>Many <span>AI</span>-linked empirical studies also appear to rest on flawed assumptions. A paper by Erik Brynjolfsson of Stanford (ranked fifth by <span>ideas</span>/Re<span>pec</span>) and colleagues suggests that young people’s employment in <span>ai</span>-exposed occupations has sharply dropped, implying that the technology is already transforming the labour market. Yet attributing the trend to <span>ai</span> means believing that firms started shedding young workers on the very first release of Chat<span>gpt</span>—a product that was nowhere close to being good enough to replace humans.</p>
<p>Academic economists may be slow and sloppy for two reasons. The first relates to the type of shock that <span>ai</span> represents. In 2020 covid changed the world almost overnight, and the effects were visible almost instantly in the data (see chart 3). By contrast, <span>AI</span> is changing the economy under its bonnet. The average unemployment rate across the <span>oecd</span>, a club of rich countries, is about the same as when Chat<span>gpt</span> was first released. <span>gdp</span> numbers also contain virtually no <span>ai</span>-specific data—investment in <span>ai</span> data centres, for instance, can only be guessed at. With no clear macroeconomic impact and no microeconomic data, there is little for wonks to analyse.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00043.png" title=""/><h4>Artificial scarcity</h4>
<p>The second factor is that economists, as a rule, are a fairly techno-sceptical bunch. Historical research shows that technology raises incomes, but only slowly, with all sorts of non-technological factors (including financial frictions and cultural resistance) holding it back. In Britain, it was decades before the technological breakthroughs of the industrial revolution translated into faster growth.</p>
<p>A recent paper by Mr Halperin and colleagues, reporting on the results of a survey, captures this scepticism. Even under a scenario where <span>ai</span> progress is “rapid” by 2030—meaning that <span>ai</span> can compete with or surpass the brainiest humans—the median academic economist expects American <span>gdp</span> growth of just 3.5% in 2050 (compared with 5.3% for <span>ai</span> researchers). Only 11% of leading economists agree that the use of <span>ai</span> over the next decade “will lead to a substantial increase in the unemployment rates in advanced countries”, according to a survey by the University of Chicago. If most academic economists do not think that <span>ai</span> will be transformational, they may prefer to stick with other research areas they consider weightier.</p>
<p><span>AI</span>-curious economists are finding a cosier home in two places away from the academy. The first is government, and in particular statistical offices and central banks. Surveys from America’s Census Bureau and Statistics Canada track <span>ai</span> adoption across the economy. The Bank of England’s monthly “decision-maker panel” has explored businesses’ perceptions of <span>ai</span>, while the British government recently created the <span>ai</span> Economics Institute to improve research on the topic. At a recent conference at the <span>oecd</span>, government beancounters puzzled over how to update measures of productivity for the <span>ai</span> age. Little of this work will set the world on fire, but it performs a crucial public service: building the data infrastructure on which future economists will rely.</p>
<p>The more notable home for the <span>AI</span>-pilled wonks is on the front lines of the technology. In the 2010s <span>ai</span> labs hoovered up many brilliant computer scientists to design their models. Ufuk Akcigit of the University of Chicago and colleagues find that by 2019 more than two-thirds of <span>ai</span> researchers worked in industry, up from less than half in 2001. Now something similar is happening to economists.</p>
<p>Anthropic has appointed Anton Korinek of the University of Virginia (who comes in second on the <span>ideas</span> ranking) to its economics-research team. Open<span>ai</span> has enlisted Ronnie Chatterji of Duke University as its chief economist. Google DeepMind, the technology giant’s in-house frontier lab, recently hired Alex Imas of the University of Chicago as its “director of <span>agi</span> economics” (referring to the “artificial general intelligence” that would match or best humans at most intellectual tasks). According to <i>The Economist</i>’s rough tally, a few dozen dismal scientists have accepted jobs at the big labs.</p>
<p>The attraction of an <span>AI</span> lab is clear. They have access to the best data, as well as the ear of policymakers. Accept a position there and before long Dwarkesh Patel, Silicon Valley’s favourite podcaster, will ask you on his show. Tech firms also have deeper pockets than universities do. Even relatively junior economist positions at an <span>ai</span> lab can pay $300,000 a year or more: not high relative to an <span>ai</span> programmer, perhaps, but well above what an early-career professor teaching Econ 101 would earn. (Some lucky ones may get their hands on stock options in the world’s hottest firms.)</p><h4>Real-time intelligence</h4>
<p>The quality of extramural <span>AI</span> research is rising. In work at the Peterson Institute for International Economics, a think-tank, Mr Korinek and Patrick McKelvey of the Bank of Canada have built what they call “<span>ai gdp</span>” for America. The paper shows that, properly measured, it grew by more than 2,000% in both 2024 and 2025. Mr Imas publishes a useful tracker of the effect of <span>ai</span> on productivity. According to his judgment, there is encouraging evidence of small-scale productivity gains, but little evidence of large macro effects.</p>
<p>All very exciting (at least to <span>ai</span>-pilled economic journalists). But for every clever study by Mr Korinek or Mr Imas the labs still produce a dud. Anthropic’s “economic index”, released to great fanfare, is not really an index but a random collection of data about usage of its chatbot, Claude. In March Anthropic published a report concluding that “people get better at using Claude through experience”. No duh. Last year Open<span>ai</span> published descriptive work showing that 20-25% of messages on Chat<span>gpt</span> involved “seeking information”. Riveting stuff.</p>
<p>No doubt the quality will improve over time. Still, if frontier <span>ai</span> research migrates inside firms, economists may follow the path already taken by the “tech economists” at Microsoft, Google and elsewhere. These wonks typically spend less time on big questions of social import (asking, say, whether social media is good for children) and more on narrow questions, such as how best to design auctions for selling ads. Mr Akcigit’s study notes that after making a permanent transition from academia to industry, <span>ai</span> researchers produce fewer papers but more patents, in effect a “reorientation from open science towards proprietary innovation”.</p>
<p>Then there are conflicts of interest. Lab researchers are likely to face pressure to publish work that makes <span>ai</span> look useful and safe. Last year Tom Cunningham, an economist, left Open<span>ai</span> after reportedly growing frustrated about what he could and couldn’t publish. He ended up at <span>metr</span>, an institute dedicated to evaluating <span>ai</span> models and the threats they pose. In a world with great possibilities but also great dangers, society needs impartial researchers to say what they really think. Academic economists have ground to make up.■</p>
