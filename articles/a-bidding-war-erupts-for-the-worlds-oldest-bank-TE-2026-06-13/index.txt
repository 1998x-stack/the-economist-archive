3:Tcd1,<p><span><span><span>Timeless fashion</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>A bidding war erupts for the world’s oldest bank</span></span></p><p><span><span>Monte dei Paschi di Siena is suddenly all the rage</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jun 11, 2026 05:50 AM | ROME</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-13-00027.jpg"/></p><p><span>F</span><span>OR MONTHS</span> Italian finance has been gripped by the €16bn ($18bn) takeover by <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/04/09/the-latest-italian-banking-whodunnit-has-it-all">Monte dei Paschi di Siena</a> (<span>MPS</span>), the world’s oldest surviving lender, of Mediobanca, an investment bank which also owns a 13% stake in Generali, a giant insurer. Completed last September, it was the <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2025/05/01/a-takeover-bid-promises-consolidation-in-italian-finance">biggest</a> European banking merger in years. It involved the abrupt sacking of <span>MPS</span>’s boss by the board in April and his rapid reinstatement by shareholders.</p><p>If that was not enough drama, on June 7th Banco <span>BPM</span>, Italy’s fourth-largest lender, proposed a tie-up with <span>MPS</span> that would create a heavyweight worth some €50bn. The next day Carlo Messina, boss of Intesa Sanpaolo, Italy’s biggest domestic lender, dismissed <span>BPM</span>’s offer as a “love letter” and launched a rival and, he claimed, less frivolous €31bn bid for <span>MPS</span>. The result would build a behemoth with market value of €130bn or so.</p><p>Intesa is offering <span>MPS</span>’s owners 16 newly issued shares of its own for every ten in <span>MPS</span>, plus €1 per share on top of that. That represents a premium of 12.5% on <span>MPS</span>’s closing share price on June 5th. Intesa has called a shareholders’ meeting for September 10th to approve the necessary issuance of shares. If both banks’ shareholders go for it, the deal would create the euro zone’s second-biggest lender by market value, after Santander of Spain.</p><p>To ease trustbusters’ worries, Intesa has struck a deal with Unipol, an insurer that has the biggest holding in another Italian lender, <span>BPER</span>, to divvy up <span>MPS</span>’s retail business. Intesa would keep 625 of <span>MPS</span>’s 1,260 branches. The brand and many of <span>MPS</span>’s central operations would go to <span>BPER</span>, creating Italy’s third-biggest banking group.</p><p>That might satisfy Italy’s trustbusters. Giorgia Meloni’s conservative—and nationalist—government would certainly prefer Intesa’s bid to Banco <span>BPM</span>’s. Crédit Agricole, a French bank, holds a stake of 20% in <span>BPM</span>. Politicians in Rome sometimes cheer on the equally drama-filled pursuit of Germany’s Commerzbank by UniCredit, another big Italian lender. They may prove less keen to let foreigners grab a bigger slice of Italian finance. ■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in economics, finance and markets, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/money-talks"><span>Money Talks</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","a-bidding-war-erupts-for-the-worlds-oldest-bank-TE-2026-06-13","d"]
