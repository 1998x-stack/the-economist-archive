3:T1d7b,<p><span><span><span>The world this week</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Politics</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 04:55 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00089.jpg"/></p><p>Hopes that<span> America</span> and <span>Iran</span> were close to reaching a deal to end their war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz were on <a href="index_split_044.html#filepos257223">shaky ground·</a>. In what it described as “purely defensive” measures, America shot down Iranian drones and conducted air strikes against military targets in Iran. Iran retaliated; Kuwait, the home of an American air base, intercepted a barrage of drones and missiles. Days after claiming that a deal was nearly done, Donald Trump dismissed Iranian claims that one was close, insisting that America would not allow Iran to control the strait in any settlement. </p><p><span>Israel</span> stepped up its offensive against <span>Hizbullah</span>, an Iranian-backed militia, in southern Lebanon. It extended its combat zone against Hizbullah’s operations to all areas south of the Zahrani river, which lies 40km (25 miles) north of the Israeli border, and told all residents to evacuate. Separately, <span>Hamas</span> confirmed that Israel had killed the new leader of its armed wing in an air strike on Gaza. His predecessor had been killed by Israel just a week earlier.</p><p>The <span>WHO</span> called for a ceasefire in the east of the Democratic Republic of Congo to help tackle the <a href="index_split_043.html#filepos251993"><span>Ebola·</span></a> outbreak there. More than 1,000 suspected cases and around 250 deaths have been reported; both figures are almost certainly undercounts. Fighting has hindered response efforts and exacerbated the spread. Meanwhile, Canada and the Bahamas imposed similar travel bans to America’s on residents from three African countries.</p><p>Bassirou Diomaye Faye, the president of <span>Senegal</span>, dissolved the government and replaced Ousmane Sonko, the prime minister, following months of disagreement over tackling the country’s ballooning debt. Days after he was fired, parliament reinstated Mr Sonko as an <span>MP</span> and elected him speaker. Mr Faye hoped that Mr Sonko’s departure would ease talks with the <span>IMF</span>, but it will probably deepen the disruptive rivalry between the two men.</p><p><span>Smackdown</span></p><p>Donald Trump’s favoured candidate won the Republican primary for a Senate seat in <a href="index_split_030.html#filepos171547"><span>Texas·</span></a>. Ken Paxton, the scandal-plagued state attorney-general, trounced John Cornyn, the incumbent senator since 2002, by 64% to 36%. Mr Trump had attacked Mr Cornyn as “very disloyal” for occasionally breaking with his agenda. Mr Paxton will face James Talarico, the Democratic candidate, in November’s midterm election.</p><p><span>Tulsi Gabbard</span> said she would step down as America’s director of national intelligence. Ms Gabbard is leaving the job to care for her husband, who has been diagnosed with a rare form of bone cancer. A long-time opponent of war with Iran, she had increasingly been sidelined by Mr Trump.</p><p>The death toll from a <a href="index_split_025.html#filepos144370">gas explosion·</a> at a coalmine in <span>China’s</span> northern province of Shanxi stood at 82. It is the country’s worst coalmining disaster since 2009.</p><p>The Baloch Liberation Army claimed responsibility for the suicide-bombing of a train in Quetta, the capital of Balochistan in south-west <span>Pakistan</span>,<span>
</span>that killed at least 30 people. The militants said they had rammed a car filled with explosives into the train, which was carrying security personnel and their families. </p><p>Kyiv endured one of the heaviest bombardments by <span>Russia</span> since the start of the war in 2022. Hundreds of drones and missiles rained down on the <span>Ukrainian</span> capital. One of the strikes destroyed the museum commemorating the Chernobyl nuclear disaster of 1986, when Ukraine was part of the Soviet Union. Vladimir Putin vowed to pummel Kyiv again, in retaliation for what he claims was a Ukrainian strike on a building in Starobilsk, in occupied Ukraine, killing 21 people. Mr Putin says the building was a student dormitory. Ukraine insists it did not attack the dwelling but had targeted an elite Russian drone unit.</p><p>As it steps up its attacks on “decision-making centres” in <span>Kyiv</span>, Russia’s foreign ministry warned foreign citizens, diplomatic staff and personnel at international organisations “to leave the city as soon as possible”. The European Union summoned the top Russian diplomat in Brussels to voice its anger. “The <span>EU</span> is not going anywhere,” said the bloc’s ambassador to Kyiv.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00029.jpg"/></p><p>In <span>Turkey</span> riot police were deployed to the headquarters of the main opposition Republican People’s Party (<span>CHP</span>) to force the removal of its leader, Ozgur Ozel. This came after a court overturned the results of the <span>CHP</span>’s leadership election in 2023, claiming there were procedural irregularities. The court reinstated Kemal Kilicdaroglu, the opposition’s losing candidate against Recep Tayyip Erdogan at the previous presidential election, as <span>CHP</span> leader. The party described the court’s decision to remove Mr Ozel as a “judicial coup” designed to hurt the <span>CHP</span> ahead of possible early elections.</p><p>Police entered the headquarters of the ruling Socialist Party in <span>Spain</span> as part of an investigation into corruption. Earlier, tens of thousands of people marched in Madrid demanding the resignation of Pedro Sánchez, the prime minister.</p><p>Mette Frederiksen was given another chance to form a centre-left government in <span>Denmark</span>, after talks aimed at forming a coalition among her centre-right rivals broke down. At an election in March Ms Frederiksen’s Social Democrats won the most votes, though it was the party’s worst result since 1903. Ms Frederiksen has remained interim prime minister since March.</p><p>Nationwide protests continued across <span>Bolivia</span>. Rodrigo Paz, the centrist president, said he would cut his salary in half along with other members of his cabinet to demonstrate their commitment to the country. Protesters want the government to curtail its austerity programme. One minister’s car was pelted with stones when he visited a security operation to remove protesters’ roadblocks.</p><p><span>Don’t leave me this way</span></p><p>Mark Carney, <span>Canada’s</span> prime minister, warned separatists in Alberta that they would regret leaving the federation if they chose to do so, comparing them to Brexiteers in Britain who voted to leave the <span>EU</span>. His remarks came after Alberta’s premier called a public ballot for October 19th that will decide whether a referendum on separatism should be held.</p><p>The Mexican government said that <span>Iran’s football team</span> would be allowed to stay in Tijuana and cross the border into America to play in the World Cup, which starts on June 11th. Donald Trump has said the players should not stay in America for their own safety. They were supposed to be based in Arizona.
