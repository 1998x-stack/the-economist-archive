3:T113e,<p><span><span><span>Swindrone</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Why Swindon is emerging as a centre for Britain’s drone industry</span></span></p><p><span><span>How to muster a cluster</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 05:30 AM | Swindon</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00041.jpg"/></p><p><span>D</span><span>RONES HAVE</span> revolutionised battlefields. Many hope they can now transform an ailing British town. The Ministry of Defence (<span>M</span>o<span>D</span>) plans a drone-testing site, the size of eight football pitches, in Swindon, 130km west of London. During the second world war the site turned out Spitfires that roared the allies to victory. Quieter machines are meant to lift the town’s fortunes and Britain’s military strength once more.</p><p>Armies across the world are scrambling to mesh uncrewed drones with crewed jets, ships and infantry. Britain’s “Strategic Defence Review” promised to build such systems at scale, at pace and, crucially, at home. The <span>M</span>o<span>D</span> has in theory committed £4bn ($5.3bn) to drone innovation.</p><p>Around Plymouth, in the south-west, a cottage industry for maritime drones is drawing on the town’s naval heritage. In the West Midlands <span>BAE</span> Systems, a large defence firm, makes high-end kits. Leonardo, an Italian defence giant, is building autonomous helicopters in the south-west around Yeovil. Along the Welsh coast, a drone-testing facility has popped up. What Britain lacks is a mass-production hub and a cluster where scientists, generals, entrepreneurs and defence types can interact, says Mark Westwood of Cranfield University. Enter Swindon.</p><p>The town of 230,000 people has already attracted Tekever, a Portuguese dronemaker, which is investing £400m there and will start producing advanced surveillance drones later this year. Stark, a German startup, is assembling loitering munitions capable of carrying a 5kg payload. Smaller firms, including Flyby Technology and Munin Dynamics, are moving in. Empty aircraft hangars ten miles south at a former air-force base are being prepped for testing drones. An <span>M</span>o<span>D</span> facility will lend a military cachet to the embryonic cluster.</p><p>That all this is happening in Swindon may seem puzzling. Passengers stepping off the train are met by a 12-storey block of boarded windows. The town centre is hardly thriving. Council data show business births lag behind those in comparable areas in England. But above the shuttered fronts, a thin row of plush offices house insurers and financial advisers that push productivity above the national average.</p><p>Industrial decline has its benefits. Empty warehouses have dragged industrial rents down. Drone firms, keen to move fast, prize brownfield sites, those setting up locally say. Workers left adrift by the closure of a Honda factory following Brexit offer a pool of manufacturing know-how. Housing is relatively cheap. Firms also credit a <span>YIMBY</span>-minded local council with speedily stamping planning permits, and an energetic <span>MP</span> for courting defence firms.</p><p>Yet Swindon’s greatest asset may be its location. Mike Armstrong, who runs Stark’s British arm, says the town sits at the heart of Britain’s defence-aerospace corridor. London lies one way, Bristol the other, with a chain of military institutions dotted across the region.</p><p>The palpable excitement about the drone cluster comes with caveats. Local elections on May 7th ousted the Labour council, which has been replaced by a minority one led by Conservatives. Still, there seems to be broad agreement on the town’s economic future. </p><p>More concerning is uncertainty plaguing British defence contractors. They are chafing at a long delay to the government’s “defence investment plan”, which was due to be published in the autumn. It is supposed to set out what the government intends to buy over the next decade. Now, at last, the plan’s publication appears imminent. The hope for Swindon is that it will give its incipient cluster more confidence for the long term. ■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in Britain, </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/blighty"><span>sign up</span></a><span> to Blighty, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","why-swindon-is-emerging-as-a-centre-for-britains-drone-industry-TE-2026-05-30","d"]
