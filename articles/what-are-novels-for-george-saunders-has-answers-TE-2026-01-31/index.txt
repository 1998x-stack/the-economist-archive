3:T15f7,<p><span><span><span>A different sort of ghost story</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>What are novels for? George Saunders has answers </span></span></p><p><span><span>The author behind one of this century’s best books is back with “Vigil”</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 29, 2026 05:49 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-31-00001.jpg"/></p><p><span>Vigil. </span>By George Saunders. <span>Random House; 192 pages; $28. Bloomsbury; £18.99</span></p><p><span>W</span><span>HAT IS</span>
<span>FICTION</span> for? In an idealistic view, it lets readers clamber inside other minds and see the world through strangers’ eyes. George Saunders, a zany American author, is a proponent of this theory, aiming, he says, to foster compassion and “human connection”. He has dreamed up a way to include such leaps of empathy in the plot of his novels—first in <a href="https://www.economist.com/books-and-arts/2017/03/23/george-saunders-comic-supernatural-moral-novel">“Lincoln in the Bardo”</a>, which won the Booker prize in 2017, and now in “Vigil”.</p><p>“Lincoln” was his debut novel. (A creative-writing teacher at Syracuse University and on Substack, Mr Saunders has also published short stories, novellas and <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2023/07/21/critics-are-getting-less-cruel-alas">criticism</a>.) A cacophony of souls in limbo make up that book’s antic cast; they hover in a cemetery in 1862, where Willie Lincoln, son of Abe, is interred. In a beautiful central passage, two ghosts sit on top of one another and in doing so are able to grasp each other’s lost loves, longings and regrets. “So many years I had known this fellow and yet had never really known him at all,” one of these spectral figures observes.</p><p>“Vigil” elaborates on such magical moments. It unfolds in the final hours of K.J. Boone, once a big-oil <span>CEO</span>, now dying cantankerously in his mansion in Dallas. “His heart was beating irregularly, like an afterthought,” Mr Saunders writes, with his sensitive Chekhovian eye for human frailty of all sorts; Boone’s nappies are changed by carers to “the sound of efficient under-the-breath co-ordination”. Once again, ghosts mingle with the oblivious living. The fading man’s wife and daughter are here, as is the narrator, the spirit of a woman named Jill Blaine. Her heaven-sent mission is to ease his passage across life’s threshold.</p><p>Wearing the clothes her mortal self was buried in—“beige skirt, pale pink blouse, black pumps”—Jill is perceptible only to other spirits and to Boone. When she enters “the orb of his thoughts”, she can eavesdrop on them and hold hallucinatory conversations with him. More than that, as in the <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/10/31/an-enthusiast-wanders-through-the-worlds-graveyards">graveyard</a> epiphany in “Lincoln”, she and her fellow shades can plunge into each other’s forms, and Boone’s, and through such temporary cohabitations share their feelings and memories.</p><p>She is not the only ambassador from the next world. The shade of a Frenchman, who invented the combustion engine and now regrets it, wants Boone to repent for his past as a climate-change denier. The spectres of people and animals who have suffered in the planet’s despoliation crowd in, too. Jill and the Frenchman in effect stage a deathbed debate over free will and predeterminism. Charitably, Jill suggests that Boone, and everybody else, could not be other than they are; her only job, she reasons, is to “comfort, for all else is futility”. Prickly and spiteful, Boone is disinclined to repent and none too keen on the comforting malarkey either.</p><p>If all that sounds heavy or abstruse, it isn’t. The prose zips with the author’s trademark wisecracks and quick-fire repartee. These ghosts are aware of their situation’s absurdity and drolly joke about it. Jill flits entertainingly into other lives, such as those of the adulterers at the wedding party next door. Intermittently she is beset by poignant recollections of her own earthly years, which ended in a rogue explosion in 1976. The novel lingers on life’s priceless ephemera—kisses, kind words, dancing—even as it ponders morals and <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/11/13/an-affecting-chronicle-of-a-mans-death-and-his-life">mortality</a>.</p><p>“Lincoln in the Bardo” is widely and rightly regarded as one of the greatest novels of the century so far. Expanding as it does on its predecessor’s innovations, “Vigil” is not quite as staggeringly original. Now and then Mr Saunders overdoes the kookiness. Not everyone will appreciate the environmental politics, nor enjoy his rumbling lament for “this lovely old place, ruined forever, maybe”.</p><p>Still, at a time when doomsayers predict the demise of the novel—destined, supposedly, to be seen off by glitzier distractions and dwindling attention spans—Mr Saunders has a recipe for fiction’s survival, as well as a rationale for reading it. Finding new ways to tell stories with words on a page, his work nudges forward the boundaries of the form. He powers his empathy machine with restless invention. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","what-are-novels-for-george-saunders-has-answers-TE-2026-01-31","d"]
