3:T1124,<p><span><span><span>A bumpy ascent</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Despite a record year, airlines are grappling with big challenges</span></span></p><p><span><span>Profits remain meagre and fleets are ageing</span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 31, 2025 02:11 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00001.jpg"/></p><p><span>R</span><span>OVANIEMI AIRPORT</span> in Finland, gateway to the home of Father Christmas in Lapland, surpassed 1m arrivals for the first time in 2025. Airlines serving fans of reindeer and ice swimming are not the only ones breaking records. In 2025 the industry’s net profits will have hit an all-time high of nearly $40bn globally, reckons <span>IATA</span>, a trade group, far exceeding 2019’s pre-pandemic total of $26bn. Yet plenty of challenges remain.</p><p>The world’s carriers have made a strong post-pandemic recovery. In 2024 they flew 4.8bn passengers, cruising past the previous peak reached in 2019. In 2025 that figure probably reached 5bn. For the first time combined revenues are expected to have exceeded $1trn. As demand continued to run ahead of additional capacity, load factors (a measure of seats filled) also hit a record, averaging nearly 84%, and should rise higher still in 2026.</p><p>Not everything has been in the ascent. European and North American airlines, which account for three-fifths of the industry’s net profits, have had to contend with circuitous long-haul routes to avoid Russian airspace since the start of the war in Ukraine. This year parts of the Middle East became no-go zones after Israel’s strike on Iran in June. America’s airlines were hit by a government shutdown that stopped federal workers from travelling and kept unpaid air-traffic controllers at home, disrupting flights.</p><p>What is more, despite a drop in fuel prices, which account for 25-30% of airlines’ operating expenses, other costs have risen. This is mostly the result of a shortage of new aircraft. Airlines may have recovered since the pandemic, but Boeing and Airbus are still under strain. The aircraft duopoly’s combined deliveries of under 1,400 new planes in 2025 were some way off the record of just over 1,600 in 2018.</p><p>Boeing has struggled to increase its production ever since two fatal crashes of 737 <span>MAX</span> short-haul aircraft in late 2018 and early 2019 led to a 20-month grounding. It has continued to struggle with quality control; in early 2024 a fuselage panel on another 737 <span>MAX</span> blew off mid-air. Airbus, meanwhile, has had troubles of its own. In early December it cut its target for deliveries for the year, from 820 planes to 790, as a result of a production flaw at a supplier of fuselage panels.</p><p>Ongoing trouble with suppliers, which scaled back during the pandemic and have had trouble scaling back up, may continue to disrupt planemaking. Problems with some Pratt &amp; Whitney engines have led to the grounding of a third of the global fleet of Airbus’s <span>A</span>320neo single-aisle jets.</p><p>The resulting shortfall in the supply of aircraft is unlikely to be resolved before 2031 at the earliest, according to <span>IATA</span>. Fuel efficiency, maintenance costs and emissions have all suffered as carriers have been forced to keep older planes in the air for longer. The average age of the global fleet is around 15 years, compared with 13 years in 2019. In the past fuel efficiency tended to improve by around 2% a year as new planes came into service, but this slowed to 0.3% in 2025. A recent report by <span>IATA</span> and Oliver Wyman, a consultancy, estimates that the cost to the airline industry of its ageing fleet, including extra fuel, repairs and spare parts, was over $11bn in 2025. Moreover, some airlines are failing to expand their routes as fast as planned owing to a lack of available aircraft.</p><p>Thus, despite their record year, airlines still eked out only a meagre net margin of 4%, equivalent to just $7.90 of profit per passenger. The industry’s bosses should not be celebrating just yet. ■</p><p><span>To track the trends shaping commerce, industry and technology, sign up to “</span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/the-bottom-line"><span>The Bottom Line</span></a><span>”, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter on global business.</span></p>6:["slug","despite-a-record-year-airlines-are-grappling-with-big-challenges-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
