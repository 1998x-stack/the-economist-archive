3:T15c7,<p><span><span><span>The world this week</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Business</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 08, 2026 05:48 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00080.jpg"/></p><p><span>Oil prices</span> rose slightly after America’s intervention in Venezuela, but soon settled back, with Brent crude trading at around $60 a barrel. The Trump administration <a href="index_split_066.html#filepos375169">planned meetings with executives from America’s oil companies·</a> to discuss how they might return to the country; reports suggest the companies remain wary about investing there. Venezuela sits on 300bn barrels of oil, a fifth of the world’s reserves, though the amount of capital expenditure required for extraction and production would be huge.</p><p><span>Back in the game</span></p><p>Chevron, a big player in Venezuela, was reportedly teaming up with Quantum Capital, a private-equity group, to buy the international assets of <span>Lukoil</span>. The Russian oil company was forced to sell after it was subjected to American sanctions, along with Rosneft, last October. Lukoil says the assets are worth up to $22bn.</p><p>After months of talks the <span>OECD</span> finalised changes to the accord on a global 15% <span>minimum corporate tax</span> that will exempt American multinationals. The global accord was agreed to in 2021, but the Trump administration renegotiated it last year. The amended version, in effect, recognises that American multinationals are subject instead to America’s minimum tax of 12.6% on foreign profits. Scott Bessent, the <span>US</span> treasury secretary, described it as a “historic victory” in protecting American businesses from “extraterritorial overreach”.</p><p><span>Tesla</span> is no longer the world’s biggest seller of electric vehicles. The carmaker delivered 1.6m <span>EV</span>s in 2025, down by 9% from 2024, while <span><span>BYD</span></span> sold 2.3m <span>EV</span>s, up by 28%. The Chinese company is expanding rapidly overseas, especially in Europe, which has fallen out of love with Elon Musk’s Tesla. In Britain sales of <span>BYD</span> and other Chinese <span>EV</span>s doubled in 2025. In total nearly 25% of new-car sales in Britain were electric.</p><p><span>Ford</span>, meanwhile, chalked up its best year for annual sales in the United States since 2019. The Detroit giant sold 2.2m vehicles in America in 2025. Like others in the industry it recorded a steep decline in sales of pure electric vehicles with the ending of the government’s <span>EV</span> tax credit, but sales of part-electric part-petrol hybrids surged by 22%.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00098.jpg"/></p><p><span>Stockmarkets</span> had a fine start to 2026, continuing their good run in 2025. Despite some wobbles towards the end of the year the <span>S&amp;P 500</span> rose by 16% over the 12 months, the <span>NASDAQ</span> Composite by 20% and the Dow Jones Industrial Average by 13%. Indices in Hong Kong, Japan and Germany all outpaced the <span>S&amp;P 500</span>. Britain’s <span>FTSE 100</span> increased by 22% over the year, and continued its rally in early 2026 by rising above the 10,000 mark for the first time. Emerging markets were the top-performing equity markets in 2025, according to analysis from asset managers at JPMorgan Chase, returning 34% in dollar terms.</p><p><span>Nvidia</span>, which saw its share price rise by almost 40% in 2025, said it would start delivering its latest chip to customers later this year. The Rubin chip (named for Vera Rubin, an American astronomer) can perform <span>AI </span>tasks more quickly, with less power and at a lower cost than Nvidia’s previous chips. The company described it as “the next generation of <span>AI</span>”.</p><p>The board of <span>Warner Bros</span> formally rejected Paramount’s $108bn hostile takeover bid and recommended that shareholders accept Netflix’s $83bn offer for most of the company. The board said the large amount of debt underpinning Paramount’s proposal would make it, in effect, “the largest leveraged buy-out in history”, entailing too much risk. At least one Warner investor said it had made an error.</p><p>Novo Nordisk started selling its Wegovy <span>weight-loss treatment pill </span>in America,<span>
</span>weeks after its approval by the Food and Drug Administration. Until now the treatments have been sold as an injection. The company is trying to claw back some of the market share it has lost to Eli Lilly, and has slashed the cost of the pill to entice people who cannot get the jabs through their health insurance. Eli Lilly expects to begin selling its own pill later this year.</p><p>Underscoring the change in its leadership, <span>Berkshire Hathaway</span> decided to pay Greg Abel, its new chief executive, a salary of $25m. Mr Abel recently took over the job from Warren Buffett, who famously drew a salary of just $100,000 (though his net worth is estimated at almost $150bn).</p><p><span>Making a splash</span></p><p>A new record was set for the price of a single <span>bluefin tuna</span> at Tokyo’s fish market. The first tuna sale of the year is said to bring good luck to the buyer, prompting high bids at the auction. Kimura Kiyoshi, owner of a sushi chain and nicknamed the Tuna King, paid ¥510m ($3.2m) for the 243kg (535lb) fish.
