3:Tca3,<p><span><span><span>German politics</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Germany’s Social Democrats gaze into the abyss</span></span></p><p><span><span>But an electoral drubbing could open a window for national reforms </span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM | BERLIN</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00130.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE SOCIAL DEMOCRATS</span> (<span>SPD</span>) have helped rule Germany for all but four of the past 28 years. Yet rarely has the party known such gloom. On March 22nd it was soundly defeated by the centre-right Christian Democrats (<span>CDU</span>) in an election in Rhineland-Palatinate, long an <span>SPD </span>stronghold. This followed a drubbing in Baden-Württemberg, where the <span>SPD</span> barely crossed the 5% threshold to enter parliament. In recent years the <span>SPD</span>, junior partner to Friedrich Merz’s <span>CDU</span> in Germany’s coalition government, has lost ground in almost every state and national election (see chart). Many in the <span>SPD</span> openly admit they no longer know what their centre-left party stands for.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00036.jpg"/></p><p>Yet Mr Merz’s troops did not crow over their victory, because an embittered <span>SPD</span> may hinder their plan to revive the economy. After his much-heralded “autumn of reforms” flopped last year, the chancellor wants to push a series of changes to tax, health insurance and pensions before the Bundestag adjourns for summer. Whether he gets his way depends largely on whether his coalition partner can swallow tricky compromises.</p><p>The party’s centrists have long sought to recapture the energy of the 2000s, when an <span>SPD</span>-led government pushed through far-reaching labour and welfare reforms. Some think the <span>SPD</span> has since lost its way. “For too long we gave the impression of caring more about those who don’t work than those who do,” says Dirk Wiese, an <span>SPD</span>
<span>MP</span>, adding that this perception is false. But some younger <span>MP</span>s want to focus on redistribution and preserving the welfare state. And the coalition’s slim majority creates opportunities for spoilers.</p><p>The crucial figure is Lars Klingbeil, the vice-chancellor and <span>SPD</span> co-leader. On March 25th he gave an important speech aimed mainly at his own party, urging reforms to labour, tax and pension rules to increase working hours, and calling for restraint on spending.</p><p>It will have landed well with Mr Merz. The question is whether Mr Klingbeil, who is mistrusted by his left-wing comrades, can bring his own party with him. Ursula Münch at the Tutzing Academy for Political Education says the <span>SPD</span>’s position is so dire that Mr Klingbeil may feel he has nothing to lose by taking on his internal foes. If so, the <span>SPD</span>’s biggest fights may lie ahead. ■</p><p><span>To stay on top of the biggest European stories, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/cafe-europa"><span>Café Europa</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","germanys-social-democrats-gaze-into-the-abyss-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
