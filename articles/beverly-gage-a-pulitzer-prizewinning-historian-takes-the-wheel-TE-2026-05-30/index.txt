3:T1064,<p><span><span><span>A perfect brake</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Beverly Gage, a Pulitzer-prizewinning historian, takes the wheel</span></span></p><p><span><span>A new book offers an enjoyable tour of America at 250</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 05:19 AM | NEW YORK</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00109.jpg"/></p><p><span>A backward glance can often lift the heart</span></p><p><span>This Land Is Your Land</span>. By Beverly Gage. <span>Simon and Schuster; 352 pages; $30. Oneworld; £22</span></p><p><span>A</span><span>MERICANS DRIVE</span> a lot. That habit has some detrimental effects, from air pollution and <a href="https://www.economist.com/interactive/united-states/2024/08/31/americans-love-affair-with-big-cars-is-killing-them">immense car</a> parks to an inflated reverence for Jack Kerouac. But it also has benefits, such as the American road trip, used by generations of parents to annoy their captive children and by novelists to tell stories of new beginnings (<a href="https://www.economist.com/the-economist-reads/2024/04/25/what-to-read-about-the-history-of-california">“The Grapes of Wrath”</a>) and catastrophic endings (<a href="https://www.economist.com/1843/2013/05/17/cormac-mccarthys-bleak-road">“The Road”</a>).</p><p>In a charming new book, Beverly Gage—a historian at Yale, who won a Pulitzer prize for her biography of J. Edgar Hoover—drives through American history. From George Washington to Ronald Reagan, and from Philadelphia to California, she roams among historic homes, monuments, parks and presidential libraries “in an attempt to explore, if not reconcile, the greatest tensions of American history”.</p><p>That sounds like a tall order for a road trip, but Ms Gage is a fun travelling companion. And her timing is good, with this year marking <a href="https://www.economist.com/interactive/america-at-250?utm_medium=cpc.adword.pd&amp;utm_source=google&amp;ppccampaignID=18156330227&amp;ppcadID=&amp;utm_campaign=a.22brand_pmax&amp;utm_content=conversion.direct-response.anonymous&amp;gclsrc=aw.ds&amp;gad_source=1&amp;gad_campaignid=18156330848&amp;gbraid=0AAAAADBuq3Kvpe_YmHbh6hIde8v8TVkxW&amp;gclid=CjwKCAjwrNrQBhBjEiwAoR4VO6PL-iN-SunIn7xdgSjLqwmZviiXYC9CGrQpFfEclwr5BQgTBrLGBxoCUgMQAvD_BwE">America’s semiquincentennial</a> and the centennial of Route 66, one of the nation’s original highways.</p><p>The book begins, fittingly, with Washington, who tried to discover his country by road. Six months after taking office, he was back in the saddle, riding around New England and sleeping in taverns and inns “to commune with ordinary Americans”. Washington found that most Americans did not think of themselves as such: they identified with their town or region rather than their country. That was for good reason: the United States was barely an adolescent.</p><p>To her credit, Ms Gage avoids both the simplistic history of the <a href="https://www.economist.com/topics/donald-trump">Trumpian</a> right and the fatalism of the progressive left. Visiting the Legacy Museum in Montgomery, Alabama, focused on racial injustice, Ms Gage takes issue with the museum’s contention that “Slavery did not end. It evolved.” Such pessimism is unhelpful: abolition involved “massive, heroic efforts and near-impossible efforts…I’m not sure what’s gained by suggesting they didn’t make much difference,” she argues.</p><p>The book’s pleasure is its journey, so it spoils nothing to say that she ends her trip feeling buoyed. On the road Ms Gage found faith in American progress that opinion polls fail to capture. However fractious today’s politics are, the country retains a great capacity for reinvention. Putting down the phone and picking up car keys this summer may also make others feel heartened. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","beverly-gage-a-pulitzer-prizewinning-historian-takes-the-wheel-TE-2026-05-30","d"]
