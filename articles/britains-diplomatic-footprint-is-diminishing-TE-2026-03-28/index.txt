3:T132c,<p><span><span><span>Losing grandeur</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Britain’s diplomatic footprint is diminishing</span></span></p><p><span><span>The country’s overseas estate is crumbling and shrinking</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00080.jpg"/></p><p><span>“H</span><span>ARD POWER</span>”, Sir Keir Starmer told the Munich Security Conference in February, “is the currency of the age.” Such thinking helps explain his Labour government’s plans to downsize Britain’s Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office.<span>
</span>Spending is expected to fall by an average of 6.1% a year in real terms between now and 2028-29, estimates the Institute for Fiscal Studies, a think-tank: the largest cut to any department, compared with a 1.5% increase across government. The prime minister wants to boost spending on defence; diplomacy will be squeezed.</p><p>Foreign aid will be <a href="index_split_069.html#filepos386788">hit hardest·</a>. But the Public and Commercial Services Union, which represents some foreign-office staff, expects cuts to all roles, including diplomatic and consular positions. Under current plans, between 15% and 25% of the workforce could lose their jobs. In some sections, the cuts will reportedly be even more severe. There are plans to chop 35% of roles in the analysis directorate, which is made up of subject-matter experts. Entire research areas will stop. On March 16th the union began balloting its members about taking industrial action.</p><p>In addition to staff cuts, the foreign office plans to sell off more of its overseas estate, continuing a decades-long process. This has happened under Conservative and Labour governments alike.</p><p>Under the Conservatives in 2018, for example, when Boris Johnson was foreign secretary, the British embassy in Bangkok was sold for £420m ($580m). The grand old building was demolished to make way for offices and shops. Edward Lister, a former aide to Mr Johnson, says he argued against the decision, as embassies are “a little bit of England, with the Union Jack flying”, and laments the loss to the country’s stature. The staff decamped to a nondescript corporate tower nearby.</p><p>A valuable chunk of Britain’s compound in Tokyo was sold to Mitsubishi Estate Group, a developer, in a deal completed in 2022. A regular visitor to the embassy calls the sale “distasteful”. The site has a prestigious position opposite the Imperial Palace. “It is now”, one former government adviser says ruefully, “a building site.”</p><p>More recently, the former British consulate-general’s office in Paris (pictured) was sold for £6.2m. Britain has also offloaded properties in Bilbao, Bogota, Lusaka and Montserrat. The bean-counters are eyeing a £12m apartment that was bought for a diplomat in New York.</p><p>However, large embassy sales of the Bangkok sort are no longer in fashion. Sir Oliver Robbins, the foreign office’s top civil servant, told a committee of <span>MP</span>s in December that flogging historic properties could be “more hassle than it is worth”. Besides, there aren’t all that many left to choose from. According to the National Audit Office, today the foreign office owns only 27% of its properties. Fifteen years ago the foreign office’s figure was 46%. Some of those remaining have been gifted by host countries, with conditions stipulating that they cannot be sold.</p><p>As for the 73% of its premises that Britain occupies but does not own, the government has often disposed of buildings to raise money, only to have to rent elsewhere or indeed in the exact same place. The consulate-general’s office in Munich was sold under a previous Labour government and then leased straight back.</p><p>Some of the buildings that comprise the remaining £2.5bn portfolio are in a poor, sometimes even unsafe, condition—15% have been rated as such by the foreign office. Over the past 15 years, according to the response to a freedom-of-information request from <span>The Economist</span>, £1.46bn has been raised from auctioning off 215 assets overseas, largely to help fund repairs and upgrades elsewhere.</p><p>A long, laborious process of minor sales now looks likely: an office here, a garden there. It promises to be an undignified, if discreet, syphoning off of assets. “We shape our buildings,” Winston Churchill said of the reconstruction of the House of Commons after the second world war, “and afterwards our buildings shape us.” Britain’s crumbling diplomatic footprint will do much the same.■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in Britain, </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/blighty"><span>sign up</span></a><span> to Blighty, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","britains-diplomatic-footprint-is-diminishing-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
