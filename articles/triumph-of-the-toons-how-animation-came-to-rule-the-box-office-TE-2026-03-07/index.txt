Picture perfect<h1>Triumph of the toons: how animation came to rule the box office</h1>This may be the genre’s most successful year yet<p>3月 05, 2026 04:15 上午</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00069.jpg" title="Beaverton University scientists Dr. Sam and Nisha explain how a revolutionary new technology gives them unprecedented access to the animal world in Disney and Pixar’s Hoppers."/>Beavering away at this experiment
<p><b>B</b><span>EAVERTON IS HOME </span>to a thriving colony of the furry, wood-chewing creatures. The mayor, however, doesn’t give a dam: he decides their forest habitat should be dislodged to make room for a freeway. Mabel, a teenage environmental activist, is outraged by these plans but faces apathy from the locals. After she discovers an experimental science project—one that allows her to “hop” her consciousness from her body into that of a robotic beaver—she befriends the animals and galvanises them to fight tooth and claw.</p>
<p>This is the zany premise of “Hoppers”, a new film (pictured), which is released on March 6th. It is one of a string of highly anticipated animated movies coming soon to cinemas: indeed, an unlikely coalition of beasts, toys and Italian plumbers may make 2026 the genre’s best-ever year. This summer there will be new instalments in the lucrative “Despicable Me” and “Paw Patrol” franchises, not to mention “The Super Mario Galaxy Movie” and <a href="https://www.economist.com/prospero/2019/06/21/toy-story-4-is-another-enjoyable-nostalgic-adventure">“Toy Story 5”</a> (both of which are expected to exceed $1bn in ticket sales). “<span>Goat</span>”, which imagines what would happen if various hoofed animals were allowed to play professional sport, has made $130m since its release in February, making it the second-highest-grossing film of the year so far.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00070.png" title=""/>
<p>Animated films are typically aimed at small children, but they are mighty popular with audiences of all ages. In 1995 animated films made up just 2.8%<b> </b>of the film market in America and Canada; in 2024 they accounted for 23.9%<b> </b>(see chart).<b> </b>That year “Inside Out 2” became the highest-grossing animated film of all time, taking $1.7bn at the global box office. In early 2025 it was eclipsed by “Ne Zha 2”, a <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2024/02/08/chinese-animated-films-are-booming">Chinese animated</a> fantasy which has now earned over $2bn. (Only six other films have surpassed the $2bn mark.) “Ne Zha 2” may soon be beaten by “Zootopia 2”, released in November, which has made $1.9bn. How did animation become such a draw?</p>
<p>First, storytellers have aimed for broad appeal. Many animated films touch on universal themes—the struggles of growing up or the experience of losing a loved one—rather than delving into politics. The best movies heed the “22 Rules for Storytelling” devised in 2011 by Emma Coats, then a storyboard artist at Pixar, a pioneering animation studio owned by Disney. She advised film-makers to focus on character and emotion. Viewers “admire a character for trying more than for their successes,” she reckoned. For impact, “stack the odds against” the protagonist.</p>
<p>Although the rules weren’t company dogma, the dictums are evident in Pixar’s most popular tales, from “Toy Story” and “Finding Nemo” to <a href="https://www.economist.com/1843/2016/02/26/inside-inside-out">“Inside Out”</a>. (So evident is Pixar’s formula that it has inspired an internet meme: “What if toys had feelings? What if fish had feelings? WHAT IF FEELINGS HAD FEELINGS?”)</p>
<p>Second, animation has global reach. It is not simply that, being made for children, such films generally avoid risqué content and are therefore less likely to anger overzealous censors than other Hollywood fare. It is also that animation translates well across boundaries. The most successful stories have simple premises or fantastical settings that are not culturally specific. Characters’ faces are stylised and expressive: think of Remy’s wide-eyed wonder in “Ratatouille” or the taciturn yet emotive robots in “Wall-<span>E</span>”.</p>
<p>Third, distributors have been canny about timing. The biggest animated films are released during school holidays, be it Thanksgiving, the Lunar New Year or the summer break. In<b> </b>2022, 44% of the animated films released by the top American film studios went straight to streaming services; in 2025 just 8% did. “A trip to the cinema is an easy win for parents looking to get their children out of the house and entertained for a few hours”, says Olivia Deane of Ampere, a media-analytics firm.</p>
<p>Clever scriptwriters include jokes for adults to enjoy—with or without kids in tow. When Shrek sees Lord Farquaad’s castle, he wonders: “Do you think maybe he’s compensating for something?” “Zootopia 2” includes nods to “Pulp Fiction” and <a href="https://www.economist.com/books-and-arts/2020/04/25/the-shining-is-perfect-quarantine-viewing">“The Shining”.</a> Film franchises tap into nostalgia, too. “Toy Story 5” will be watched by more than a few adults who saw “Toy Story” as youngsters 31 years ago.</p>
<p>Audiences become less animated when studios stray too far from these tenets. It does not bode well for “Hoppers”, with its tricky conceit and glaringly obvious ecological message; the beavers’ tale will almost certainly have less heft than other films released this year. (Like <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/08/11/bambi-offers-a-horrifying-portrait-of-eco-catastrophe">“Bambi”</a>, its darkness may prove offputting: the third act features a murderous humanoid robot getting its face ripped off and a horribly realistic depiction of a wildfire.) But the genre’s overall success contradicts a longstanding Hollywood tenet. It’s a great idea to work with animals and children. ■</p>
