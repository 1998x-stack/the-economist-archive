The world this week<h1>Business</h1><p>3月 05, 2026 04:16 上午</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00004.jpg" title="An arial view of the Pentagon."/>
<p>Donald Trump ordered American government agencies to <a href="../../feed_4/article_2/index_u78.html">stop using ·</a><a href="../../feed_4/article_2/index_u78.html"><b>Anthropic’s</b></a><a href="../../feed_4/article_2/index_u78.html"> ·</a><a href="../../feed_4/article_2/index_u78.html"><span>AI</span></a><a href="../../feed_4/article_2/index_u78.html"> technology·</a> within six months amid a row over its use in defence. The Pentagon wanted to use Anthropic’s <span>AI</span> for all legal purposes, but the startup wanted safeguards in place when it came to its use for mass surveillance as well as for autonomous weapons.  Anthropic is an “out-of-control radical left <span>AI</span> company”, fumed the president. It was pronounced a supply-chain risk to national security, an unprecedented designation for an American firm, although the tech is still reportedly being used in the Iran attacks. Anthropic and the Pentagon are said to be in talks to resolve their differences. https://t.me/demagazinesharing</p><h4>Where angels fear to tread</h4>
<p>Open<span>AI</span> stepped into the breach and signed a contract allowing the Pentagon to use its <span>AI </span>models, which it said dealt with Anthropic’s  objections. A few days later <b>Sam Altman</b>, the startup’s boss, said he would amend the contract. The rush to sign the deal “looked opportunistic and sloppy”, he said. “The issues are super-complex and demand clear communication.”</p>
<p><b>Block</b>, which owns Cash App, the Square payments app and bitcoin assets, announced that it would cut more than 40% of its workforce, or 4,000 jobs. Jack Dorsey, Block’s chief executive, said <span>AI</span> tools have “changed what it means to build and run a company,” and that most firms would soon make “similar structural changes”. Sceptics weren’t so sure, and said Mr Dorsey was using <span>AI</span> as a cover to slash staffing costs. The number of people Block employs has ballooned since the pandemic.</p>
<p>Brendan Carr, chairman of the Federal Communications Commission, suggested that <b>Paramount’s</b> proposal to buy <b>Warner Bros Discovery</b> could be approved, as it didn’t represent the same threat to competition as Netflix’s offer. The Department of Justice will carry out the work on an antitrust investigation into the combination of the two firms, which brings together big Hollywood studios as well as the <span>CNN</span> and <span>CBS</span> News networks. Netflix dropped its bid after <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/02/27/what-a-warner-bros-paramount-colossus-would-look-like">Warner described Paramount’s proposal as superior</a>. </p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00005.png" title=""/>
<p>Rachel Reeves, <b>Britain’s</b> chancellor of the exchequer, presented her <a href="../../feed_12/article_1/index_u19.html">spring statement·</a> on the country’s economic outlook to Parliament. The accompanying forecast from the Office for Budget Responsibility lowered its estimate of <span>GDP</span> growth for 2026 from 1.4% to 1.1%, and projected that unemployment would rise to 5.3%. Annual inflation is expected to fall to 2.3% (it was 3% in January). Welfare spending is set to rise to 10.9% of <span>GDP</span>.<span> </span>Observers described it as a snoozefest. That probably pleased the Labour government, which is trying to project economic stability. Events in Iran, however, might mean that some of the forecasts quickly become outdated.</p>
<p>The euro area’s annual <b>inflation</b> rate rose to 1.9% in February, from 1.7% in January. Surging energy prices caused by the Iran conflict are expected to add to inflationary pressures in Europe, and elsewhere.</p>
<p>The Chinese government set this year’s<span> </span><span><a href="../../feed_1/article_2/index_u73.html">GDP ·</a></span><a href="../../feed_1/article_2/index_u73.html">growth target·</a> at between 4.5% and 5%, the lowest range in decades, on account of the country’s property slump. Last year the economy expanded by 5%, right on the official target. <b>China</b> also laid out a strategic plan to boost <span>AI</span> and its “core digital economy industries”, and pledged to keep its competitive advantage in rare earths. </p>
<p><b><span>BYD</span></b>, the world’s biggest maker of electric vehicles, reported another drop in monthly sales. The Chinese company sold 190,190 new-energy vehicles in February, a drop of 41% year on year, in part because it is contending with a slowing domestic market. </p>
<p>The <b>Brazilian economy</b> grew by 2.3% in 2025, its slowest pace since the pandemic in 2020. <span>GDP</span> expanded by just 0.1% in the fourth quarter compared with the previous three months. The central bank is expected to cut interest rates for the first time since May 2024 when it next meets.</p>
<p>Greg Abel issued his first annual letter to shareholders at <b>Berkshire Hathaway</b>, having taken over the job of chief executive from Warren Buffett in January. Mr Abel described Berkshire’s massive cash pile of $373bn as a “strategic asset” that would enable it to make acquisitions “at the right time”.</p><h4>Brewer’s droop</h4>
<p><b>BrewDog</b>, a British craft-beer company which has seen its fortunes fall in recent years, agreed to sell its global brand, British brewing operations and 11 brewpubs to Tilray Brands, a “global cannabis and wellness leader”. Thirty-eight pubs will close immediately. BrewDog was founded in 2007 and became immediately popular with hipsters for its beers, such as Punk <span>IPA</span>. Its success spurred many imitators and now a lot of pubs sell selections of what they describe as craft beer.</p>
