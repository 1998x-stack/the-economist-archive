3:T20bc,<p><span><span><span>Brexit revisited</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>It’s time to rethink Britain’s relationship with the EU</span></span></p><p><span><span>Almost a decade after Brexit, both sides should be more courageous in getting closer together </span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 31, 2025 02:48 AM | Brussels and London</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00052.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HIS WEEK</span> marks the fifth anniversary of the trade and co-operation agreement (<span>TCA</span>) that sealed Britain’s departure from the European Union. In June it will be ten years since the Brexit referendum. It is a good moment to consider how Brexit has worked and where relations go next.</p><p><a href="index_split_050.html#filepos266944">The economic impact has become clearer·</a>. The Office for Budget Responsibility, Britain’s fiscal watchdog, sticks by the estimate that Brexit has reduced Britain’s <span>GDP</span> by 4% compared with its size had it not happened. A new assessment by the <span>US</span>-based National Bureau of Economic Research concludes that the hit was bigger still. And far from leading to <a href="index_split_049.html#filepos263516">a fall in immigration·</a>, the overall numbers coming in rose sharply.</p><p>One consequence has been a huge change in public opinion. The Brexit referendum was won above all on promises of taking back control and lower immigration at no economic cost. Yet in a new survey conducted for <span>The Economist</span> by the pollster More in Common, 52% of respondents now say the decision to leave the <span>EU</span> was wrong; just 32% think it was right (see chart). Only among those over 65 does the balance shift towards Brexit being right. If a referendum were held today, 47% would vote to join, compared with 32% preferring to stay out. A large majority think Brexit has led to higher not lower immigration. And 55% of respondents favour a closer trade relationship with the <span>EU</span> even at the expense of more barriers to trade with America.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00067.jpg"/></p><p>None of this necessarily translates into a burning desire to reopen the Brexit question. As many as 42% of respondents to our poll think it could have worked well but was badly done by politicians. Yet the shift in opinion against Brexit has encouraged Sir Keir Starmer to talk more openly about the damage done, even if the prime minister evinces little desire to soften Labour’s manifesto red lines of saying no to rejoining the <span>EU</span>’s<span>
</span>single market, no to a customs union and no to free movement of people. </p><p>Changed geopolitics has made a big difference. Russia’s war in Ukraine, an increasingly assertive China and the return of Donald Trump as American president make closer links with Europe look more desirable. A revivified “coalition of the willing” on defence and a bigger role for an <span>E3</span> group comprising Sir Keir, Emmanuel Macron of France and Friedrich Merz of Germany have a similar effect. All agree that in foreign policy and security, Britain and Europe must work more together.</p><p>Domestic politics are also shifting. After Labour’s 2024 landslide victory the new government seemed not to want to talk about Brexit. But, oddly, the rise of Nigel Farage’s populist Reform <span>UK</span> has changed this. As Brexit becomes politically tarnished, many in Labour hope to unite more pro-European voters behind their party. The idea of joining a customs union seems newly appealing: at least one cabinet minister, Wes Streeting, is openly floating it. Whether it would help much is less clear: no European country bar Turkey sees much benefit from a customs union alone, not least as it stops independent trade deals with third countries.</p><p>Last May Sir Keir and the European Commission’s president, Ursula von der Leyen, announced a “reset” to bring Britain back under the <span>EU’</span>s sanitary and phytosanitary (<span>SPS</span>) rules, create a youth-experience scheme for freer movement of young people, set up new arrangements for trading electricity and energy, and align carbon-border-adjustment schemes. Yet though the common understanding that underlay this reset was more ambitious than many expected, its impact will still be rather small: by the government’s reckoning, it may add only around 0.3% to Britain’s <span>GDP </span>by<span> 2040</span>.</p><p>The nitty-gritty of trade negotiations is also hard. Nick Thomas-Symonds, the minister in charge who is newly promoted to the cabinet, says both sides are going as fast as possible, but he admits that the earliest an <span>SPS</span> deal will take effect is 2027. <span>EU</span> diplomats in Brussels complain that the British always look for exemptions, in this case to continue with gene editing. Agreeing on greater youth mobility is tricky, too. The <span>EU</span> wants any scheme to be unlimited, but Britain wants a cap on numbers. On universities, the British have now agreed to rejoin the Erasmus system of student exchanges in 2027, at a discounted one-year cost of around £570m ($763m). But there will be further arguments about whether <span>EU</span> students should pay similarly high tuition fees to other foreigners.</p><p>Another <span>EU</span>-<span>UK</span> summit is due this summer. It is not clear if it will add new suggestions for the trade agenda. In the past the <span>EU</span> has resisted what it calls cherry-picking bits of its single market without accepting the obligations of alignment with <span>EU</span> rules, budget contributions and the free movement of people. Yet the reset is, in effect, an agreed form of cherry-picking. Other areas where the British might now seek to add preferential trade access could include steel, chemicals, pharmaceuticals and cars.</p><p>A recent House of Lords committee report suggested that more could be done for services trade. British exports of services to the <span>EU</span> have done relatively well, but new barriers have still had an impact. The Lords report suggested a renewed effort to facilitate touring by musicians and to seek mutual recognition of professional qualifications. Yet as one <span>EU</span> diplomat puts it, there is an “asymmetry of interest” when it comes to services.</p><p>And then there is money, not mentioned in May’s common understanding. The <span>EU</span> now insists Britain must pay to play. In November arguments over money derailed a British bid to join a new European defence fund known as <span>SAFE</span>. A French-inspired demand that Britain must pay up to €6bn ($7bn) was described by Lord Ricketts, chairman of the Lords Europe committee, as “bonkers”. Even a lower demand for €2bn proved too much. Charles Grant of the Centre for European Reform, a London-based think-tank, believes the French want to exclude Britain from <span>SAFE</span>. Mr Thomas-Symonds calls himself “ruthlessly pragmatic”: he will not sign up to anything if the price is too high.</p><p>The harsh truth is that the <span>EU</span>’s interest in softening the impact of Brexit is limited. It has many higher priorities, including Russia, internal economic reform and its own expansion. The <span>TCA</span> negotiated five years ago was pretty favourable to the <span>EU</span>. In Brussels there is also nervousness over Mr Farage’s rise. Why agree to a better trade deal with Sir Keir today if the next prime minister may tear it up tomorrow?</p><p>Yet even a low-priority Britain matters. It is the <span>EU</span>’s largest trading partner. It has a vital role in security. And it has done much to show the folly of leaving the club: Brexit has killed any idea of following suit. Even the risk of a Farage-led government argues for tearing down more barriers. Mr Thomas-Symonds says it would be damaging and unpopular to unpick any deals, as it would mean renewed red tape. The bigger risk is of missing the chance to do more to undo the damage of Brexit.■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in Britain, </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/blighty"><span>sign up</span></a><span> to Blighty, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","its-time-to-rethink-britains-relationship-with-the-eu-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
