3:T1554,<p><span><span><span>Edging together</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Britain and the EU should be bolder in getting closer</span></span></p><p><span><span>How post-Brexit Britain should approach relations with its nearest and biggest market </span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 02, 2026 01:46 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00061.jpg"/></p><p><span>N</span><span>EARLY A</span>
<span>DECADE</span> after Britain’s reckless vote to leave the European Union, debate over Brexit is back. The intervening years have not been happy. Tortuous negotiation under one prime minister, Theresa May, was followed by the hardest possible Brexit deal under another, Boris Johnson, damaging Britain’s economy through new barriers to goods exports. <a href="index_split_050.html#filepos266944">Estimates of the resultant loss to British ·<span>GDP</span> range from an irksome 4% to a dismal 8%·</a>. Even the promise of reduced immigration was not kept. As our latest polling confirms, most Britons now think Brexit was a mistake and favour closer ties with the <span>EU</span>, even more so than with America. Some voices in Britain’s governing Labour Party are floating the once-taboo idea of rejoining the customs union.</p><p>The<span> EU</span>, for its part, has plenty of other things to worry about, and Brexit seems like yesterday’s problem. But shifts in geopolitics—a less reliable America, a more menacing Russia—mean that it, too, has reason to rethink its ties with Britain. All this creates an opportunity for the two sides to move closer.</p><blockquote><span><span>Dig deeper</span></span></blockquote> <ul><li value="1"><a href="index_split_048.html#filepos254294">It’s time to rethink Britain’s relationship with the EU·</a></li><li value="2"><a href="index_split_050.html#filepos266944">Brexit has deepened the British economy’s flaws and dulled its strengths·</a></li><li value="3"><a href="index_split_049.html#filepos263516">How the “take back control” crowd boosted immigration to Britain·</a></li></ul><p>There have been promising, if modest, first steps. Britain has rejoined the <span>EU</span>’s Horizon programme, which promotes scientific collaboration. It is returning to the Erasmus scheme, letting young Brits study in Europe and vice versa. As part of a reset in May Britain agreed to realign with the <span>EU</span>’s rules on food safety and animal health, unblocking trade in farm goods and reducing barriers between Northern Ireland and Great Britain. The reset included plans for a broader youth scheme, easier cross-border travel, and a linkage of carbon-adjustment mechanisms and electricity trading. But the talks on all these have been slow.</p><p>Both sides are clinging to red lines. Sir Keir Starmer’s government is doggedly sticking to manifesto promises not to rejoin the single market, the customs union or the <span>EU</span>’s system of free movement of people within its borders. Red lines are often unhelpful when negotiating with a much bigger partner. But the<span> EU</span> is also too rigid in insisting on no exemptions from its rules and, as Britain starts moving closer to its single market, on unconstrained free movement. And it is prone to unrealistic demands for money. This recently sank any chance of Britain joining its new <span>SAFE</span> defence-spending fund, which would have been good for both Britain and the <span>EU</span>.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00006.jpg"/></p><p>More flexibility is needed. Britain rejoining the customs union might seem like a good start, but is not a quick fix. It would usefully end the need to prove that goods exported from Britain qualify as British, but it would not deal with the obstacles to trade that arise because regulations (eg, on chemicals) now differ between Britain and the <span>EU.</span> It would also mean undoing Britain’s trade deals with America, Australia and East Asia. Better to keep chasing a bigger prize: closer alignment with the single market, with its nearly 500m consumers.</p><p>Here the <span>EU</span> resists “cherry-picking” and insists on its reciprocal demand for free movement. However, the reset already allows cherry-picking when both sides agree. As for unrestricted free movement, Liechtenstein (admittedly tiny) is in the single market without it, as is Northern Ireland for goods alone. Switzerland, similarly in the single market for goods, has an emergency brake on migration. Baroness May’s Brexit proposal of 2019, voted down by Britain’s Parliament, would in effect have meant single-market membership in goods without free movement of people.</p><p>The best way for Britain to negotiate this would be to seek (and offer) a form of partial membership of the single market in exchange for partial application of free movement (for example, offering free movement for certain professions or services). The <span>EU</span> may anyway need to do this for several applicant countries to its east, including Ukraine. For both Britain and the <span>EU</span>, it is past time for a bolder approach.■</p><p><span>Subscribers to The Economist can sign up to our </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/opinion"><span>Opinion newsletter</span></a><span>, which brings together the best of our leaders, columns, guest essays and reader correspondence.</span></p>6:["slug","britain-and-the-eu-should-be-bolder-in-getting-closer-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
