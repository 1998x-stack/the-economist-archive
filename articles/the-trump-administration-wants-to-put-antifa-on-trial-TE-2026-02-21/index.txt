3:T12d2,<p><span><span><span>Anti-antifa</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The Trump administration wants to put antifa on trial</span></span></p><p><span><span>A case in Texas offers an opportunity to do so</span></span></p><p><span><span>Feb 19, 2026 05:47 AM | Fort Worth</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-02-21-00055.jpg"/></p><p><span>P</span><span>RECISELY WHAT</span> happened outside the Prairieland immigrant-detention facility in Alvarado, Texas, on the night of July 4th 2025 is still hazy. No one disputes the violence: that a protester shot and injured a police officer. Everything else—why he did it and, crucially, whether he had help—is contested. The other protesters that night maintain they came to light fireworks and yell nice things into a megaphone in Spanish to cheer up the detainees. The government accuses them of a co-ordinated, premeditated ambush. On February 23rd it will put nine of them on trial for, among other charges, supporting terrorism.</p><p>Elsewhere Donald Trump’s effort to tar protesters as guerrilla agitators has flopped. The two shot dead by federal agents in Minneapolis were a mother and a nurse. That officials in the administration rushed to label them terrorists was galling and it backfired. Across the country prosecutors have downgraded or dropped charges against protesters accused of impeding immigration agents. In one case in Chicago they charged a woman with assault, only to recant after footage showed Border Patrol ramming her with a car and shooting her five times. What were once rare and embarrassing setbacks for prosecutors are now commonplace.</p><p>The trial in Fort Worth will be different. The nine defendants deny the charges. But they may well stick—a cop was shot and government property was vandalised. The Trump administration wants to extract as much political juice from the case as it can. The Department of Justice (<span>D</span>o<span>J</span>) contends that the protesters belong to an “antifa cell”, part of a larger “militant enterprise”.</p><p>Though only one person fired a gun at Prairieland, at least 19 were arrested, some of whom did not attend the protest at all. One defendant is charged with evidence tampering for moving a box of anarchist zines out from under the eyes of police. The initial complaint in July alleged crimes such as attempted murder, but made no mention of antifa or terrorism. Then Charlie Kirk was assassinated in September and Mr Trump, vowing retribution, ordered law enforcement to train its might at antifa.</p><p>Terrorism charges against the Prairieland defendants followed in October. Kash Patel, head of the <span>FBI</span>, went on Fox News to argue that antifa is a terrorist organisation, not an amorphous social movement, which is how practically everyone else understands it. He cited the <span>D</span>o<span>J</span>’s own charging document. “We are treating them as terrorists just as we do <span>ISIS</span> or al-Qaeda.”</p><p>Thomas Brzozowski, an ex-<span>D</span>o<span>J</span> hand, calls this “breathing life into a construct that they’ve created”. To secure convictions on the terrorism charges, prosecutors will need to prove that even the defendants who didn’t shoot intended to help murder an officer or vandalise property. “This is not the type of folks we’re talking about,” says Patrick McClain, a lawyer for one of them; they are “people who want to stand up for people they have empathy for”.</p><p>Defence lawyers will have to square that with some of the evidence. Two defendants allegedly scoped out the facility before the protest to log security cameras and police presence; others packed guns and body armour in their cars. The protesters allegedly communicated on messaging apps with an auto-delete feature for “opsec” purposes.</p><p>The trial may not end the argument. “If this was a group of people that sat around and talked about how violence is necessary to influence the government, and if there’s evidence of those conversations, it’s not extreme to call that group a terrorist cell,” says Francesca Laguardia of Montclair State University. “That doesn’t make antifa a terrorist organisation.
