3:T12f5,<p><span><span><span>Runners-up club</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Europe’s long-awaited free-trade deal with India</span></span></p><p><span><span>One way to show America and China that also-rans have options, too</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 29, 2026 08:47 AM | Delhi</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-31-00119.jpg"/></p><p><span>N</span><span>ARENDRA MODI’S</span> guests of honour at Indian Republic Day on January 26th were treated to the usual military parade—helicopters overhead, hypersonic missiles on lorries. This year, however, they were joined by a small contingent of soldiers flying the European Union’s banner. The bloc’s two top officials were in attendance: Ursula von der Leyen, president of the European Commission, and António Costa, president of the European Council, which represents the <span>EU</span>’s member states. At a summit the next day the two sides announced a range of accords, among them a free-trade pact. Donald Trump deserves some credit: without his <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/01/19/donald-trumps-greenland-tariffs-are-no-great-blow-to-europe">predilection for levying tariffs</a> and alienating allies, the pair might not have struck a deal.</p><p>Europe and India face a similar problem. “They are trying to preserve their strategic autonomy as secondary players in their respective spheres, India v China in the Indo-Pacific and Europe v America in the Atlantic,” says Chietigj Bajpaee of Chatham House, a British think-tank. India is increasingly looking west for trade relationships, while shunning Asian competitors. The <span>EU</span> looks for opportunities anywhere. On January 17th it signed a <a href="https://www.economist.com/europe/2026/01/11/europe-and-south-america-seal-a-trade-pact-for-the-trump-era">pact with Latin America’s Mercosur trade zone</a>.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-01-31-00103.jpg"/></p><p>In the short run, an <span>EU</span>-India deal will change little. India is only the <span>EU</span>’s ninth-biggest trading partner. At about €76bn ($91bn), exports to India are just 0.4% of the <span>EU’</span>s <span>GDP</span>. The current average tariff rate on Europe’s exports to India is only about 9% anyway. But some reductions matter: tariffs on European cars will gradually fall from 110% to 10%, subject to a quota. Non-tariff barriers, such as India’s quality-control orders that can keep exports stuck at ports for weeks, are worse. The agreement promises some improvements, but mostly left contentious regulatory areas untouched.</p><p>The effect on India is a bit larger. It is trying to soften the blow from the 50% rate Mr Trump imposed on it as part of his global tariff rampage, and from his punitive tariffs on countries buying Russian oil. India’s €105bn in exports of goods and services to the <span>EU</span> roughly matches the $129bn it exported to America in 2024. </p><p>The current average <span>EU</span> tariff on Indian imports is just 4%. As India is strongly protectionist, the deal is shallower than others the <span>EU</span> has recently signed. Controversial segments, including many agricultural goods, were excluded. Others are reserved for separate agreements, notably the geographical indicators that protect collective brands such as Greece’s feta or India’s basmati rice.</p><p>The deal is mainly about promoting longer-term co-operation. A security and defence partnership was signed alongside the free-trade pact. This “shows a higher level of trust”, says Garima Mohan of the German Marshall Fund, an American think-tank. India is hoping that its ability to produce at scale will complement European arms producers. A deal on skilled migration, mostly from young India to ageing Europe, was also signed. An agreement to expand India’s participation in Horizon, the <span>EU</span>’s flagship research-funding programme, is being explored.</p><p>The two sides still have plenty to disagree on. India will not jettison its long-standing relationship with Russia, despite that country’s war on Ukraine. The <span>EU</span>’s latest package of sanctions on Russia included three Indian entities. India, for its part, considers the <span>EU</span>’s new carbon border adjustment mechanism, which taxes imports whose production emits a lot of greenhouse gases, to be protectionism masquerading as environmentalism. But this week’s deals are striking: they signal that runners-up to the global superpowers have options of their own. ■</p><p><span>To stay on top of the biggest European stories, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/cafe-europa"><span>Café Europa</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","europes-long-awaited-free-trade-deal-with-india-TE-2026-01-31","d"]
