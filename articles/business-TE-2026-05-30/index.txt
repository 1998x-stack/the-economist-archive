3:T1656,<p><span><span><span>The world this week</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Business</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 04:54 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00084.jpg"/></p><p><span><span>BP</span></span><span>’s</span> share price fell sharply, after the oil company <a href="index_split_066.html#filepos392886">abruptly removed Albert Manifold·</a> as chairman. In a statement the board said that serious concerns had been raised about Mr Manifold’s “governance oversight and conduct”, which news reports suggested was to do with his pugnacious managerial style. At <span>BP</span>’s annual general meeting in April 18% of shareholders voted against a resolution to make Mr Manifold a director. Mr Manifold said that the claims of him being bullying and overbearing in the job were a “false narrative” and suggested that he’d been ousted for his “determination to drive change on costs” among other things. He became <span>BP</span>’s chairman only last October.</p><p><span>Not all according to plan</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/leaders/2026/05/21/spacex-is-capitalism-on-rocket-fuel"><span>SpaceX</span></a><span>’s</span> inaugural test of its redesigned <span>V</span>3 Starship rocket was mostly successful. The upper part satisfactorily released 22 satellite simulators and completed a planned crash landing in the Indian Ocean. But the booster lost control and hurtled into the Gulf of Mexico. Jared Isaacman, <span>NASA</span>’s boss, was at SpaceX’s launch site in Texas. His agency hopes to use a modified Starship to land humans on the Moon.</p><p>The <span>Dutch government</span> blocked a takeover of Solvinity, a firm that provides the platform for the Netherlands’ digital <span>ID</span> app, by Kyndryl, an American company, concluding that it was a “possible risk to the public interest”. It is the first time the Dutch agency that screens foreign investments has blocked an American acquisition since its creation in 2020.</p><p>More consolidation beckoned in the food-delivery industry as <span>Delivery Hero</span> confirmed that it had been approached about a takeover by <span>Uber</span>. Uber has increased it stake in Delivery Hero, which is based in Berlin and operates under various brands in 65 countries outside the United States. Last year DoorDash, Uber’s main rival in America, agreed to buy Deliveroo, which conveys pizza and Chinese dishes to households across Britain and the <span>EU</span>.</p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00068.jpg"/></p><p>Powered by huge demand for their memory chips <span>Micron</span> and <span><span>SK</span></span><span> Hynix</span> both passed $1trn in market value, joining Samsung, which recently rose above that mark. Prices for memory chips were twice as high in the first quarter than in the previous one because of the voracious appetite from data centres for use in <span>AI</span>, causing a supply crunch in consumer electronics, such as smartphones and laptops. Samsung’s and <span>SK</span> Hynix’s stock drove South Korea’s <span>KOSPI</span> index to another record. The two companies account for half the <span>KOSPI</span>’s market capitalisation.</p><p>American and South Korean chip companies are doing well, but “Taiwan is the epicentre of the <span>AI</span> revolution”, according to Jensen Huang, <span>Nvidia’s</span> chief executive. Mr Huang was speaking at a ceremony that marked the start of construction on Nvidia’s new offices in Taiwan, which brings it physically closer to <span><span>TSMC</span></span>, a big manufacturer of the advanced semiconductors used in <span>AI</span> and an important partner for Nvidia. Mr Huang reckons his company will eventually invest $150bn a year in Taiwan.</p><p><span>Labour and capital</span></p><p>Staff who work in <span>Samsung’s</span> chip business, meanwhile, voted to accept a deal that will see them share 10.5% of the company’s operating profit. The agreement averts a damaging strike. Some workers will reportedly get bonuses of around $400,000 under the settlement.</p><p>The European Central Bank held a meeting with <span>banks in the euro zone</span> to discuss their plans for dealing with security risks from <span>AI</span>, and Anthropic’s Mythos model in particular. Luis de Guindos, who is stepping down as the <span>ECB</span>’s vice-president, said that the banks should invest more in cyber-security as the risks of <span>AI</span> tapping into their systems was “going to be quite structural in the near future”.</p><p><span>Sri Lanka’s</span> central bank raised its benchmark interest rate by one percentage point, to 8.75%. It was the biggest increase in three years and took markets by surprise. Like neighbouring India, Sri Lanka is battling surging inflation caused by the spike in energy prices and a depreciating currency, as it pays more in dollars for its imported energy.</p><p>A marketing campaign by the operator of Starbucks in South Korea has backfired spectacularly. <span>Starbucks Korea</span> promoted a drink tumbler it called the “tank” and designated May 18th as “Tank Day”. Unfortunately, that is also the anniversary of a revolt in the city of Gwangju in 1980 against the country’s then dictatorship, when actual tanks killed over 200 protesters. The campaign also used the strapline “Thwack it on the table!”, which some took as a reference to the killing of a student activist.
