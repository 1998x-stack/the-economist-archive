My con and only<h1>Why Westerners are falling for love scams</h1>Romance fraud is driven by poverty in west Africa and loneliness in the West, a new book argues<p>6月 18, 2026 03:40 上午</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00069.jpg" title="A cyber cafe in Lagos, Nigeria"/>
<p><b>The Yahoo Boys.</b> By Carlos Barragán. <i>Farrar, Straus and Giroux; 304 pages; $29. W&amp;N; £22</i></p>
<p><b>I</b><span>t was love</span> at first sleight. As with many relationships, Brian’s and Silvia’s courtship started on Tinder. Brian, a dashing American soldier, sent Silvia, a Spanish dentist, passionate affirmations of love: “I want you to be a happy wonderful woman, someone I would love to share more than my life with.” Brian said he was coming to Spain, and Silvia bought two rings.</p>
<p>But Brian was not a real soldier; he was a <a href="https://www.economist.com/audio/podcasts/scam-inc">scammer</a> posing as a suitor. He told Silvia he had found bars of solid <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2026/05/14/the-weird-wild-story-of-humanitys-obsession-with-gold">gold</a> while on deployment in Syria. Silvia’s son grew suspicious. He thought it might be a scam and that his mother would be asked to send money to cover the shipping. They used an <span>IP</span>-address tracker to find out where Brian’s emails were coming from: the fraudster was in Lagos, <a href="https://www.economist.com/topics/nigeria">Nigeria</a>.</p>
<p>Silvia is not the only lonely Westerner to be wooed by a phoney Romeo. Romance scams are one of the most costly types of fraud in America, according to the Federal Trade Commission: people gave away nearly $1.5bn to so-called “love scammers” in 2025. In Britain reports of romance fraud jumped by nearly a third last year, with people losing as much as £1m ($1.3m).</p>
<p>Silvia’s son, Carlos Barragán, was distressed and baffled that his mother had been duped. So in 2022 he flew to Lagos to search for the scammer. Though it proved impossible to find the real Brian, Mr Barragán discovered the “criminal underworld” of romance fraud. In an engrossing new book he takes readers from Kentucky to Lagos to explore how loneliness in the West and poverty in west Africa are “two sides of the same screen”.</p>
<p>Many love scams are run by crime syndicates in South-East Asia. Mr Barragán expected to find a “sophisticated network of fraudsters” in Nigeria, too: instead he encountered unemployed young men in rough neighbourhoods, who use copy-and-paste scripts rather than complex manipulation tactics. Many work alone, staying up all night to avoid their parents’ prying eyes.</p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00070.jpg" title="A small boy who had previously worked as a scammer sits in a chair in Ikotun, Nigeria. "/>
<p>In Nigeria <a href="https://www.economist.com/1843/2025/03/14/inside-the-world-of-vigilante-scam-baiters">Yahoo Boys</a>—a nickname that nods to the email addresses scammers once used—say they rely on fraud to survive. Between 2022 and 2025 Mr Barragán spent months in Ikotun, a poor neighbourhood in Lagos, witnessing the effects of high youth unemployment and rampant inflation. He saw that for scammers like Azeez (pictured), a 14-year-old tailor’s apprentice, a $10 gift card from a British victim is worth more than his family pays in rent a month. (With some of their earnings Yahoo Boys prop up local businesses, splashing out at fancy boutiques and nightclubs.) Mr Barragán asks workers, policemen, academics and Yahoo Boys themselves to estimate the share of young men who are involved in such scams in Lagos: their answers range from 45% to 80%.</p>
<p>The Yahoo Boys call their victims “clients” to make their work seem legitimate; some see their relationships as transactional, providing companionship in exchange for cash. They share tactics with each other for finding vulnerable victims online, as well as the latest pickup lines and plausible scenarios to ask for money to be wired. Some pretend to have a sick child who needs help; others blackmail victims, threatening to share intimate pictures if they do not pay up.</p>
<p>Mr Barragán wonders how credulous Westerners can fall head-over-heels for such smarmy scripts. He looks “for a con so intricate it might justify” his clever mother believing it. But he finds something alarmingly simple: the scammers are successful because they provide attention to <a href="https://www.economist.com/christmas-specials/2025/12/18/the-most-friendless-place-on-earth">lonely</a> people.</p>
<p><span>AI</span> will complicate love scams. It could make it easier to <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/08/19/how-ai-powered-hackers-are-stealing-billions">target</a> a much larger number of victims with personalised correspondence, without relying on teams of boys and men (and growing numbers of women) in Nigeria or elsewhere. But <span>AI</span> could also offer safer ways for people to feel less lonely. Various apps now provide <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2026/03/05/who-wants-a-partner-to-toady-to-them-quite-a-lot-of-people">virtual lovers</a> that are even more fawning than fraudsters. As Mr Barragán puts it, <span>AI</span> partners provide “a fake relationship without the betrayal”. Yahoo Boys may struggle to compete with that. ■</p>
