3:T16e2,<p><span><span><span>Pilgrims’ progress</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>War has not deterred Asian Muslims from the hajj</span></span></p><p><span><span>Yet they are finding the costs ever harder to bear</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 04:55 AM | Singapore and Dhaka</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00064.jpg"/></p><p><span>Y</span><span>OU “NEED</span> a lifetime to prepare” for a “once-in-a-lifetime opportunity”, says Zahir Junid, a 57-year-old from Selangor, a state in western Malaysia. He has been saving money for decades. Recently he has been getting physically fitter, by running and playing pickleball, and keeping mentally sharp by attending talks at mosques. So when, a few months ago, he at last secured a spot on the <span>hajj</span>, he knew nothing would stop him from undertaking the Islamic pilgrimage to Mecca. Not even a war.</p><p>In recent months Iran has fired missiles and drones towards countries in the Gulf, including Saudi Arabia. Travel to the region has been disrupted. It has also become more expensive, as jet-fuel prices have soared. Yet around 1.7m pilgrims are nonetheless expected to take part in this year’s <span>hajj</span>, of which the majority will travel from Asia. On May 25th they begin five days of rituals that have changed little for centuries. These include the <span>tawaf</span>—walking seven times anti-clockwise around the <span>Kaaba</span>, a building at the centre of Mecca’s Grand Mosque—and the <span>sa’i</span>, walking or running seven times between two hills.</p><p>Demand for the <span>hajj</span> remains high because it is one of the five “pillars” of Islam. The Koran instructs all Muslims to complete it once if they are financially and physically able. Some countries, such as Malaysia and Indonesia, always easily use up an annual quota of visas that Saudi Arabia grants them. Around 5.7m Indonesians are on a waiting-list, but the world’s biggest Muslim-majority country gets only 220,000 places. Some wait 40 years to be selected. If they are still alive when their turn comes, they are unlikely to decline it.</p><p>Saudi Arabia is reported to have asked Donald Trump to delay any new strikes on Iran until after the <span>hajj</span>. This year some pilgrims have had to fly via alternative routes; there remains some worry that if fresh conflict were to close airspace in the region many of them would get stuck there. The very devout have the least to worry about. “If people die during <span>hajj</span>, they think they will get into heaven as a reward,” says Hussein Ahmed, who runs an Islamic tour company in Bangladesh.</p><p>“We see more police and army,” says a tour guide in Mecca, but otherwise “everything is normal”. There will be little discussion of the war; Saudi Arabia has formally banned political and sectarian slogans and stressed that the gathering is “dedicated to worship”. It is expecting to welcome 30,000 Iranians. Absar Uddin, a Bangladeshi businessman on the <span>hajj</span>, has found it “hard to tell who is Iranian” anyway, as “thousands of people are praying together” dressed in the same white garments.</p><p>Some Asian countries have offered pilgrims extra help meeting the costs of travel during the war. Indonesia’s government has reportedly allocated 1.8trn rupiah ($100m) from the state budget to cover their additional expenses. Other pilgrims must stump up themselves. An Indian government body that organises pilgrimages is charging an extra 10,000 rupees ($105) because of the “situation in the Middle East”. This is lower than an amount requested by Indian airlines, but still a fortune for many.<br/>Yet even before recent hostilities began, many Asian pilgrims were having to make peace with rising prices. Mr Ahmed says that in 2019 a package cost around 300,000 Bangladesh takas ($2,440); by this year it was 600,000 takas. Few salaries have doubled in that time.</p><p>This is in part because flights have become more expensive since the pandemic. In several Asian countries inflation has also surged and currencies have depreciated. Saudi Arabia has knocked down old, cheaper accommodation as it has built new, swanky hotels in Mecca. “<span>Hajjis</span> are happier with the facilities,” says Mr Ahmed, but “<span>hajj</span> is now affordable only for the rich.” Mr Junid says middle-class Malaysians who could have paid for the trip a few years ago now struggle to do so.</p><p>Higher prices meant Bangladesh failed to fill its <span>hajj</span> visa quota between 2023 and 2025, despite usually doing so before the pandemic. This year it agreed to lower its allowance to 78,500, from 127,000. To cut costs in future, the government is considering sending pilgrims to Mecca by sea, something it has not done since the 1980s. Some Bangladeshis now choose instead to perform the <span>umrah</span>, a cheaper mini-pilgrimage that happens year-round. It restarts on June 1st.</p><p>But the <span>umrah</span>, too, is becoming more expensive. On that pilgrimage, as on the <span>hajj</span>, the gap between rich and poor is stark. Wealthy pilgrims pay for VIP packages and stay in nicer hotels, closer to the Grand Mosque. And, partly because there is no quota for the <span>umrah</span>, it is easier to perform it more than once. Some brag about how many times they have made the trip and post their stats on social media. This seems to somewhat undermine the spirit of the event. All Muslims are meant to be equal before <span>Allah</span>. ■</p><p><span>For exclusive coverage of Asian politics, economics and security, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/asia-bulletin"><span>Asia Bulletin</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","war-has-not-deterred-asian-muslims-from-the-hajj-TE-2026-05-30","d"]
