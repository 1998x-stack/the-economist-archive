3:T26d7,<p><span><span><span>A selection of correspondence</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>What can you learn from the Premier League? </span></span></p><p><span><span>Also this week, school meals in Indonesia, commuting, Barney Frank, MAGA tax, condoms</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jun 11, 2026 05:51 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-13-00055.jpg"/></p><p><span>Letters are welcome via email to </span><a href="mailto:letters@economist.com"><span>letters@economist.com</span></a><br/><a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2025/03/26/a-note-for-readers"><span>Find out more</span></a><span> about how we process your letter</span></p><hr/><p><span>A goal for the government</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/leaders/2026/05/21/lessons-from-the-premier-league-for-britains-next-premier">The British government could learn a thing or two from the success of football’s Premier League</a>, you said (“How a deflated Britain can bounce back”, May 23rd). If Britain truly replicated the league it would mean that every major sovereign asset would be foreign-owned, over 75% of the top workforce would be imported and our domestic leadership class would be so thoroughly hollowed out that we have to hire overseas managers to run both our enterprise and our national teams. That is not a strategy for national renewal; it is the definition of economic dependency.</p><p>Furthermore, your dismissal of the new independent football regulator ignores the catastrophic failure of the league’s in-house self-regulation. The system is structurally broken. A lower-tier club like Southampton can be hit with a near-existential expulsion from the championship play-off final for “spying” offences, whereas an elite club like Chelsea escapes with a paltry fine for admitting to making secret payments to avoid player-transfer rules from 2011-18, before the new owners took over. Meanwhile, the entire football world holds its breath for the Manchester City financial-scandal verdict, amid predictions that, owing to the club’s geopolitical and Gulf-state connections, it will face nothing more than a slap on the wrist. Self-regulation in English football has simply resulted in one lenient rule for the global elite and a heavy hammer for the rest.</p><p>Britain should look past the Premier League’s caution-to-the-wind capitalism and turn to Germany’s Bundesliga. By enforcing the 50+1 rule, which ensures that domestic fans own the clubs, and mandating strict domestic player and coaching quotas Germany has maintained full-capacity, affordable stadiums, financial sustainability and an elite conveyor belt of domestic managerial talent.</p><p>The Premier League is an entertaining global circus, but Britain merely provides the tent while overseas actors take the profits and the prestige. </p><p><span>MARCUS HUDSON</span><br/><span>Benwick, Cambridgeshire</span></p><p>Openness and competition are certainly factors in Britain’s football success, but the foundation of a common standard also matters. In 1848 students arrived at Cambridge from schools with incompatible versions of football. They met on Parker’s Piece, a large park, reconciled their differences into 11 simple rules and nailed them to the trees. Those Cambridge Rules became the defining influence on the Football Association’s code in 1863, and ultimately on the game now played in every country on earth. Today’s monument on Parker’s Piece reflects this; it is engraved with the rules in various languages. The rules travelled not because they were imposed, but because they were simple, trusted and open, allowing serendipity to do the rest. Parker’s Piece was not a market. It was a protocol. Britain’s revival needs both the competition you celebrate and the institutional spaces where common standards can emerge.</p><p><span>DAVID CLEEVELY</span><br/><span>Cambridge</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/britain/2026/05/20/in-football-britain-has-a-world-beating-industry">Sweden’s parliament may use the term “Spursy” to warn “against messing up despite being well placed to succeed”</a> (”World champions”, May 23rd). Indeed, much like the way scurvy is caused by a lack of vitamin <span>C</span>, perhaps Spursy should properly refer to chronic hypertension brought on by a severe lack of trophies at Tottenham Hotspur despite endless potential.</p><p><span>ROBERT SHEPHERD</span><br/><span>Calgary, Canada</span></p><hr/><p><span>Benefits of free school meals</span></p><p>Your description of <a href="https://www.economist.com/briefing/2026/05/14/indonesias-president-is-jeopardising-the-economy-and-democracy">the Indonesian government’s free-school-meals scheme as a “populist” policy</a> misses the point of this highly effective form of government intervention (“Archipelagoing fast”, May 16th). School-feeding programmes attract more children to school, reduce the prevalence of stunting and other diseases associated with malnutrition and are a strategic investment in human capital.<br/>Perhaps most important, these programmes encourage parents to send more girls to school, and girls who spend more time in study tend to marry later, have fewer children and have more career choices. School-feeding programmes are often launched by <span>UN</span> agencies and adopted by governments, which then carry the budgetary burden of supporting them going forward. They are an enlightened and effective example of aid in action.</p><p><span>GREG BARROW</span><br/>Formerly of the <span>UN</span> World Food Programme<br/><span>London</span></p><p>President Prabowo Subianto’s full response to our briefing on Indonesia is at <a href="https://www.economist.com/letters/2026/06/10/president-prabowo-subianto-responds-to-our-recent-briefing">economist.com/prabowo-response</a></p><hr/><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-06-13-00052.jpg"/></p><p><span>On the buses</span></p><p>Bartleby (May 9th) ran through <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/05/07/the-pros-and-cons-of-commuting">the pros and cons of commuting in terms of economic costs</a>. But what about the opportunity benefits? For 20 years I rode a commuter bus from the Maryland suburbs to the nearest metro station to take me to downtown Washington. The commute provided chance encounters with new contacts, knowledge of new contracting opportunities, expanded my networks of contacts, led to several informal job interviews, acquisitions of vital reports, new professional references, and surprise invitations to house parties from riders of that 8.10 bus. These morning and evening commuting experiences led to at least several million dollars in overseas development projects. Meanwhile, other passengers on the same commuting run hooked up with new partners and spouses. More was often accomplished in my commutes to and from work than at my desk and during meetings the rest of the day.</p><p><span>GEORGE GUESS</span><br/><span>Potomac, Maryland</span></p><hr/><p><span>To be Frank</span></p><p><a href="https://www.economist.com/obituary/2026/05/28/barney-frank-always-took-the-underdogs-side">The obituary for Barney Frank</a> (May 30th) was notable in two ways. First, it broke the habit of defining him primarily by his sexual orientation, listing three minorities to which he belonged: left-handed, Jewish and believing in government. You mentioned his being gay only later. Second, it challenged readers to acknowledge the degree to which anti-government attitudes dominate American political life. Frank belonged to a minority who believe we can do better together. We often get absorbed in political issues, which need discussing, but miss this point.</p><p><span>DR GORDON HARPER</span><br/><span>Chestnut Hill, Massachusetts</span></p><hr/><p><span>The public’s choice</span></p><p>Yes, <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/05/18/how-much-is-donald-trump-costing-americas-economy">Donald Trump has produced an economic drag from his tariffs, immigration policies and policy uncertainties</a> about nearly everything (“Imagining America without the <span>MAGA</span> tax”, May 23rd). But at the election in 2024 the alternative was Kamala Harris, who as good as guaranteed to increase marginal tax rates, add tens of thousands of regulatory pages to the Federal Registry, allow fewer corporate mergers, and add more constraints on credit allocation, all of which would have slowed America down even more. Choosing the perfect, <span>Economist</span>-reading libertarian could have produced a much better “rocketing” economy. But that wasn’t the political reality in 2024.</p><p><span>RONALD FIELDING</span><br/><span>Kiawah Island, South Carolina</span></p><hr/><p><span>Taking a long, hard look</span></p><p>Your report on<a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/05/27/the-worlds-top-condom-maker-is-getting-squeezed"> Karex, the world’s top condom maker</a>, was heavy on the puns (“War and love”, May 30th). The company was “looking flaccid”, and its “failure to perform” had sapped its “virility”, though “investors would be foolish to pull out now.” My only complaint is that the article should have been a column.</p><p><span>HOLLY JAMES</span><br/><span>Port Hope, Canada</span></p><p>Gosh, until I read your article on the contraception industry I had no idea that condom manufacturers faced such stiff global competition.</p><p><span>DEBRA BROCK</span><br/><span>Harker Heights, Texas</span></p><p>I found the language used in the article on Karex to be underwhelming and limp. In the audio version I anticipated hearing <span>AI</span> renditions of the voices of Hattie Jacques, Joan Sims or Barbara Windsor, but my expectations were sadly unfulfilled.
