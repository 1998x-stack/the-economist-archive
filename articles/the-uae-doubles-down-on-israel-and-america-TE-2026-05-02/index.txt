3:T151e,<p><span><span><span>Geopolitics and the Gulf</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The UAE doubles down on Israel and America</span></span></p><p><span><span>The consequences of the Emirates’ departure from OPEC</span></span></p><p><span><span>Apr 30, 2026 07:52 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-05-02-00006.jpg"/></p><p><span>A</span><span>T LAST IT </span>has jumped. For years the United Arab Emirates (<span>UAE</span>) chafed at restrictions that came with membership of the Organisation of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (<span>OPEC</span>). It had even threatened to quit; and on April 27th, in the middle of a war, it stormed out.</p><p>That raises two sets of questions. The straightforward one is about the future of <span>OPEC</span> itself. A more complex one is about security in a part of the world rocked by the war with Iran. In spite of all the problems that America and Israel have caused the Emirates with their unfinished campaign, the <span>UAE</span>’s abandonment of <span>OPEC</span> has sent an unambiguous signal that it remains in their camp. As much as that may please President Donald Trump, it is a slap in the face for the rest of the Gulf, and especially Saudi Arabia.</p><blockquote><span><span>Dig deeper</span></span></blockquote> <ul><li value="1"><a href="index_split_040.html#filepos212083">The ·<span>UAE</span>’s departure from ·<span>OPEC</span> may not break the cartel·</a></li></ul><p>The Emiratis have good reason for leaving <span>OPEC</span>. In the next few years they want to increase oil production, from 3.6m barrels a day to 5m. Other members prefer to try to keep the price of oil high; the Emiratis want volume.</p><p><a href="index_split_040.html#filepos212083">Does that mean ·<span>OPEC </span>is·<span>
</span>dying?·</a> Perhaps slowly. The Emirates’ departure is another blow to an already waning body. Other members, such as Venezuela, may follow. But the Saudis, who in effect lead the organisation, downplayed the news. The Emiratis often broke production quotas and the remaining members, plus allies such as Russia, will still account for some 40% of global oil output. It may even be easier to keep discipline without the troublemaker inside. In any case, <span>OPEC </span>will matter again only after the war, when a global oil glut is once again possible.</p><p>The geopolitics are more pressing. The news of the <span>UAE</span>’s decision was met with glee in Israel. People there are delighted by anything that weakens Iran, which remains inside the cartel. And the <span>UAE</span>’s pullout suggests it wants warmer relations with the Jewish state. The two governments, signatories of the Abraham accords, share an intense antipathy towards Islamist extremism. The <span>UAE</span> has avoided clashes with Israel even as it pursued destructive wars in Gaza, Lebanon and beyond. Other Arab states fear Israel as a disruptive regional power, even a dominant one; the <span>UAE</span> still wants to do deals with it.</p><p>Second, and closely related, the <span>UAE</span> is sending a message to America, where Mr Trump can see <span>OPEC</span>’s loss as his own strategic gain. The <span>UAE</span> has suffered more than any of its near neighbours from Iranian missile and drone strikes. Yet rather than publicly blame Mr Trump for starting the war, the <span>UAE</span> has chided other countries for offering too little solidarity. By quitting <span>OPEC </span>now, the <span>UAE</span> shows that it looks to America and Israel for security. As important, it is seeking to further develop an economy that complements its exports of fossil fuels, with ties to America in travel, investment and technology.</p><p>Last, the <span>UAE</span> is showing it is willing to further alienate the six-member Gulf Co-operation Council, and most obviously Saudi Arabia. The two monarchies have been feuding, needling one another in proxy conflicts even as both are threatened by Iran. The bitterest clash is in Yemen’s protracted civil war, where the Saudis bombed an Emirati arms shipment in December, bringing the two sides close to a direct military clash. The bloodiest effects of their rivalry are in Sudan, where the Saudis support the government and the <span>UAE</span> has backed a genocidal rebel force (an allegation that it continues to deny strenuously).</p><p>Much remains uncertain, not least the question of how America and Iran will eventually make something resembling peace. The <span>UAE</span>’s bet on forging closer ties with America and Israel, while drawing away from its Gulf neighbours, comes laden with risk. For now, the <span>UAE</span> must expect to pump less oil, not more, regardless of its relationship with <span>OPEC.</span> In the medium term, Iran could emerge from the war more powerful, with a lingering chokehold over the Strait of Hormuz, and thus over its neighbours’ economies. That is a danger for all the other Gulf countries. Finding ways to unite against it would seem wise. Instead, the <span>UAE</span> is setting its own course. ■</p><p><span>Subscribers to The Economist can sign up to our </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/opinion"><span>Opinion newsletter</span></a><span>, which brings together the best of our leaders, columns, guest essays and reader correspondence.</span></p>6:["slug","the-uae-doubles-down-on-israel-and-america-TE-2026-05-02","d"]
