Six books to understand the Gilded Age 

Stories about the ultra-rich—and the ultra-poor

Mar 26, 2026 05:57 AM

IN THE HALF-CENTURY or so between the civil war and the first world war, America was transformed. Millions of immigrants arrived from abroad, flocking to growing cities where new factories made the country an industrial power. The “robber barons” amassed vast fortunes on the backs of hard-pressed workers. Many politicians looked the other way. Reform-minded journalists stepped into the breach, appealing to Americans’ professed ideals. These six books chronicle a deeply unequal, often cynical old order slowly giving way to something new.

The Age of Innocence. By Edith Wharton. Barnes & Noble; 336 pages; $8.95. Penguin Books Ltd; 336 pages; £8.99.

Before she began publishing novels, the author wrote about design, particularly interiors. In this book, published in 1920, she expertly describes the stuffy rooms and dinner parties of elite 1870s New York. This is the Gilded Age, the closing decades of the 19th century. High society’s self-involvement and tired customs act like gold leaf on a rotting frame, covering up social problems wrought by immigration, industrialisation and urbanisation. The author shows how the 1% managed even to smother themselves. One contemporary reviewer warned readers that the “uncanny” novel would catapult them back 50 years to the era’s claustrophobia and stagnation—“the total loss of life in upholstered experience”.

Death in the Haymarket. By James Green. Knopf Doubleday; 400 pages; $17.95. Random House; 400 pages; £15.99.

Historians segment centuries into eras. Such divisions helpfully corral related events, but can obscure the basic fact that the people who participated in them had no idea what “age” they were living in. In May 1886 a group of socialists and anarchists were finishing up a meeting at Haymarket Square in Chicago when someone threw a bomb, and police opened fire. Today we can observe that the activists were responding to the excesses of the Gilded Age. But the author’s lively narrative reminds readers that the Americans devouring the news were thinking about a different historical chapter: the civil war. The Haymarket affair captivated the country in part because it rekindled the fear, as a Chicago theologian observed, that it was merely “the first explosion”.

The Republic for Which It Stands. By Richard White. Oxford University Press;  941 pages; $28.99, £21.99.

This book about the second half of the 19th century also uses the civil war as a reference point. It begins with the funeral of Abraham Lincoln. The following 900 pages recount how the president’s ideals—freedom, integrity, equality—were seemingly buried along with him. The author blames the breakdown of Reconstruction squarely (and harshly, some reviewers insist) on Lincoln’s Republican Party, which went on to oversee decades of corruption and scandal. Fat cats got rich even while the country was plunged into a series of economic panics and depressions. For the common man, living standards fell. The author marshals statistics to show how Gilded Age Americans were shorter and had shorter lifespans than their 18th-century counterparts. Lincoln’s promise of “a new birth of freedom” would not be realised quickly.

Titan: The life of John D. Rockefeller Sr. By Ron Chernow. Random House; 774 pages; $30

John Rockefeller was the quintessential American success story. The son of a con artist, Rockefeller built a monopolistic empire through ruthlessness, guile and underhanded tactics. He became the richest man in the world, and arguably the most reviled. His immense charitable giving did not wash away the stain of his crimes during his lifetime, as he had hoped, but did burnish his family name after his death. Until Mr Chernow came along. His monumental biography offers an unparalleled glimpse into the hardened soul of one of the most private, inscrutable figures in the modern history of business and finance. It is an ideal lens for understanding the rise of American titans and of outsize corporate power—and the emergence of liberal forces who challenged them.

How the Other Half Lives. By Jacob Riis. Barnes & Noble; 304 pages; $9.95. Penguin; 256 pages; £8.99
The Jungle. By Upton Sinclair. Barnes & Noble; 400 pages; $9.95. Penguin; 448 Pages; £12.99

Muckraking journalists aimed to expose corporate greed and highlight social injustices. Few did it better than the authors of these books. Jacob Riis’s work, published in 1890, features bleak (and sometimes staged) photographs of life in New York City’s crowded tenements. It led to reforms that increased fire safety and required flats to have such luxuries as windows.

Upton Sinclair’s novel is about Chicago’s meatpacking plants and the immigrant workers who toiled in them. It drew on the seven weeks he spent reporting in the community. The story is wildly melodramatic and, like Riis’s photographs, heavy-handed. It was also effective, prompting the passage of food-safety laws—though not labour laws. “I aimed at the public’s heart,” Sinclair conceded, “and by accident I hit it in the stomach.” ■
