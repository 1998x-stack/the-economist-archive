3:Tfb9,<p><span><span><span>Lines of control</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Snarled airports and frozen funding test the new DHS secretary</span></span></p><p><span><span>Markwayne Mullin promises a kinder, gentler department</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM | LOS ANGELES</span></span></p><p><img src="/the-economist-archive/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00056.jpg"/></p><p><span>“M</span><span>Y GOAL IN</span> six months is that we’re not in the lead story every single day.” Markwayne Mullin, confirmed as boss of the Department of Homeland Security (<span>DHS</span>) on March 23rd, wants to soften its image. Over the past year it has become the spearhead of Donald Trump’s mass-deportation campaign. Its officers have killed two American citizens and, critics say, beaten up many more. Mr Mullin, a former senator, suggests Immigration and Customs Enforcement (<span>ICE</span>) should act more like a “transport” service than a front-line force. He agrees that agents should obtain judicial warrants before entering private property, and has described the administration’s fight with unco-operative cities as a “misunderstanding”.</p><p>His first test, however, is more mundane. For over a month Democrats have refused to fund the <span>DHS</span> unless the bill includes new restrictions on <span>ICE</span> officers. Many of the department’s staff are deemed essential and therefore required to keep working without pay. That has caused hardship and resentment. More than 450 Transportation Security Administration (<span>TSA</span>) officers have quit during the standoff. On March 23rd nearly 11% of <span>TSA</span> officers called in sick, says the department, leading to hours-long queues at airports.</p><p>The situation has become so strained that Mr Trump has dispatched <span>ICE</span> agents to help. The union representing <span>TSA</span> officers says this is unsafe and unhelpful: <span>ICE</span> agents are not trained to screen passengers. For now they appear to be guarding exits and checking <span>ID</span>s. “None of this makes sense,” says a former <span>ICE</span> official, who wonders why the administration did not instead tap customs officers, many of whom already work at airports.</p><p>Throngs of angry travellers may focus minds in Congress. (So, perhaps, may Delta Air Lines’ announcement that members of Congress will no longer be allowed to jump the queues.) One option under consideration is a deal to fund most of the <span>DHS</span>, but not <span>ICE</span>’s removal operations. Republicans would then try to pass a separate <span>ICE</span> funding bill using a procedure requiring only a simple majority. That measure would also contain elements of the <span>SAVE</span> America Act, an obsession of Mr Trump’s, which would require Americans to provide proof of citizenship to register to vote and a photo <span>ID</span> when casting a ballot.</p><p>Democrats, however, say any funding bill must include reforms to <span>ICE</span>, such as requiring agents to wear body cameras and remove their masks—something they have done at airports, but which Mr Trump insists they should not do when making arrests. Some Republicans also appear uneasy about carving out <span>ICE</span> funding. And the president himself has been non-committal. “I don’t want to comment until I see the deal,” Mr Trump said after Mr Mullin was sworn in. “But I think any deal they make, I’m pretty much not happy with.” ■</p><p><span>Stay on top of American politics with </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/us-in-brief"><span>The </span><span><span>US </span></span><span>in brief</span></a><span>, our daily newsletter with fast analysis of the most important political news, and </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/checks-and-balance"><span>Checks and Balance</span></a><span>, a weekly note that examines the state of American democracy and the issues that matter to voters.</span></p>6:["slug","snarled-airports-and-frozen-funding-test-the-new-dhs-secretary-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
