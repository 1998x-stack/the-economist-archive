3:T126f,<p><span><span><span>Food poisoning</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Big food’s troubles go from bad to worse</span></span></p><p><span><span>High prices and healthier diets are gnawing away at sales</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00012.jpg"/></p><p><span>N</span><span>EARLY A CENTURY</span> ago the unlikely combination of a margarine business and a soapmaker created Unilever, one of the world’s largest conglomerates. Now it is being unwound. On March 20th the British company announced that it is exploring selling its remaining portfolio of food products, which includes Hellmann’s mayonnaise, Knorr soups and Marmite, to McCormick, an American spicemaker, having rid itself of its ice-cream division last year. That would leave the company focused on personal-care and household-cleaning products.</p><p>It has been a <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/07/17/kraft-heinz-is-not-the-only-food-giant-in-trouble">bruising few years</a> for makers of snacks, spreads and other pre-made edibles. The value of packaged-food companies in the <span>S</span>&amp;<span>P</span> 500 has declined by a third from a peak in mid-2023, even as the index as a whole has risen by three-fifths. The industry outperformed during the post-pandemic surge in inflation, during which it managed to pass on rising costs to consumers—and then some. Between 2021 and 2024 large food brands increased their prices in America by a cumulative 11 percentage points above inflation, reckon analysts at <span>TD</span> Cowen, an investment bank.</p><p>That, however, left them vulnerable to competition. Shoppers turned to cheaper private-label offerings from retailers such as Costco and Aldi, as well as upstarts. Goodles, a macaroni-and-cheese brand launched in 2020 and endorsed by Gal Gadot, an actress, has already captured 6% of the market in America. That has been bad news in particular for Kraft Heinz, which last year said it would break itself up. Its new boss, who took over in January, has paused the separation in order first to get the business on surer footing.</p><p>That will not be easy. War with Iran, and the resulting spike in energy prices, has raised the prospect of another wave of inflation. Already there are concerns that the industry will face a surge in the cost of plastic packaging. Raising prices for shoppers, whose patience has already been tested, may prove difficult.</p><p>Pressure is also mounting on the industry from consumers’ growing interest in healthy eating. Google searches related to ultra-processed foods have risen 30-fold worldwide since the start of 2022, helped by campaigners such as Robert F. Kennedy junior, America’s health secretary. In January five American states barred recipients of federal food vouchers from using them to buy sweets and other items of dubious nutritional value. Several more are set to follow. Nor is Uncle Sam the only one waging war on big food. In January British regulators announced a <a href="https://www.economist.com/britain/2025/09/29/britains-strict-new-curbs-on-junk-food-marketing">ban on advertising junk food</a> online, and before 9pm on television.</p><p>Then there is the impact of weight-loss drugs such as Wegovy and Zepbound, which are spreading rapidly <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/10/28/weight-loss-drugs-are-spreading-across-the-world">across America and beyond</a>, and not just among the obese. A survey published last year by <span>EY</span>, a professional-services firm, examined the change in spending patterns of consumers already taking them, and concluded that weight-loss drugs could erase a cumulative $12bn of snack sales in America over the next decade, around 3% of the total. And although most users currently live in the rich world, generic versions are beginning to roll out in India and other markets in the global south, which company bosses had been eyeing as a source of future growth.</p><p>Big food is aware of the difficulties that lie ahead. Many of the industry’s largest companies have developed new product lines aimed at health-conscious shoppers. And nearly all have appointed a new chief executive since the beginning of 2025. That includes Nestlé, the biggest of the bunch, which last month said that it too would sell its ice-cream business. Among other things, the Swiss company is betting on healthy frozen meals. On March 23rd Danone, a French dairy giant that has benefited from the rising popularity of yogurt, announced that it would gobble up Huel, a British maker of protein-packed meal-replacement drinks.
