The Bank of England’s eyes and ears

Even in the age of AI, human intelligence matters for monetary policy

Mar 26, 2026 05:57 AM | Birmingham and Edinburgh

IN A NONDESCRIPT office in Birmingham, Graeme Chaplin nods as a manufacturer complains about increases to the minimum wage. These have not just made lower-skilled workers “unaffordable to employ”, the industrialist says, but prompted senior staff to demand big pay rises. Seasoned employees’ pride requires them to maintain a wage premium over the “young lad who sweeps the floor”.

A month later, in Edinburgh, a property businessman tells a similar tale. The minimum-wage increase has left old-timers paid little more than new-starters. “That just doesn’t feel right,” he says. Will Dowson, one of Mr Chaplin’s colleagues, listens without passing judgment.

Messrs Chaplin and Dowson are not besuited therapists for disgruntled capitalists. They are agents of the Bank of England, serving as the bank’s representatives in, respectively, the West Midlands and Scotland. Their job is to tell Threadneedle Street what is happening in the real economy. Each year the bank’s agents conduct 6,000 interviews with businesses, from independent restaurants to large conglomerates. The conversations are mostly face-to-face and can be very candid. Mr Dowson says that contacts sometimes tell him sensitive news before almost anybody else. During the financial crisis, he recalls, one executive told him about plans for large layoffs before informing his own board.

Why does the bank bother with such an old-fashioned operation? Reams of near-real-time economic information are produced daily, from credit-card data to Google trends to satellite images of car parks. AI stands ready to synthesise it all at a speed and scale that no network of agents could match. At first glance, the agents seem an anachronism.

Mr Chaplin disagrees. “Agents’ intelligence is as important as it has ever been,” he says. Andrew Bailey, the bank’s governor, has called agents “our eyes and ears”. He visits each of the 12 regions at least once a year to hear from businesses directly. Nor is Britain unusual in using such methods. The Federal Reserve publishes its Beige Book, a summary of anecdotal reports gathered by its 12 regional banks, eight times a year.

Agents offer two advantages over other data, says Mr Chaplin. The first is timeliness. Official numbers often arrive slowly and are then heavily revised. Agents’ reports can fill the gap. High-frequency indicators have narrowed this advantage, but not eliminated it. There are still areas, such as business investment, where the latest data are not always publicly available. Take April 2nd last year, when Donald Trump announced large tariff increases. The markets panicked, and calls mounted for emergency action. But businesses were surprisingly sanguine in their feedback to agents, which helped steady rate-setters’ nerves.

The second advantage of agents’ intel, according to Mr Chaplin, is that it provides the “flesh on the bone”: why firms do what they do and what they plan to do next. In Birmingham one manufacturer relates how higher employment costs mean that next year he will focus on investing in AI and automation, not people. A restaurateur talks of the need to cut staff. When that message is repeated thousands of times across the country, it provides further evidence of a weakening labour market.

Anecdotal intelligence has limits. Businesses may not be straight with agents, whether out of embarrassment or a British desire to grumble. Those willing to talk may not be representative; a firm on the verge of insolvency is unlikely to have time to chat with their friendly local central banker. But as Mr Chaplin points out, agents’ intel is there to complement more quantitative data, not replace it.

The network has its 200th anniversary this year. Charles Cripps, the first agent, was sent to Gloucester in 1826 with a sub-agent, four clerks and two porters on a mission to increase the circulation of bank notes. The role has since changed almost beyond recognition. It is likely to change again. The bank is already exploring whether large language models can help summarise agents’ reports and counteract some human biases. But even as it does so, it may find that some of the best intelligence still begins with a conversation. ■
