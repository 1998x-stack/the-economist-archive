3:T18a3,<p><span><span><span>Back Story</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The ice-cool hero of “The Night Manager” saunters to the rescue</span></span></p><p><span><span>John le Carré’s enthralling character saves the show’s shaky second season  </span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 09, 2026 12:42 PM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00044.jpg"/></p><p><span>I</span><span>T BEGINS WITH</span> the slyly ambiguous title. Superficially, to be a “night manager” sounds servile and tedious. Yet with a moment’s reflection the moniker becomes mysterious, even sinister. “Night” suggests darkness, moral as well as nocturnal. “Manager” hints at the firm grip of someone who handles that turpitude on your behalf: the world’s darkness, and perhaps his own.</p><p>A decade after the first season of “The Night Manager” brought <a href="https://www.economist.com/obituary/2020/12/14/john-le-carre-died-on-december-12th">John le Carré</a>’s novel to the small screen, his unrufflable protagonist is back for a second (on <span>BBC</span> iPlayer in Britain and Amazon Prime Video elsewhere from January 11th). Among its other lessons—not least how best to roll the sleeves of deluxe linen shirts—the drama is a study in character, and the varied ways to craft one who captivates and endures.</p><p>That <a href="https://www.economist.com/1843/2016/02/22/the-night-manager-i-spy-a-tv-hit">first series, a hit in 2016</a>, introduced Jonathan Pine, a British ex-soldier and night manager in upmarket hotels, played by Tom Hiddleston with a feline stride and misleading grin. In an atmosphere humid with omen and intrigue, he became embroiled in a shadow world of subterfuge and gun-running; his foe was the suavely wicked Richard Roper, “the worst man in the world” (and, in the book, a reader of <span>The Economist</span>, as villains often are). The<span> TV</span> version had extra homicide and hanky-panky, plus a comeuppance that Roper evades in the illusionless original.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00106.jpg"/></p><p>He is missed in the new season, as is his deliciously vituperative henchman, Corkoran, whom Pine bumped off. As the story opens, Pine, now going by Alex Goodwin—the latest but not the last in a dizzying carousel of aliases—is a different sort of manager, overseeing a nocturnal surveillance operation in London. A ghost from the past draws him into the orbit of another charismatic <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/03/06/prisoner-swaps-are-controversial-but-there-are-sure-to-be-more">arms-dealer</a>, “Roper’s true disciple”, and away from his desk to an explosion in Spain and a decadent pool party in <a href="https://www.economist.com/topics/colombia">Colombia</a>.</p><p>The new adventure is untethered from the novel, and it shows. It strives to recapture le Carré’s mix of dodgy traders and rogue spies, sexy glamour and filthy cynicism, but too often the plotting and dialogue lack his worldly bite. Eking out a franchise beyond its source material—as in the long <span>TV </span>run of <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/05/22/the-handmaids-tale-reveals-the-limits-of-dystopian-television">“The Handmaid’s Tale”</a> or “Lord of the Rings” spin-offs—can seem venal and unimaginative. That “The Night Manager” just about pulls it off is down to Pine, a character of genius.</p><p>From ancient myths to modern fan fiction, some charmed characters have broken free of their texts and assumed lives of their own, leaping between narratives and art forms. Among the best loved and most resilient are some, such as <a href="https://www.economist.com/christmas-specials/2025/12/12/how-jane-austen-revealed-the-economic-basis-of-society">Jane Austen</a>’s heroines, whom readers and viewers feel they know intimately, sharing their anxieties and thinking about them after a story ends. These figures can seem as lifelike as real people, or more.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00009.jpg"/></p><p>Pine is not one of those. True, in Mr Hiddleston’s lithe turn, he is vividly physical. Like <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2021/10/15/good-evening-ms-bond-weve-been-expecting-you">James Bond</a>, a part for which the season of 2016 made Mr Hiddleston a favourite, he has a talent for violence (both dispensing and withstanding it) and a wayward libido. This guy likes women and they like him back, even before he discards his dowdy glasses early in the new series like a hot secretary in a corny film. He can hold his booze and psychotropic drugs.</p><p>Yet he also has a sentimental streak, a moral compass and an infernal conscience. Though rarely mentioning them, he is haunted by former lovers, the military father he lost young, and his own grimmest deeds. Even when he isn’t running, which he does a lot, he is a soul in flight. In the book he is glimpsed “lunging up and down the pool like a man who could never get clean”.</p><p>And all of this—the appetites, the guilt, the moral drive and the pressures of his deceptions—is suppressed behind his “hotelier’s smile”. For all the derring-do, Pine is a master of self-control, keeping his cool emotionally as well as barely seeming to sweat. He is as much a passive observer as an action hero, with what le Carré calls “the watcher’s stare that stays on you after he’s looked away”.</p><p>As Pine/Goodwin/whoever thrashes around in a jungle of conspiracies, the viewer strives to excavate the real him from the swirl of bluffs and impersonations. “You give the air of looking for someone,” a doomed paramour tells him in the novel, “but I think the missing person is yourself.” Rather than a surrogate friend or confidant, he is an avatar for all that is opaque and inscrutable in other people. He shows that some great characters last not because you know them well, but because you can’t. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","the-ice-cool-hero-of-the-night-manager-saunters-to-the-rescue-TE-2026-01-10","d"]
