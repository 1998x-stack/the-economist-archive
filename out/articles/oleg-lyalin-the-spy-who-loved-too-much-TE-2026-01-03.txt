3:Tf62,<p><span><span><span>Espionage</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Oleg Lyalin: the spy who loved too much</span></span></p><p><span><span>His defection in the cold war helped MI5 turn the tables on the KGB</span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 30, 2025 09:06 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00045.jpg"/></p><p><span>The Defector. </span>By Richard Kerbaj. <span>Blink; 304 pages; £25. To be published in America by Pegasus in September; $29.95</span></p><p><span>F</span><span>OR OLEG LYALIN</span> it was a dream posting. After a dull stint in Klaipeda, on the Baltic coast, he was on his way to “swinging London” in 1969. Ostensibly he was joining a Soviet trade mission; in fact Lyalin, a hard-drinking philanderer, worked for Department <span>V</span>, a <span>KGB</span> outfit that specialised in sabotage and assassinations. Two years later, he became one of the cold war’s most important defectors, handing Britain’s security service, <span>MI5</span>, a trove of intelligence that restored its reputation and led to the expulsion of 105 Soviet spies.</p><p>Drawing on recently declassified <a href="https://www.economist.com/the-economist-reads/2022/12/21/what-to-read-to-understand-intelligence-and-espionage">intelligence</a>, Richard Kerbaj tells a gripping story of Lyalin’s role in helping to turn the tables in a <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2024/06/06/how-to-hire-a-spy">spying game</a> that the West had been losing. Unlike <a href="https://www.economist.com/books-and-arts/2018/09/20/a-gripping-tale-of-cold-war-espionage">Oleg Gordievsky</a>, who began passing secrets to <span>MI6</span>, Britain’s foreign intelligence service, a few years later, the reasons for Lyalin’s defection were personal rather than ideological.</p><p>Lyalin walked into a police station and declared himself a <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/07/17/uncovering-the-foibles-of-the-kgb-and-the-cia"><span>KGB</span></a> officer. He wanted to be formally expelled so he could return home as a hero and divorce his wife. (He was involved with five other women.) In return, he would provide <span>MI5</span> with information about the <span>KGB</span>’s mission in Britain and continue working for it from Moscow.</p><p>A few months later, police arrested Lyalin, who was driving erratically down Tottenham Court Road, “shouting and swearing”. Lyalin’s disgruntled wife, meanwhile, had been in touch with his bosses in Moscow questioning his loyalty to the cause. The result was an order to pack his bags and face “severe administrative action”.</p><p>Defection was the only option. Three weeks later, the British government ordered the mass expulsion of <a href="https://www.economist.com/books-and-arts/2019/04/20/a-rollicking-biography-of-richard-sorge-a-master-soviet-spy">Soviet spies</a>. The author argues that Lyalin’s defection “saved” <span>MI5</span>. That may be going too far, but it restored the agency’s confidence after <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/12/14/a-spy-among-friends-dramatises-the-treachery-of-kim-philby">Kim Philby</a>, a British spy, was revealed in 1963 to be working for Moscow.</p><p>The way the game is played has changed, but the story still has resonance: Vladimir Putin choreographs the same kind of “grey-zone” operations against the West as his Soviet predecessors. As Sir Richard Moore, the outgoing head of <span>MI6</span>, recently stressed: “Many of our best and most highly motivated agents have been volunteers...Our door is always open.” ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","oleg-lyalin-the-spy-who-loved-too-much-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
