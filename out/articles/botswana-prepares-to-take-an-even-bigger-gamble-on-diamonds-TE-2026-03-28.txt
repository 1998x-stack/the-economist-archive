3:T1e76,<p><span><span><span>From Anglo to Botswanan</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Botswana prepares to take an even bigger gamble on diamonds</span></span></p><p><span><span>The African country wants to own more of De Beers, the firm that helped it to escape poverty</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM | CAPE TOWN</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00018.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE DIAMOND</span> pendant worn by Bogolo Kenewendo (pictured, below), Botswana’s minerals minister, in February at a mining conference in Cape Town, was statement jewellery. The diamonds mined by De Beers, the firm long synonymous with the gem, helped turn the southern African country into one of the richest states on the continent. And today Botswana wants to double down on the gems, by adding to the 15% stake it has in the storied firm.</p><p>The looming sale of <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2024/05/22/can-anyone-save-the-worlds-most-important-diamond-company">De Beers</a> by its parent company, Anglo American, comes as the price of natural diamonds has plummeted because of cheap <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2025/02/06/dont-propose-with-a-diamond">lab-grown alternatives</a> and, to a lesser extent, fewer marriages (and thus sales of engagement rings) in America and China, two large markets. The estimated value of De Beers has fallen to $2.3bn from $12.75bn in 2011. For some potential investors this represents a bargain. But for Botswana, historically Africa’s largest producer of diamonds by value, raising its stake even at that valuation would be a gamble—and could prove a parable of how, in trying to extract more value from natural resources, African countries become more dependent on them.</p><p>The history of Botswana is linked to De Beers like diamonds in a gold necklace. The firm, which takes its name from two South African brothers on whose land diamonds were discovered in the 19th century, was jointly founded (with another magnate) by Cecil Rhodes, a Victorian imperialist. In 1967, a year after Botswana’s independence from Britain, geologists discovered the first of several big deposits. Two years later the country set up Debswana, a joint mining venture with De Beers. Over the years Botswana has negotiated better and better terms, including in 2004 the acquisition of the minority stake in De Beers.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00021.jpg"/></p><p>From 1970 to 2000 Botswana’s economy grew by an average of 10.6% per year, a slightly higher rate than China’s 9% average for the period. It prudently managed the windfall, investing in infrastructure, schools and clinics. Governments put budget surpluses into rainy-day funds like the Government Investment Account.</p><p>But Botswana does not sparkle as much these days. The slump in diamonds, which account for about 25% of <span>GDP</span> and 80% of exports, has in effect halted growth since 2023. And as revenue from diamonds has fallen, Botswana has emptied the rainy-day fund. The ratio of debt to <span>GDP</span> has almost doubled since 2023; last year two credit-rating agencies downgraded Botswana’s sovereign bonds. The budget deficit for the fiscal year beginning in April is forecast to be almost 9% of <span>GDP</span>.</p><p>All potential buyers of De Beers, whether private consortia or diamond-producing countries (Angola and Namibia are pondering bids, too) believe that the slump in natural diamonds can be reversed. The industry has always been a mix of mining, manufacturing and marketing. The slogan “A diamond is forever”, coined in 1947 for De Beers, helped revive interest in the gems in the wake of the Great Depression. Gareth Penny, a former De Beers boss and part of one consortium, says that turning the firm around requires reviving demand for natural diamonds, a luxury product. “In over 30 years in this business I have never met a consumer that bought a diamond simply because it was shiny,” he says.</p><p>But Botswana, which accounts for 72% of De Beers’s production, has other reasons to bid. It is seeking a stake of at least 25%, since that would give it a “blocking minority” to veto major decisions. The government would like more say in the running of the country’s most important firm.</p><p>Reports last year that Angola was eyeing a majority stake caused panic in Botswana. Angola has recently ramped up diamond production. In 2024 it displaced Botswana as the largest producer by value in Africa for the first time in 20 years. From Botswana’s perspective, Angola’s acquisition of De Beers would feel a bit like Qatar or the United Arab Emirates buying Saudi Aramco, Saudi Arabia’s huge oil firm.</p><p>Botswana believes that, if it owns more of De Beers, it can make money beyond mining. “There’s a whole ecosystem in the business that we are interested in. We believe that will help catapult other industries in Botswana,” says Ms Kenewendo. Duma Boko, the president, has said: “We are not just custodians of the earth’s wealth, we must become architects of its value.”</p><p>Yet sceptics question Botswana’s thinking, for three reasons. The first is a shortage of money. In December the <span>IMF</span> warned against the country increasing its stake, citing its “fiscal situation”. If Botswana raises money by pledging a portion of future diamond sales, that only delays the problem.</p><p>The second is concern that Botswana is unlikely to extract more benefit from the diamond value chain. It has struggled to rival India in the cutting and polishing of stones—a low-margin business, like the trading of the gems that De Beers has partly located in Botswana. Efforts by De Beers to develop a retail line have been unsuccessful. Keith Jefferis of Econsult Botswana, a consultancy, argues that the most profitable part of De Beers remains mining. He questions why the government would want to increase its exposure to relatively poor-performing parts of the firm. “There is no evidence that there is money being made down the De Beers value chain [and] that, if we increased our stake, we would get more revenue.”.</p><p>The third criticism of the bid is that Botswana should be trying to diversify away from diamonds. While the gems have undoubtedly been a boon for the country, Botswana is also a “classic case of Dutch disease”, argues Mr Jefferis, making other potential export industries less competitive. He suggests using scarce resources to help local export-oriented manufacturing.</p><p>Despite Mr Boko’s remarks in November that Botswana wants a majority stake, the most likely scenario is that De Beers is bought by a broad consortium involving private investors and African governments, with the former in majority control. “It probably doesn’t make sense to go all out,” concedes an executive at Debswana. Angola, too, has rethought the wisdom of bidding for a majority stake. In Cape Town the country’s mining minister, Diamantino (really) Pedro Azevedo, talked about the importance of “all market players working together”.</p><p>As for Botswana, it is unclear whether it should be bidding at all. The country’s elite is inevitably influenced by the role diamonds have played in the country’s history. The danger is that Botswana ends up looking like an irrational romantic trying to raise money for a diamond ring it will struggle to afford—for an engagement that may not be the right match.■</p><p><span>Sign up to the </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/analysing-africa"><span>Analysing Africa</span></a><span>, a weekly newsletter that keeps you in the loop about the world’s youngest—and least understood—continent.</span></p>6:["slug","botswana-prepares-to-take-an-even-bigger-gamble-on-diamonds-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
