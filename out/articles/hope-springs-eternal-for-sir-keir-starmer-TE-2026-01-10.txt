3:Tac3,<p><span><span><span>Turning a corner?</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Hope springs eternal for Sir Keir Starmer</span></span></p><p><span><span>The latest reading from our Starmer tracker</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 09, 2026 08:40 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00062.jpg"/></p><p><span>N</span><span>EW YEAR</span>, new me. That is what Sir Keir Starmer would like the British public to believe. In an interview on January 4th with the <span>BBC</span>, the prime minister asserted half a dozen times that Britain would “turn a corner” this year.</p><p>Sir Keir’s first full calendar year in power has been bruising. His government backed down on several key policies in the face of backbench rebellions; he has struggled to lead with conviction. <a href="https://www.economist.com/interactive/2025-british-politics">Our poll tracker</a> suggests that Labour would win just 17% of the vote if a general election were held tomorrow. Never has a governing party slumped so low.</p><p>Could change be just around the corner? In 2024 Sir Keir formulated six goals to achieve by 2029: raise incomes across Britain; add 13,000 neighbourhood police; ensure 75% of five-year-olds are “ready to learn”; build 1.5m homes; cut hospital waiting lists and—by 2030—generate 95% of electricity with clean power. It is too soon to judge how Labour is doing on the first three, but on the latter three it is far behind schedule.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-10-00070.jpg"/></p><p>Judging by <span>The Economist</span>‘s own “<a href="https://www.economist.com/interactive/2025-british-politics/starmer-tracker">Starmer tracker</a>”, which aggregates eight indicators that voters tell us matter most to them, the government is flatlining. There has been some good news: household utility bills have fallen thanks to mellowing global energy prices. In April they will decline by another £150 ($200) a year when green levies are removed from domestic bills.</p><p>However, housebuilding is at a ten-year low and progress towards the National Health Service’s target that 95% of patients arriving in <span>A&amp;E</span> should be seen within four hours has been marginal, from 58% to 60% (see charts). Meanwhile, efforts to “smash the gangs” of people smugglers have so far proved futile. People crossing the Channel on small boats increased by 13% in 2025.
