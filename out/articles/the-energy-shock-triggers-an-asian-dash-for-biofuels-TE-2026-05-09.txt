3:Tadc,<p><span><span><span>Farm to fuel</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The energy shock triggers an Asian dash for biofuels</span></span></p><p><span><span>But tackling one crisis could exacerbate another </span></span></p><p><span><span>May 07, 2026 07:58 AM | SINGAPORE</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-05-09-00116.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE BLOCKADE </span>of the Strait of Hormuz is forcing Asia’s energy importers to scour new markets. Thailand wants to buy more oil from Nigeria and Kazakhstan; Vietnam is now sourcing from Angola and Argentina. But even as countries cast their nets wider for crude, they are also reaching for a resource closer to home: farms.</p><p>Across Asia, governments are announcing or accelerating plans to expand the use of biofuels. Indonesia, for example, wants to roll out <span>B</span>50—a 50% palm-oil blend of diesel—by July 1st. Vietnam has begun selling <span>E</span>10, a blend of ethanol (10%) and petrol, ahead of a mandated switch on June 1st. Last week India, which has already rolled out <span>E</span>20, began the process to step up to even stronger blends.</p><p>The push is driven by energy security. In Indonesia the move to <span>B</span>50 is expected to save the country around 48trn rupiah ($2.8bn) in subsidies and slash consumption of diesel by some 4bn litres a year, which the energy minister hopes will mean the end of diesel imports. In India the introduction of<span> E</span>20 in 2014 and the resulting reduction in oil imports has already saved around 1.4trn rupees ($16bn) in foreign exchange.</p><p>But there are problems. For one thing, blended fuels are often unpopular. In India motorists grumble that their older engines aren’t equipped to handle <span>E</span>20. The competition for cropland may be the biggest cost. Growing crops for fuel can hurt food production and drive up prices. In 2024-25 the Food Corporation of India diverted rice from its vast public stocks to the country’s ethanol programme. In the Philippines the president has proposed pausing the coconut-based biodiesel mandate, fearing it would push up prices.</p><p>The effects can transcend borders. If Indonesia funnels more palm oil into fuel tanks, that will leave less for exports, increasing global cooking-oil prices and hurting consumers in countries that rely on imports. All this would come as the stalemate in the Middle East raises fertiliser costs.
