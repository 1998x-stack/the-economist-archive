3:T1e1c,<p><span><span><span>Yemen’s civil war</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>A lightning advance by separatists has reshaped Yemen’s civil war</span></span></p><p><span><span>It has also deepened the rift between the Saudis and Emiratis</span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 31, 2025 02:04 AM | DUBAI</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00106.jpg"/></p><p><span><span>Editor’s note:</span></span><span> On December 30th the United Arab Emirates said it would withdraw its troops from Yemen after Saudi Arabia backed the Yemeni government’s demand for their departure. Its military presence in the country was small, and it made no announcement about a pullback by the Southern Transitional Council.</span></p><p><span>C</span><span>IVIL WARS</span>, like revolutions, tend to devour their children. For over a decade the conflict in Yemen has ostensibly pitted the Houthis, an Iranian-backed Shia militia, against a coalition of their foes supported by Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates (<span>UAE</span>). Yet the latest chapter in the war has little to do with the Houthis. Instead the coalition has turned on itself.</p><p>On December 2nd the Southern Transitional Council (<span>STC</span>), secessionists backed by the <span>UAE</span>, went on a lightning offensive across the south-east. They seized territory held by Saudi-backed factions and now control the eight governorates that made up the former South Yemen (see map), an independent state from 1967 to 1990. The power grab has reshaped the war in Yemen. It has also deepened a rift between the two Gulf powers, already at odds in Sudan and elsewhere.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00022.jpg"/></p><p>The <span>STC</span>’s romp caught everyone by surprise. Yemen-watchers are still trying to identify the trigger. The Saudis want to negotiate a peace with the Houthis; perhaps the <span>STC</span> wanted to pre-empt that. The separatists may also have been responding to an attempted power grab by a tribal leader in Hadhramaut, an eastern province.</p><p>Two weeks before the offensive Muhammad bin Salman, the Saudi crown prince, visited Donald Trump at the White House. He asked the president to push for a peace deal in Sudan, which has been riven by civil war since 2023. The Saudis back the Sudanese army while the <span>UAE</span> has sent arms and money to the Rapid Support Forces (<span>RSF</span>), a militia, though it denies it is still doing so. The crown prince suggested that Mr Trump might need to expand American sanctions against the <span>RSF</span>.</p><p>For Saudi Arabia the timing of all this does not look coincidental. The kingdom believes the <span>UAE</span>’s leaders got the impression that Prince Muhammad asked for sanctions against their country—which the Saudis say is untrue—and subsequently mobilised the <span>STC</span> in Yemen as a riposte. (The <span>UAE</span> did not respond to a request for comment.) Propagandists from the two countries have spent the past few weeks sparring with one another on social media.</p><p>Either way, the secessionists now hold a sizeable swathe of territory. Their fief also includes most of Yemen’s oil and gas resources (the country once exported more than 100,000 barrels of crude a day).</p><p>The anti-Houthi camp has always been fractious. The Saudis were happy to work with the likes of Islah, an offshoot of the Muslim Brotherhood, while the Emiratis, who abhor political Islam, preferred partners such as the <span>STC</span> and Tareq Saleh, a nephew of Yemen’s late longtime dictator. In 2022 the Saudis abruptly sacked the country’s feckless president, and replaced him with an eight-man Presidential Leadership Council (<span>PLC</span>). Its leader, Rashad al-Alimi, is a Saudi-backed politician who favours national unity. His deputy, Aiderus al-Zubaidi, is the head of the <span>STC</span> and prefers to partition the country. How they were meant to work together was unclear.</p><p>Some diplomats fret that the Saudi-backed camp may join the Houthis against the separatists. That seems unlikely: sometimes, the enemy of your enemy is still your enemy. But the <span>STC</span>’s takeover will deepen tensions within the coalition.</p><p>Saudi Arabia shares a long border with Yemen; the Houthis fired hundreds of missiles and drones at its cities throughout the war. The <span>UAE</span> is not immune to Houthi attacks: a 2022 drone strike killed three people in Abu Dhabi, its capital. But it is more distant from the conflict. Whereas the Saudis want a stable Yemen that does not shoot at them, the Emiratis see it as <a href="#filepos188546">part of a broader strategy to exert influence·</a> in the Red Sea and the Horn of Africa. They have worked patiently to build a network of allies across the south.</p><p>The question now is how the <span>STC</span> uses its newfound power. One option is to secede. That would be risky without foreign support—but Mr Zubaidi might be able to play a trump card (pun intended). He has said that an independent South Yemen could join the Abraham accords, the 2020 agreements that saw four Arab states recognise Israel. His advisers have made quiet overtures to Jerusalem in recent days.</p><p>That may give him leverage with an American president keen to expand the accords. In the waning days of his first term Mr Trump recognised Morocco’s sovereignty over occupied Western Sahara, a territorial claim accepted by only a handful of other countries. That was a reward for Morocco’s decision to join the accords. Could he be convinced to do the same by recognising South Yemen?</p><p>But secession would cause a lasting rupture between Saudi Arabia and the <span>UAE</span>. The former wants the Houthis to accept a power-sharing deal that would limit their role in Yemen’s government. Were the south to break away, the rump northern state would probably be too weak to impose one. Saudi Arabia would be left with a worst-case scenario: an impoverished North Yemen dominated by the Houthis but cut off from the country’s oil and gas resources. It would probably be stuck bankrolling the Houthis indefinitely.</p><p>The <span>STC</span> could also try to consolidate its gains without declaring independence. The status quo looks shaky, though. On December 26th a Saudi jet carried out an air strike near an <span>STC</span>-held military camp in Hadhramaut. No one was killed, but the strike was a clear warning. Four days later the Saudis bombed Mukalla, a port in eastern Yemen, after what the kingdom says was an arms delivery to the <span>STC</span> on two ships that came from the <span>UAE</span>. The separatists are vulnerable internally as well. There have been protests of late in <span>STC</span>-controlled cities over unpaid salaries, blackouts and other indignities. The group’s overtures to Israel are unpopular.</p><p>A more optimistic scenario is that Saudi Arabia and the <span>UAE</span> negotiate a deal whereby the south enjoys greater autonomy and the Emiratis have more say over a possible settlement with the Houthis. Officials from both countries are talking daily about a possible <span>STC</span> withdrawal from parts of the south-east. That would head off further conflict. It would also reinforce a long-running trend: after a decade of war, Yemen’s future will be decided not by Yemenis but by their foreign backers. ■</p><p><span>Sign up to the </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/middle-east-dispatch"><span>Middle East Dispatch</span></a><span>, a weekly newsletter that keeps you in the loop on a fascinating, complex and consequential part of the world.</span></p>6:["slug","a-lightning-advance-by-separatists-has-reshaped-yemens-civil-war-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
