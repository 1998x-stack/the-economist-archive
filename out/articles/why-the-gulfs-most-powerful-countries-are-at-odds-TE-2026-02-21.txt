3:T1f88,<p><span><span><span>The Saudis v the Emiratis</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Why the Gulf’s most powerful countries are at odds</span></span></p><p><span><span>A feud between Saudi Arabia and the UAE will have far-reaching consequences</span></span></p><p><span><span>Feb 19, 2026 06:23 AM | DUBAI AND RIYADH</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-21-00013.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE LANGUAGE</span> of diplomats is polite. That is especially true for those who speak Arabic, a tongue inclined towards courtesies and honorifics. In December the Saudi foreign ministry issued a five-paragraph statement that used the term <span>shaqiqa</span> (“brotherly”) four times in reference to the United Arab Emirates (<span>UAE</span>). The fraternal tone was rather at odds with the subject matter: the kingdom had just bombed an Emirati weapons shipment in Yemen and was accusing the <span>UAE</span> of threatening its national security.</p><p>Almost two months later, no one is bothering with such niceties. The Gulf’s biggest powers are mired in a <a href="https://www.economist.com/middle-east-and-africa/2025/12/30/a-lightning-advance-by-separatists-has-reshaped-yemens-civil-war">worsening spat</a>. Top officials barely speak. State-backed propagandists have been unleashed to attack each other. The feud has reshaped the war in Yemen and complicated cross-border business. Diplomats and executives are nervous about what comes next. Some fear an echo of the Qatar crisis in 2017, when Gulf countries (including Saudi Arabia and the <span>UAE</span>) imposed an embargo on the tiny emirate over its support for Islamists. Things are unlikely to deteriorate that far—but even a lesser quarrel may have far-reaching consequences.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-21-00107.jpg"/></p><p>Saudi Arabia and the <span>UAE</span> have been close allies for decades. Both are leading members of the Gulf Co-operation Council (<span>GCC</span>), a club of petro-monarchies, and of the <span>OPEC</span> cartel. They fought together for years in Yemen against the Houthis, an Iran-backed militia that seized much of the country in 2014. The <span>UAE</span> is Saudi Arabia’s fifth-largest export market for goods; Saudi Arabia ranks ninth for the <span>UAE.</span> Bilateral trade is worth $31bn a year. Flights between Dubai and Riyadh are the world’s seventh-busiest international route.</p><p>Like all allies they have had their troubles. Their interests in Yemen began to diverge in 2018; a dispute over oil-production quotas paralysed <span>OPEC</span> for several months in 2021. But their leaders saw their partnership as the bedrock of Gulf security and tried to limit disagreements.</p><p>A turning point came in 2023, when Sudan tipped into civil war. The Saudis backed Sudan’s army. The <span>UAE</span> sent money and arms to the Rapid Support Forces, a militia accused of genocide<span>
</span>(the <span>UAE </span>acknowledges providing some early support but denies it is still doing so). The Saudis saw this as dangerous meddling in a war just 200km across the Red Sea. The Emiratis said the army was dominated by Islamists. Then came the quarrel in Yemen in December, when the Southern Transitional Council (<span>STC</span>), Emirati-backed secessionists, unexpectedly seized a large chunk of territory from Saudi-backed forces. The Saudis forced the <span>STC</span> to retreat and expelled the <span>UAE</span> from Yemen.</p><p>As ever in the Gulf, the feud is a mix of the political and the personal. Saudi Arabia resents the <span>UAE</span>’s support for separatists and rebels in Somalia, Sudan, Yemen and elsewhere, which it sees as destabilising. Emirati officials counter that many of these are states in name only: better to ally with strong separatists than a failed central government. The Emiratis despise political Islam, while the Saudis are willing to tolerate Islamist groups. They also disagree when it comes to Israel, which the <span>UAE</span> recognised in 2020.</p><p>Saudi Arabia has the <span>GCC</span>‘s largest population by far, with 20m citizens to the <span>UAE</span>’s 1m. A <span>G</span>20 economy and the home of Islam’s holiest sites, it sees itself as <span>primus inter pares </span>in the Gulf. Last month a commentator close to the royals described the <span>UAE</span> as a rebellious “younger sibling”. Such talk annoys the Emiratis, whose economy is more diversified and army more capable (if smaller). They no longer want to follow the kingdom on foreign policy.</p><p>Their dispute has turned into a battle of narratives. Commentators in Saudi Arabia accuse the <span>UAE</span> of being a handmaiden for Israeli interests, while their opposite numbers allege that Muhammad bin Salman, the Saudi crown prince, has fallen in thrall to Islamists. A few months ago anyone posting such comments might have earned a summons from the police: in normal times, Gulf monarchs do not tolerate criticism of one another. Today both governments seem to be encouraging the vitriol.</p><p>Diplomats from four Western countries have heard complaints from <span>UAE</span>-based firms who say they are facing new bureaucratic obstacles in the kingdom. Lorries have been held up at the border; employees cannot get Saudi business visas. Meanwhile, Emirati firms pulled out of a big defence expo in Riyadh this month. Bosses are drawing up contingency plans. Few expect a Qatar-style embargo: economic ties are probably too deep. But they still worry.</p><p>Business is not the only concern. Another anxiety is that competition between Saudi Arabia and the <span>UAE</span> will exacerbate other conflicts. That could happen in the Horn of Africa, where Ethiopia (an Emirati ally) and Eritrea (a Saudi partner) seem close to war. The Saudis are also concerned about Syria, where the Emiratis remain sceptical of Ahmed al-Sharaa, the ex-jihadist who took power in 2024 after Bashar al-Assad’s regime collapsed.</p><p><span>Two households, both alike in dignity</span></p><p>Saudi officials say the feud will end if the <span>UAE</span> stops backing regional rebels. It is less clear what the Emiratis want—they did not respond to multiple requests for comment—but they are unlikely to yield to the kingdom’s demands. In an amusing twist, Qatar is now trying to mediate between its former blockaders. On February 4th its emir met with Khalid bin Salman, the Saudi defence minister and the crown prince’s brother. Ten days later he flew to Abu Dhabi to see Muhammad bin Zayed, the <span>UAE</span>’s president. Bahrain, Egypt and Turkey are also trying to help with diplomacy.</p><p>So far, though, these meetings seem to have made little progress. A nudge from America might help. Never doubtful of himself, Donald Trump thinks he could end the crisis: “I can settle it very easily,” he told reporters on February 16th. Yet diplomats in the region say the president is trying not to wade into a dispute between two allies who have enriched his family. The <span>UAE</span> has stepped up its lobbying in Washington, where its alignment with Israel is a source of support. “Knock it off, Saudi Arabia,” said Lindsey Graham, a Republican senator, on February 13th.</p><p>The Saudis, meanwhile, are trying to stir up trouble across the border. In recent days Saudi-linked social-media accounts have spread unsubstantiated rumours about the health of Sheikh Muhammad. Commentators in the kingdom have tried to drive a wedge between Abu Dhabi, the <span>UAE</span>’s capital, and the leaders of its other six emirates—not all of whom agree with Sheikh Muhammad’s hard-edged foreign policy. This latest crisis has been years in the making; it shows no signs of ending. ■</p><p><span>Sign up to the </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/middle-east-dispatch"><span>Middle East Dispatch</span></a><span>, a weekly newsletter that keeps you in the loop on a fascinating, complex and consequential part of the world.</span></p>6:["slug","why-the-gulfs-most-powerful-countries-are-at-odds-TE-2026-02-21","d"]
