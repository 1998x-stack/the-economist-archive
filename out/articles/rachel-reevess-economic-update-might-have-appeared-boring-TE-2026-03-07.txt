The spring snorecast<h1>Rachel Reeves’s economic update might have appeared boring</h1>But it hinted at big problems to come<p>3月 05, 2026 04:15 上午</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00029.jpg" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer, Rachel Reeves."/>
<p><b>W</b><span>hen he entered</span> Downing Street, Sir Keir Starmer pledged to “end the era of noisy performance”. After <a href="https://www.economist.com/britain/2026/02/09/sir-keir-starmer-clings-to-office-but-not-power">a cacophonous first 20 months</a>, his chancellor finally honoured that promise. Rachel Reeves’s economic update on March 3rd was, above all, dull. She walked the Commons through the latest forecasts of the Office for Budget Responsibility (<span>OBR</span>). She boasted that the watchdog’s numbers vindicated her previous choices. But she notably did not announce a single major policy decision that will help Britain break out of its malaise. The real action was in the questions the chancellor did not resolve, and which will define the year ahead.</p>
<p>Ms Reeves’s restraint was mostly welcome. Her tenure has been plagued by feverish conjecture about tax changes. The resulting uncertainty was highly damaging, causing businesses to put off investment decisions and consumers to rein in spending. By keeping her “spring statement” low-key, she has provided some needed domestic stability.</p>
<p>The <span>OBR</span>’s forecasts were as uninspiring as the statement itself. The long-term outlook for inflation and real <span>GDP </span>barely budged from last November. The bigger shifts were short-term, though still muted. The <span>OBR </span>downgraded its growth forecast for 2026 from 1.4% to 1.1%, and projected that unemployment would peak at 5.3% in 2026, up from 5% in its previous forecast. The flipside of this weakness was lower inflation and interest rates, leaving the government’s debt position slightly improved.</p>
<p>Unfortunately for Ms Reeves, this picture of relative stability is unlikely to last. Her speech pointed to three areas that are likely to cause trouble. The most obvious is the war in and around Iran, which has led to yields on ten-year British gilts spiking by 0.3 percentage points on March 2nd-3rd, reflecting rising oil and gas prices. If the conflict drags on, and energy costs remain high, this could produce inflation and a hole in Ms Reeves’s budget.</p>
<p>Before the war with Iran began, the Treasury and the Ministry of Defence (<span>MOD</span>) were already locked in a battle over defence spending. Securocrats want to bring forward Labour’s promise to raise defence spending to 3% of <span>GDP </span>from the next parliament to the current one. The Institute for Fiscal Studies, a think-tank, calculates that this would cost an extra £14bn ($19bn) a year by the end of the decade. Ms Reeves has been pushing back on cost grounds, but recent events will have strengthened the <span>MOD</span>’s hand.</p>
<p>The second worry is migration. The <span>OBR </span>had expected 290,000 net arrivals to Britain in 2025; the actual figure was 204,000. If migration continues to fall, the British population will be much smaller by 2030 than previously expected. The spring forecast made some adjustments, but the <span>OBR </span>flagged that a fuller reckoning—taking in new projections and tighter immigration policy—awaits in the autumn. Fewer people means fewer taxpayers, which would cause problems for Ms Reeves.</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00030.png" title=""/>
<p>Perhaps the most worrying problem for the government, though, is related to jobs and wages. Unemployment among 16- to 24-year-olds rose above 16% in the last quarter of 2025, and is now higher than the <span>EU </span>average. Higher minimum wages for young people, combined with a more onerous tax load, have made companies more reluctant to give them a chance. Ms Reeves promised in her statement that she will set out further measures to tackle youth unemployment in the weeks ahead.</p>
<p>Wages could become an even greater concern. Ms Reeves boasted that “real wages have risen by more in the first 18 months of this Labour government than in the first ten years of the Tory government.” Real wages have grown by over 3% since Labour took office. However, many people do not feel richer, partly because much of this growth simply undid the real-wage falls that occurred during the high inflation of 2022-23. And voters have already received around two-thirds of the wage bump they are expected to get over the course of this parliament. Pay growth in the coming years is forecast to be anaemic, at only 0.5% a year (see chart).</p>
<p>In her speech Ms Reeves set out a simple test for voters to consider at the next election: “Are me and my family better off?” Once these worries play out, the public’s answer might very well be “No”. ■</p>
