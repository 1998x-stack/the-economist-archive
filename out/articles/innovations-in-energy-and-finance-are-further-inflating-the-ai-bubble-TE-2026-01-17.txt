3:T1bf6,<p><span><span><span>Schumpeter</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Innovations in energy and finance are further inflating the AI bubble</span></span></p><p><span><span>How to bust bottlenecks</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 16, 2026 09:32 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="https://www.economist.com/cdn-cgi/image/width=600,quality=80,format=auto/content-assets/images/20260117_WBD000.jpg"/></p><p><span>G</span><span>REED HAS</span> long been a catalyst for capitalist innovation. In the California gold rush prospectors developed water cannons to blast hills away in search of the ore. In the American mortgage boom of the early 2000s, banks built madcap loan-securitisation machines to keep the credit bender rolling. For a while, both were seen as ingenious forms of engineering—one to harness extra energy, the other to slather the housing market with cheap loans. Today energy and credit are two of the biggest constraints on the artificial-intelligence (<span>AI</span>) bonanza. Once again, the creative juices are flowing.</p><p>Energy is the most tangible bottleneck: gridlock, literally. Power suppliers are overwhelmed by demand for electricity to run <span>AI</span> chips in vast data centres. <span>ERCOT</span>, Texas’s grid operator, has received requests for more than 226 gigawatts (<span>GW</span>) of power by 2030, nearly 100 times more than it approved in 2022. President Donald Trump has joined a chorus of Americans worried that the insatiable power demand from <span>AI </span>projects will drive up electricity prices. On January 13th he promised citizens they would not “pick up the tab”. Tech giants such as Microsoft would instead.</p><p>Capital has emerged as another bottleneck. When cash-generating tech giants paid for their <span>AI</span> capital-spending binge out of their own pockets, it was not a concern. But as investment has eaten up a rising share of their cashflow, they have had to find new ways to raise money. Meanwhile, banks are up to the gills in lending to smaller, less creditworthy data-centre developers, and need to slice and dice loans to get them off their books.</p><p>In both energy and credit markets, the result is a plethora of innovations aimed at reducing strains on the grid, as well as on balance-sheets. The latter sounds more alarming than the former. Both, though, will have the dubious distinction of pumping more air into the <span>AI</span> bubble.</p><p>As usual when it comes to thinking outside the box, Elon Musk is the one to watch. For a few years before x<span>AI</span>, his model-maker, joined the <span>AI</span> race, tech giants hooked their data centres directly to America’s electricity grid. But the more demand there was, the longer it took to secure a connection—and Mr Musk was in a hurry to catch up with rivals such as Open<span>AI</span>. So he pioneered what SemiAnalysis, a research firm, christened the “<span>BYO</span>” (bring-your-own) alternative to grid-based energy. When x<span>AI</span> built a big cluster of graphics processing units (<span>GPU</span>s) in a record four months in Tennessee in 2024, it literally trucked in gas turbines and engines. Initially, this was a stopgap measure. But with grid connections now taking as long as five years to secure, <span>BYO</span>s are here to stay.</p><p>As demand surges, <span>BYO</span> creativity has gone supersonic. Last month Boom, which is building ultra-fast planes, announced that it would supply 29 natural-gas turbines based on the same technology as its jet engines to Crusoe, a data-centre developer. Wärtsilä, a Finnish firm that makes engines for cruise ships, also sells them for data centres. Other promising new technologies such as fuel cells may be harnessed, too. All told, Goldman Sachs reckons that up to a third (or 25<span>GW</span>) of incremental data-centre capacity will be built off-grid in America over the next five years. That will mean data centres can spring up more quickly.</p><p>Mr Musk is also leading the charge when it comes to financial wizardry, alongside Meta’s Mark Zuckerberg and Oracle’s Larry Ellison. Adjacent to a $20bn fundraising that x<span>AI </span>completed in early January, it will lease $5.4bn-worth of Nvidia <span>GPU</span>s from a special-purpose vehicle (<span>SPV</span>) set up by Valor Equity Partners, its long-standing backer. Both Meta and Oracle have also used <span>SPV</span>s to reduce the strain that data-centre projects are placing on their balance-sheets. Meta whipped up an extraordinary concoction of private capital, corporate bonds and debt guarantees to raise $30bn for a mammoth data centre in Louisiana called Hyperion. Fittingly, the <span>SPV</span> for the project is named after a sugar-coated New Orleans pastry called a beignet. Oracle has also reportedly raised $66bn in off-balance-sheet financing through <span>SPV</span>s, all to support Open<span>AI</span>, which has so far proved to be <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/12/29/openai-faces-a-make-or-break-year-in-2026">far better at making deals than making money</a>.</p><p>The size of such deals—as well as their concentration among a small group of borrowers—is giving the heavily regulated banking industry indigestion. It is happy to arrange bond issues for highly profitable hyperscalers. But the less creditworthy the counterparty, the trickier it is to hold loans on a bank’s books for long. This is providing opportunities for private-credit firms, often funded by life insurers, who are either originating loans to data-centre borrowers or buying bespoke tranches of the banks’ <span>AI</span>-loan portfolios. The market is potentially huge. Morgan Stanley reckons that data-centre financing involving private-credit firms will reach $800bn in the five years to 2030—or about half the total amount it expects to be borrowed in the data-centre boom. Yet many of the financiers involved are making it up as they go along.</p><p><span>Frankenstein finance</span></p><p>Salute innovation, but be mindful of the risks. They are not of the same magnitude. In the energy markets <span>BYO </span>power brings higher costs and exposes data centres to greater risk of equipment failure than they would otherwise have on the grid. But the spillovers from the frenzy of innovation will also be positive in so far as they lead to new approaches to supplying energy.</p><p>As for the credit markets, they are a good place to raise money now that <span>AI</span> investment can no longer be entirely self-funded. But the spillovers may also be dangerous. Only a few firms are making reliable profits from <span>AI</span>. If that does not change, there is a risk of a credit meltdown that could rock the financial system and the economy more broadly. Then the world will need a new type of ingenuity—at picking up the pieces. ■</p><p><span>Subscribers to The Economist can sign up to our </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/opinion"><span>Opinion newsletter</span></a><span>, which brings together the best of our leaders, columns, guest essays and reader correspondence.</span></p>6:["slug","innovations-in-energy-and-finance-are-further-inflating-the-ai-bubble-TE-2026-01-17","d"]
