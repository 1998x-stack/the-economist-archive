3:T2349,<p><span><span><span>Back in the Blue House</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>An interview with South Korea’s president</span></span></p><p><span><span>Lee Jae Myung has put his country on track again, but challenges loom</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jun 11, 2026 05:50 AM | SEOUL</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-06-13-00029.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE TILED</span> roof of the Cheong Wa Dae complex in central Seoul gives the building its colloquial name: the Blue House. Generations of political leaders in South Korea called it home until Yoon Suk Yeol, then the president, decided to move out in 2022, giving a hint of the turbulence to come under his rule. Following Mr Yoon’s <a href="https://www.economist.com/by-invitation/2024/12/11/south-koreas-crisis-highlights-both-fragility-and-resilience-writes-wi-sung-lac">declaration of martial law in 2024 and his subsequent impeachment</a> last year, South Korea’s new president, Lee Jae Myung, moved back in, signalling a restoration of normality. In an interview with <span>The Economist</span> in one of the Blue House’s opulent reception halls, Mr Lee projects calm. His country can “move beyond this normalisation of the abnormal” he declares, plausibly. He is less convincing in suggesting it could “develop into a nation that leads the world”.</p><p>Mr Lee has reason to be confident. A year after taking office, his approval rating remains around 60%, among the highest for a South Korean president at this stage. Voters give him credit for the political stability. He is fortunate for being in office during a scorching stock market rally. Mr Lee has also managed to deftly steer South Korea’s relations with notable powers, including America, China and Japan. Yet, as Mr Lee looks ahead to the rest of his five-year term, challenges loom.</p><p>After Mr Yoon’s declaration of martial law in late 2024, the country cycled through three acting presidents. Mr Lee, as opposition leader, galvanised resistance to the move. In office his lively social-media presence and habit of live-streaming cabinet meetings appeal to voters. It has not hurt that the conservative opposition People Power Party (<span>PPP</span>) <a href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2026/02/18/south-korea-is-still-haunted-by-its-disgraced-ex-president">remains in thrall to the disgraced ex-president</a>. In local elections on June 3rd, candidates from Mr Lee’s Democratic Party (<span>DP</span>) took 12 of 16 big mayoral and gubernatorial jobs (though the conservative incumbent in Seoul held on).</p><p>Somewhat unexpectedly for a left-winger with a background as a labour lawyer, Mr Lee campaigned on a promise to boost capital markets. He called for reforms to corporate governance to help raise the country’s main equity index, the <span>KOSPI</span>, from under 3,000 to 5,000 within his five-year term. Mr Lee was lucky to take office amid <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/03/17/will-south-koreas-epic-bull-market-survive-the-energy-shock">an <span>AI</span> gold rush in which South Korean tech giants, such as Samsung and SK Hynix, profit by making memory chips</a>. The <span>KOSPI</span> has nearly tripled over the past year, reaching record highs of over 8,000.</p><p>Mr Lee has lived up to his<a href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2025/01/30/who-is-lee-jae-myung-south-koreas-possible-next-president"> promise of pragmatism in foreign policy</a> as well. Leaders from the <span>DP</span> generally favour engagement with North Korea and warm to China to make that easier to do; they also typically have tetchy relations with Japan, the former colonial overlord. Mr Lee has stabilised relations with China without seeming obsequious. Most consequentially, he has forged a bond with Japan’s right-wing prime minister, Takaichi Sanae, born of a shared understanding that the neighbours must get along in the face of a more assertive China and a less reliable America.</p><p><span>Dealing with the Donald</span></p><p>Relations with Donald Trump could have been awful. Mr Trump has often railed against South Korea as an ungrateful ally. But Mr Lee’s government has pledged to raise defence spending to 3.5% of <span>GDP</span> (from 2.7% last year); it also wants to take greater responsibility within the alliance’s command structure. “When it comes to the defence of our nation, we must take matters into our own hands,” Mr Lee says. Pete Hegseth, America’s secretary of war, now calls South Korea a “model ally”.</p><p>Mr Lee entered office amid negotiations over Mr Trump’s “liberation day” tariffs. South Korea struck a deal for tariff relief in exchange for a $350bn investment pledge, with caps on annual outflows to limit the damage to the local economy. Mr Lee also slid several long-standing security goals into the package, gaining the American president’s blessing for South Korea to possess nuclear-powered submarines and to develop the capacity to enrich and reprocess nuclear fuel. He insists it will be used only to enrich to the low levels needed to power atomic reactors, and that it is “not desirable nor realistic” for South Korea to get its own nuclear weapons. Nonetheless, having enrichment facilities will bring it a big step closer to <a href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2024/08/15/what-if-south-korea-got-a-nuclear-bomb">being able to build the bomb</a>, if it ever decides to.</p><p>Implementing the deals reached with the mercurial American president is the first big challenge looming over the rest of Mr Lee’s term. Last week American officials visited South Korea to continue talks over nuclear issues. Big gaps still need to be bridged, not least over the question of where the subs will be built. (Mr Trump wants them made in America.) Congress would have to agree to giving South Korea nuclear-enrichment rights.</p><p>South Korea’s position would be a good deal less precarious were it not for the nuclear-armed neighbour to its north. Mr Lee, in keeping with <span>DP</span> tradition, has made every effort to reach out. But North Korea, which in recent years has taken to <a href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2024/10/30/north-koreas-fanatical-regime-just-got-scarier">calling South Korea a “hostile state”</a> and is now enjoying the fruits of a new alliance with Russia, has shown no interest in talking to the South. Mr Trump’s “unique personality” can be “very helpful” in the current situation, he says. Kim Jong Un, North Korea’s dictator, says <a href="https://www.economist.com/leaders/2025/09/25/the-deadly-allure-of-a-bad-deal-with-north-korea">he will meet Mr Trump only if the long-held goal of “denuclearisation” is off the table </a>(see China section). Following the war in Iran, North Korea will be even less inclined to give up its arsenal, Mr Lee notes.</p><p>More problems lie ahead at home. While <span>AI</span>-related investment has thus far lifted South Korean equities and Mr Lee’s political fortunes, his standing would suffer if the market dips. Even if the boom continues, it will raise <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/05/27/japan-south-korea-and-taiwan-are-suffering-industrial-rot">hard questions about how to share newfound wealth equitably</a>. Mr Lee says that the rise of industries generating extraordinary profits may require a rethink of tax and distribution systems, and that using a portion of those excess profits to support consumers’ purchasing power through a basic income could be a useful option. He has also called for more balanced growth among different regions, pushing chipmakers to build supply chains in less developed corners of the country. As part of that agenda, he wants to move part of the presidential administration out of the Blue House and to an administrative centre south of Seoul.</p><p>Mr Lee’s own future is also uncertain. He entered office with five trials hanging over his head, related to his earlier spells as a mayor and governor. Mr Lee calls these prosecutions politically motivated. They have been put on hold while he is in office, but seem sure to re-emerge once he leaves. Since the country’s democratisation in the late 1980s, more than half of South Korea’s presidents have been impeached, jailed or both. Mr Lee acknowledges that the possibility of something similar happening to him is “pretty high”. His legacy will thus in part depend on whether he can break the curse of the Blue House. ■</p><p><span><span>Clarification (June 10th 2026)</span></span><span>: Due to an inaccuracy in translation, an earlier version of this article misstated Mr Lee’s position with regard to the possible use of excess profits.</span></p><p><span>For exclusive coverage of Asian politics, economics and security, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/asia-bulletin"><span>Asia Bulletin</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","an-interview-with-south-koreas-president-TE-2026-06-13","d"]
