3:T15b6,<p><span><span><span>The Economist reads</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Eight of the best new novels to read this summer</span></span></p><p><span><span>Many of our selections explore family secrets</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jun 25, 2026 04:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-06-27-00009.jpg"/></p><p><span>Some like it hot</span></p><p><span>“T</span><span>HESE ARE </span>all my politics: I enjoy music and painting; a good book is an event in my life.” A character in “The Red and the Black” by Stendhal, a French writer, proclaimed those words in 1830, but plenty of people today will recognise the sentiment. If you are weary of politics and seek an escape in art, pick up one of our recommended novels. Perhaps it will even become an event in your life.</p><p><span>The Calamity Club.</span><span><span>
</span></span><span>By Kathryn Stockett. Spiegel &amp; Grau; 656 pages; $35. Fig Tree; £20</span></p><p>A rollicking work of historical fiction set in Oxford, Mississippi, in 1933. Meg endures misery in an orphanage. Birdie comes to town to borrow money from her married sister. Inevitably the characters’ paths cross; soon they find themselves joining forces with other downtrodden women intent on improving their lives. This novel has intricately woven storylines and a vibrant cast.</p><p><span>The Kindness of Strangers.</span><span> By Emma Garman. Summit Books; 304 pages; $28. Virago; 336 pages; £20</span></p><p>Set in London in 1953, this accomplished debut revolves around a ragtag assortment of residents of a Victorian boarding house. When a young man shows up and threatens the stability of the household, it becomes clear all the occupants have something to hide. A skilfully constructed mystery full of sly twists, dark wit and rich period detail.</p><p><span>Land. </span><span>By Maggie O’Farrell. Knopf; 400 pages; $32. Tinder Press; 448 pages; £25</span></p><p>In 1865 Tomás, a cartographer, and his son, Liam, travel to a remote western peninsula of Ireland to map their country for their British masters. After discovering a mysterious, mystical site, Tomás vows to carry out an act of “honour and resistance”. It is a decision that will transform his life and that of his family. A bold, epic novel about the relationship between people and place.</p><p><span>Other People’s Children. </span><span>By Ben Faccini. Granta; 336 pages; £16.99</span></p><p>Tom has a lot to contend with. He is plagued by memories of his late father. He struggles to bond with his partner’s young sons. Adding to his woes is the increasingly strange behaviour of his Italian grandmother, Alma. When he delves into her wartime past, he unearths a devastating secret that alters his outlook. This book is elegantly written and astute as it traces family ties and the <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/09/15/italians-memories-of-fascism-are-dangerously-inaccurate">long shadow of history</a>.</p><p><span>The Things We Never Say. </span><span>By Elizabeth Strout.</span>
<span>Random House; 224 pages; $29.00 Viking; 208 pages; £18.99</span></p><p>Artie Dam is a happily married man and a popular history teacher. But deep down he feels trapped in a “state of dismalness” and is contemplating suicide. A shocking revelation, however, makes him appreciate all he holds dear. This bittersweet novel about the effects of trauma, anxiety and <a href="https://www.economist.com/podcasts/2026/01/14/lone-dangers-the-physical-toll-of-social-isolation">loneliness</a> examines how well people really know each other.</p><p><span>Transcription. </span><span>By Ben Lerner. Farrar, Straus and Giroux; 144 pages; $25. Granta; £14.99</span></p><p>In this short, clever novel, an unnamed narrator conducts a magazine interview with his 90-year-old college mentor—but fails to record it. It is the first of several exchanges with different individuals, all of which offer shrewd insight into art and technology, friendship and <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/05/15/why-the-best-time-to-be-a-dad-is-now">fatherhood</a>, and the reliability of memory.</p><p><span>Uprising. </span><span>By Tahmima Anam. Canongate; 208 pages; £16.99. To be published in America by Scribner in November; 192 pages; $27</span></p><p>Kusum Khan is tricked and sold into slavery on an island. In this prison, children look on while their mothers engage in <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/03/19/the-new-economics-of-sex-work">sex work</a> to pay off crippling debts to the cruel madam. But Kusum finds a way to unite her fellow prisoners into resisting and revolting. A fierce and unflinching novel inspired by the real women of Banishanta, Bangladesh.</p><p><span>Whistler.</span>
<span>By Ann Patchett. Harper; 304 pages; $30. Bloomsbury; £20</span></p><p>Daphne hasn’t seen her stepfather, Eddie, since they were involved in a near-fatal car accident 44 years ago. A chance meeting in New York allows the pair to reconnect. It also gives Daphne the chance to finally learn the truth about why Eddie’s marriage to her mother fell apart. A tale of secrets and lasting love, enriched by sharply drawn characters. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","eight-of-the-best-new-novels-to-read-this-summer-TE-2026-06-27","d"]
