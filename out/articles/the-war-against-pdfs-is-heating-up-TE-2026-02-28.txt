Attachment issues<h1>The war against PDFs is heating up</h1>Will the file type survive the AI revolution?<p>2月 26, 2026 04:27 上午</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-28-00044.jpg" title="A mouse cursor about to delete a PDF icon"/>
<p><b>W</b><span>hen Adobe</span> introduced the portable document format (<span>PDF</span>) in 1993, a consultant from Gartner called it “the dumbest idea I’ve ever heard in my life”. Users would have to twiddle their thumbs waiting for the megabyte-sized files to download over their dial-up internet, then wait again for their <span>PC</span>s to render them. The software-maker’s board wanted to kill the project. But as sharing digital files became essential, the <span>PDF</span> triumphed—particularly after the Internal Revenue Service, America’s tax authority, started using it for its forms. Today more than 2.5trn <span>PDF</span>s float in the ether. But will the format survive the <span>ai </span>revolution?</p>
<p><span>PDF</span>s still have drawbacks. They are a pain to view on a smartphone. Copying data from them is fiddly. Software tools that read screens for blind people struggle with <span>PDF</span>s. The file type, which Adobe relinquished control over in 2008, is also a vehicle for malware: a fifth of email-based cyber-attacks utilise <span>PDF</span> attachments, according to Check Point, a cyber-security firm.</p>
<p>Lately another source of criticism has emerged. The large language models underpinning generative <span>AI </span>are often bamboozled by <span>PDF</span>s, reading a page set in columns from left to right rather than top to bottom, say, or getting confused by headers and footers. Trouble parsing <span>PDF</span>s is one of the reasons <span>AI</span> chatbots occasionally “hallucinate”, generating nonsense.</p>
<p>Enter the disrupters. Startups such as Factify are on a mission to build a new file type that is better suited to the technology. Matan Gavish, its boss, talks of his “megalomaniac” vision of displacing the <span>PDF</span>.</p>
<p>Yet Duff Johnson, head of the <span>PDF</span> Association, protector of the format, argues that the fault lies not in the file type but in ourselves. He contends that there is no reason developers cannot build bots that are able to use <span>PDF</span>s. The <span>AI</span> assistant embedded in Acrobat, Adobe’s<span> PDF</span> reader, is designed to do precisely that, notes Leonard Rosenthol, the software firm’s <span>PDF</span> guru. Google, a leader in <span>AI</span>, has rolled out a tool for developers using its Gemini models that makes it easier to ingest <span>PDF</span>s. The format’s reign is not over yet.■</p>
