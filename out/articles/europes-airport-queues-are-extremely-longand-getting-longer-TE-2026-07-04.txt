3:T102c,<p><span><span><span>EESy come, EESy go?</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Europe’s airport queues are extremely long—and getting longer</span></span></p><p><span><span>The continent’s border-control system is out of control</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jul 02, 2026 06:23 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-07-04-00045.jpg"/></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE EUROPEAN UNION</span> frets a lot about whether it is competitive, but in one sector it leads the world: acronyms that infuriate foreigners. In recent months the <span>EU</span>’s Entry-Exit Scheme (<span>EES</span>) has caused conniptions at the continent’s airports. Exasperated visitors (mostly Americans and Britons, for whom travel to Europe is supposed to be a visa-free breeze) have posted videos on Instagram and TikTok of spaghetti-like queues at passport control.</p><p>The <span>EES </span>began its rollout last October, three years behind schedule. It was supposed to be fully implemented by April. The system is meant to make it easier to track non-<span>EU</span> visitors to the Schengen visa-free travel area, replacing passport stamps with a smooth <span>IT </span>system. Travellers must register by getting their passport, face and fingerprints scanned at the border. Border guards are supposed to use these data to verify their identity on each subsequent trip.</p><p>European officials reckon the scheme is working. In May the European Commission said it had stopped some 30,000 people from improperly entering Schengen, including nearly 7,000 who had stayed longer than allowed on previous visits.</p><p>But many disagree. “<span>EES </span>is what keeps me and many other airport <span>CEO</span>s across Europe awake at night,” said Stefan Schulte of <span>ACI </span>Europe, which represents airports, on June 23rd. Some complain that self-service kiosks designed to speed up registration are ineffective. Amid heatwaves, sweat-stained fingerprint scanners have stopped working. Others grumble that the software is unreliable. Lots of travellers say they had to re-register on several trips because of tech screw-ups.</p><p>Veovo, an airport consulting firm, reckons processing times for non-<span>EU</span> travellers have more than doubled at most airports since April. An <span>ACI </span>survey found that in the worst-affected ones, queues at peak times reached up to 3.5 hours in May. Indeed, WizzAir, a Hungarian budget airline, has warned travellers to arrive at airports three hours early. Some airports are coping reasonably well, but tales of woe abound. In April more than 100 people missed an Easyjet flight from Milan to Manchester after getting stuck at border control. Small airports with lots of non-<span>EU</span> travellers are worst affected. Bottlenecks have been reported at Alicante and Lanzarote, two Spanish fleshpots popular with Britons.</p><p>Big airports are worried, too. On June 25th the boss of Rome’s airports told the <span>Financial Times</span> that the Italian capital would have to stop collecting facial and fingerprint scans during the peak season. Some countries with large tourist industries, such as Greece and Portugal, have partly suspended the scheme. The <span>EU </span>has permitted this for the summer. But after the season ends, the derogation will lapse. Travel-industry groups have urged the bloc to delay full implementation.</p><p>Holiday-makers stuck in snarling queues should brace for more changes, and more acronyms. Later this year the <span>EU </span>wants to implement a travel-authorisation scheme called the European Travel Information and Authorisation System (<span>ETIAS</span>), modelled on America’s Electronic System for Travel Authorisation (<span>ESTA</span>). Visitors will have to register online and pay €20 ($23) for an <span>ETIAS </span>pass, which will be valid for three years.
