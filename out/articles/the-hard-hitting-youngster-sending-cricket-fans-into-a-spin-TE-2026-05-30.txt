3:T15ad,<p><span><span><span>Sporting prodigies</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The hard-hitting youngster sending cricket fans into a spin</span></span></p><p><span><span>Could Vaibhav Sooryavanshi become one of the greatest batters of all time?</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 28, 2026 04:55 AM | DELHI</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-05-30-00076.jpg"/></p><p><span>W</span><span>HEN VAIBHAV SOORYAVANSHI </span>of the Rajasthan Royals lined up to bat against Mitchell Starc of the Delhi Capitals, it was a contest of innocence and experience. Mr Starc is 36, a three-time World Cup winner and regular star in the<a href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2023/03/31/the-indian-premier-league-is-taking-over-global-cricket"> Indian Premier League</a> (<span>IPL</span>), the sport’s most lucrative franchise-based tournament. Mr Sooryavanshi is 15 and has only played one full season in the <span>IPL</span>. Yet it was the youngster who put in a masterful performance on May 17th, smashing the Australian bowler around the stadium. Though Mr Starc’s team went on to win the game, the onslaught of runs was humbling.</p><p>For cricket fans, Mr Sooryavanshi’s talent is a source of unalloyed joy. He is lighting up the <span>IPL.</span> This year he has propelled the Royals into the play-offs and a shot at the final on May 31st. He exploded onto the cricket scene last season when, as a 14-year-old, he became the youngest-ever professional player to score a century (100 runs). The achievement prompted disbelief and even scepticism about Mr Sooryavanshi’s age. (His father has rejected such claims, citing bone-density tests.)</p><p>Mr Sooryavanshi has continued to rack up runs at an astounding pace. He scores at a rate of 2.4 runs a ball, far higher than any of his peers. An analysis by <span>ESPN</span>cricinfo, a website, found that he scores more off fast, vicious deliveries than anyone else in the league. He recently broke the record for the number of sixes scored in a single <span>IPL </span>season. So far he has cleared the boundary 65 times—six more than the previous record, which stood for 14 years. (He has also matched the record for the fastest half-century in <span>IPL </span>play-off<span>
</span>history.) It is hard to think of an athlete in any sport who has been so dominant at such a young age.</p><p>Mr Sooryavanshi’s success rests on technique, physique and temperament. He stands up to bowlers with an extravagant backlift, which gives him more room to swing his bat in a long, violent arc. He is burly, which gives him power when striking the ball. Coaches and analysts credit his success to a less tangible quality: an unusual blend of maturity and audacity.</p><p>He is also a product of bigger forces. The <span>IPL</span>’s launch in 2008 revolutionised the sport, helping elevate the <a href="https://www.economist.com/the-economist-explains/2016/03/13/why-twenty20-cricket-is-so-successful">Twenty20 (<span>T20</span>)</a> format it uses into cricket’s most popular. Unlike<a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/07/31/can-test-cricket-survive-a-sticky-wicket"> traditional Test cricket</a>, played over five days, <span>T20</span> matches are short and prize power-hitting over patience. Mr Sooryavanshi embodies that philosophy. He has played only a handful of red-ball, or multi-day, matches; one of his early coaches says he has never been trained in defence. In this <span>IPL</span> he has featured as an “impact player”, a rule that allows him to bat without having to field as well.</p><p>He reflects a deeper shift in Indian cricket, too. The sport has long thrived in the country’s wealthier areas, such as Mumbai and Kolkata, but the <span>IPL</span> has helped embed the game in India’s hinterlands. Coaching academies have been set up and are sustained by parents desperate to raise the next <span>IPL</span> star. Mr Sooryavanshi grew up in a small town in Bihar, India’s poorest state. His father sold his farmland to fund daily training in Patna, the state capital, a three-hour drive away.</p><p>Mr Sooryavanshi will need to prove himself on the global stage. In February he powered India to victory in the under-19 World Cup. It seems inevitable he will be selected for the senior team, maybe even this summer. But in many ways he is already a national hero. As he was dismantling Mr Starc, the Delhi crowd lapped it up, screaming at every boundary even though it was their own side that was conceding runs. Shirts bearing his name are among the most popular at <span>IPL</span> grounds; clips of his batting trend on Indian social media.</p><p>All prodigies are compared to other players, and so it is with Mr Sooryavanshi. In 1989 <a href="https://www.economist.com/asia/2013/10/19/the-meaning-of-sachin">Sachin Tendulkar</a> made his debut as a 16-year-old. Indians consider him the greatest player ever, who helped their country emerge as a force in global cricket. Now that India is the sport’s established superpower, Mr Sooryavanshi can never match that legacy. But observers reckon he is a once-in-a-generation talent who might become a megastar. He is a virtuoso, right off the bat. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","the-hard-hitting-youngster-sending-cricket-fans-into-a-spin-TE-2026-05-30","d"]
