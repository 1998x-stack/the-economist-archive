3:T115a,<p><span><span><span>An American ally is abandoned</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>How the Kurds lost control of north-eastern Syria</span></span></p><p><span><span>Once America’s ally, a Kurdish-led militia is no longer considered useful</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 23, 2026 03:02 AM | DEIR EZ-ZOR AND SHADADI</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-24-00052.jpg"/></p><p><span>S</span><span>TATUES OF THE</span> Kurds’ proud female fighters were pulled down by the banks of the Euphrates. Their American-supplied Humvees lay abandoned by the sides of roads. In a matter of days, the Syrian Democratic Forces (<span>SDF</span>), a Kurdish-led militia that was America’s partner in the decade-long campaign against Islamic State (<span>IS</span>), collapsed—and with it the Kurdish experiment in autonomy in Syria.</p><p>The collapse began after year-long negotiations between the <span>SDF</span> and Damascus floundered. Fighting had broken out on January 6th between government and <span>SDF</span> troops in a Kurdish part of Aleppo, Syria’s second city, prompting the government to send more troops. Barely a week later, the <span>SDF</span> withdrew from Aleppo. Syria’s tribes, sensing it was time to split from the Kurds and ally with Syria’s government, mobilised. That spelled the end for the <span>SDF</span>.</p><p>The group’s military campaign against <span>IS</span> was internationally lauded, but its ensuing political project became unsustainable as it tried to govern Arab-majority parts of Syria. Cities in the north-east such as Raqqa, freed from <span>IS</span> by the Kurds but home to large Sunni tribal populations, were restive under <span>SDF</span> rule. In recent years the <span>SDF </span>had grown more autocratic. Celebrating Ahmed al-Sharaa, Syria’s new president, and waving the Syrian revolutionary flag became grounds for arrest. “This is our liberation,” said an Arab lawyer in Raqqa.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-24-00121.jpg"/></p><p>By the time government forces reached Raqqa and northern Deir ez-Zor, much of the region had fallen to tribal fighters. Thousands of Arab recruits in the <span>SDF</span> defected. There was little fighting. At the same time Mr Sharaa issued a presidential decree recognising Kurdish cultural rights, eroding the <span>SDF</span>’s negotiating position.</p><p>The options for the <span>SDF</span> are bleak. Its units are to be dismantled and its fighters integrated into the Syrian army; there will be no Kurdish-majority units. The autonomous administration built during the war against <span>IS</span> will be incorporated into the new state. Weeks ago, the group was offered a better deal: three army divisions and a senior defence post for its leader, Mazloum Abdi.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-24-00041.jpg"/></p><p>America no longer seems to regard the <span>SDF </span>as useful to its strategy in the region. Its envoy to Syria said that the group’s original mission had “largely expired” and that the Kurds’ best hope now lies with the government of Mr Sharaa. Internal divisions have weakened the group further. </p><p>On January 20th the government announced a four-day truce to give the <span>SDF</span> time to hand over control of its institutions. Government troops gathered near Hasakeh, a city with a big Kurdish population, said they expected to advance once the truce expired. A Syrian government official told <span>The Economist</span> that unless Kurdish forces surrendered by then, they would be attacked. But seizing Kurdish towns, which are still heavily fortified, would be bloody. Syria’s Kurds also fear sectarian violence. Government fighters described Qamishli as a city of wealthy Kurds that would be a good place to steal cars.</p><p>Another danger remains. For years the <span>SDF</span> has guarded tens of thousands of <span>IS</span> detainees, including women and children, in north-eastern Syria. Amid the chaos, more than 100 have escaped prison. The <span>SDF</span>’s collapse<span>
