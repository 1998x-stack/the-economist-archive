3:T1dcb,<p><span><span><span>Artificially Inflated</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The flaws in India’s AI plans</span></span></p><p><span><span>Despite hosting a global summit, it is a bystander in the race to develop frontier models  </span></span></p><p><span><span>Feb 20, 2026 02:58 AM | DELHI</span></span></p><p><span>S</span><span>MALL SLIGHTS</span> matter when you want to be a big power. Take a recent exchange on a panel at Davos, an elite salon, in which the boss of the <span>IMF</span> appeared to dismiss India as a second-rate <span>AI</span> power. A few seats along Ashwini Vaishnaw, the country’s <span>IT</span> minister, bristled. Then he launched a salvo citing Indian prowess, from chips to apps. In patriotic, tech-mad India, the clip went viral. </p><p>There was lots of such talk this week, as world leaders and <span>AI</span> barons gathered in Delhi. India is the first developing country to host the annual summit, following Britain, South Korea and France. What began as an earnest discussion of risks has morphed into something like a giant trade show; “safety” has been replaced with “impact” in its title. The backdrop this time is buzz about the new models released by Open<span>AI</span> and Anthropic, two American companies. Even so, India is using the event to hype itself as an emerging <span>AI</span> superpower, alongside America and China.</p><p>In many ways that is fanciful. India has little aptitude in advanced manufacturing and cannot produce the chips on which <span>AI</span> depends. Although a government-backed semiconductor mission has made some progress, success in the coming years would at the most mean India being able to produce some lower-grade chips, while continuing to import whizzier ones. On compute it lags, too, though that is starting to change thanks to a <a href="index_split_070.html#filepos424848">data-centre boom·</a>. Like the rest of the world, it is a bystander in the two-horse race to develop frontier models.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-21-00061.jpg"/></p><p>Mr Vaishnaw cited a Stanford study that puts India third in the imaginative category of “<span>AI</span> vibrancy”—behind America and China but ahead of the likes of Britain and Japan. That ranking gives weight to India’s large tech workforce, growing number of startups and digital infrastructure. Yet other measures do not even put it in the top ten. In private, insiders admit that India’s <span>AI</span> ecosystem remains small. A partner at a venture-capital firm says the country has lots of engineers but fewer than 300 skilled <span>AI</span> researchers, far fewer than Britain or France. The government spends a paltry 0.7% of <span>GDP</span> on research and development. Risk capital remains scarce.</p><p>The summit aimed to show off India’s abilities as a global convener. Things got off to a bad start, with delegates complaining about long queues and poor Wi-Fi. But India did draw in the industry’s biggest names. On February 19th Narendra Modi, the prime minister, stood on stage with Sam Altman and Dario Amodei, the bosses of Open<span>AI</span> and Anthropic (who declined to link arms). The summit declaration is likely to focus on ensuring affordable access to <span>AI</span>, a priority for developing countries. </p><p>Despite its weaknesses, India does have attributes that will give it an important role in shaping <span>AI</span> adoption. For starters it has 900m internet users, who spend on average seven hours a day online. That makes it an unignorable market for <span>AI</span> companies looking to hoover up data on which to train models. Open<span>AI</span>, Anthropic and Google are all competing for Indian custom. On February 16th Anthropic, whose second-biggest market is India, opened an office in Bangalore. And when India played Pakistan a day earlier in the <span>T</span>20 cricket world cup—South Asia’s nearest equivalent to the Super Bowl, with an audience of 450m—the prime advertising slots were taken by Chat<span>GPT</span>.</p><p>Indian companies and consumers are also embracing the technology enthusiastically. One survey found that almost 90% of Indian firms are using <span>AI</span>, versus 62% globally. The country is genuinely world-leading in voice-driven <span>AI</span>, which is how most Indians use the technology. One company that delegates will hear much about is Sarvam <span>AI</span>, which has adapted open-source models by training them on local voice and language data. There had been scepticism about whether it would outperform general-purpose models. But recent benchmark data shows it now does so for certain (albeit niche) tasks, such as reading documents in Indian languages.</p><p>At the summit, India’s political and tech leaders will promote this distinctive approach. “We are not trying to burn millions of <span>GPU</span>s building artificial general intelligence,” says Abhishek Singh, the boss of India<span>AI</span>, a government agency. To become the world’s “adoption capital” is India’s goal. “We think it’s not just about <span>LLM</span>s and four companies running the world,” says Rudra Chaudhuri of <span>ORF</span>, a think-tank, which is a “very expensive” approach to building <span>AI</span>. “India’s approach is bottom up. It’s not the model, it’s the use case that you have to build around.”</p><p>That leads towards a focus on applications. Several Indian startups have attracted global interest and investment, in areas such as cloud computing and customer service. Others focus on applying <span>AI</span> to problems that are urgent in the developing world. Supernova, which aims to make English tuition affordable to all Indians, many of whom lack access to decent schools, has a million monthly users and is growing at a clip. Telemedicine and <span>AI</span>-triage chatbots are expanding in a country in which health care remains limited.</p><p>One question—given the way in which frontier models are galloping along—is whether such indigenous apps will prove most effective in the long run. Another is cost. Many uses will not require expensive “bleeding-edge models”, argues Mr Chaudhuri. A positive spin on limited capital and revenue streams is that Indian companies will be forced to work out how to apply <span>AI</span> frugally. Rajan Anandan of Peak XV, a venture capital firm, thinks the country will be a pioneer of “ultra-affordability” (he predicts a mini “DeepSeek moment” at the summit, with an Indian company set to launch a video-generation model that costs a fifth as much to build as its nearest Chinese competitor).</p><p>The biggest challenge for India may be holding on to talent. It has a huge tech workforce and engineers already skilled in the messy plumbing involved in helping companies adopt new technology, notes Shruti Rajagopalan of the Mercatus Centre, a think-tank. But it struggles to offer the salaries demanded by <span>AI</span> stars. And many founders feel compelled to leave in search of funding; it is increasingly common to see companies with engineers in India and management in California. The government is set to announce investment in <span>AI</span> labs and <span>P</span>h<span>D</span> programmes, designed to make staying put more attractive. Perhaps a bit of hype won’t hurt, either.<span> ■</span></p><p><span>Stay on top of our India coverage by </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/essential-india"><span>signing up to Essential India</span></a><span>, our free weekly newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","the-flaws-in-indias-ai-plans-TE-2026-02-21","d"]
