3:T2493,<p><span><span><span>The greatest show on Earth</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>OpenAI faces a make-or-break year in 2026</span></span></p><p><span><span>One of the fastest-growing companies in history is in a perilous position</span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 30, 2025 06:36 AM | SAN FRANCISCO</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00042.jpg"/></p><p><span>S</span><span>AM ALTMAN</span> is like a juggler on a unicycle. Building all-knowing chatbots powered by cutting-edge artificial-intelligence models is too meek an ambition for Open<span>AI</span>’s boss. To keep his audience rapt, he has thrown ever more balls into the air. Custom chips? Of course. E-commerce? Why not. Business consulting? Too easy. A consumer device? You betcha.</p><p>At the same time, Mr Altman must keep a hand free to hold out his cap, for the show gets more expensive by the day. Leaked figures indicate that Open<span>AI</span> expects to burn through $17bn of cash in 2026, up from $9bn in 2025, and that its losses will continue piling up in each of the subsequent three years (see chart 1). It has already raised over $60bn from investors, more than any private company ever, nearly all of it since late 2022 when Chat<span>GPT</span> shot the previously obscure <span>AI</span> lab to prominence (see chart 2).</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00068.jpg"/></p><p>Mr Altman’s hunger for cash is far from sated. In 2026 Open<span>AI</span> will “almost certainly” raise another slug of capital, says a source close to the company. It is reportedly looking for as much as $100bn, at a valuation of perhaps $830bn, up from $500bn in its latest fundraising round in October. Amazon is separately in talks to invest up to $10bn in the model-maker, which has untangled itself from an exclusive relationship with Microsoft, Amazon’s biggest rival in cloud computing. Nvidia has said it, too, may <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/09/25/nvidias-100bn-bet-on-openai-raises-plenty-of-questions">invest up to $100bn in Open<span>AI</span></a> in $10bn increments to help it buy the chipmaker’s wares. Although he has poured cold water on the idea at various points, Mr Altman is also rumoured to be mulling a public offering.</p><p>Open<span>AI</span>’s unprecedented fundraising has fuelled unparalleled growth. In 2023 its revenue passed $1bn. In 2025 it reportedly hit $13bn, rising to an annualised rate of $20bn by the year’s end. It took Google and Facebook five and six years, respectively, to pull off the same feat.</p><p>The trouble is that Open<span>AI</span>’s demand for computing power—by far its biggest cost—has remained tightly coupled to revenue. The company’s computing needs grew from 200 megawatts in 2023 to 1.9 gigawatts (<span>GW</span>) in 2025. It has signed letters of intent to add a further 30<span>GW</span> of capacity over the coming years at a cost of around $1.4trn. For now, investors are enthralled by Mr Altman’s dazzling feat. Eventually, however, he will have to prove that he can turn a profit.</p><p>Mr Altman continues to argue that Open<span>AI</span>’s economics will improve as the company grows and the upfront cost of training its models dwindles by comparison. Yet training costs keep mounting as Open<span>AI</span> contends with stiff competition from rival model-makers. According to benchmarks collated by the Stanford Institute for Human-Centred Artificial Intelligence, the gap in performance among the most advanced models has narrowed significantly over the past year. Lately the biggest threat to Open<span>AI</span> has been Google, whose Gemini 3 model, released in November, outperformed Open<span>AI</span>’s <span>GPT</span>-5.1 on various measures. Open<span>AI</span> counterpunched with <span>GPT</span>-5.2, but it was far from a knockout blow. Meanwhile, so-called open models, whose numerical parameters (known as “weights”) are freely available, have also been closing the gap in performance with closed models.</p><p>Open<span>AI</span> cannot afford to fall behind in model-making. Already there are signs that Chat<span>GPT</span> is losing momentum. According to Sensor Tower, which monitors web traffic, it had 910m monthly active users as of mid-December, compared with Gemini’s 345m. But Gemini is gaining ground. A recent study of big European countries by Deutsche Bank found that consumer subscriptions to Open<span>AI</span>’s chatbot<span>
</span>“ground to a halt” in the summer and have since barely grown. Recognising the challenge, Mr Altman instituted a temporary “code red” at the start of December, telling staff to pause other initiatives and prioritise improving Chat<span>GPT</span>.</p><p>More troubling still are reports that Open<span>AI</span> is losing money simply by running its models, which many consumers access through the free version of Chat<span>GPT</span>. In November Ed Zitron, a commentator known for his <span>AI</span> scepticism, published leaked Microsoft figures showing that Open<span>AI</span>’s so-called inference costs exceeded its revenue in the first half of 2025. To cut its losses, the company could raise prices or curb access, but that would run the risk of dragging down growth, particularly with competitors nipping at its heels.</p><p><span>High-wire act</span></p><p>All this helps to explain Open<span>AI</span>’s ever expanding focus. Part of the strategy is to develop new routes to monetise its technology. Although Mr Altman reportedly paused work on an initiative to integrate advertising into Chat<span>GPT</span> as part of his “code red”, insiders say the company still has plans to do so in 2026. Already Open<span>AI</span> allows companies including Etsy, an online marketplace, and Walmart, a retail colossus, <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/12/08/how-ai-is-disrupting-shopping">to sell their products in America through its chatbot</a> in return for a fee.</p><p>Open<span>AI</span> is also hoping to expand its revenue from enterprise customers, which tend to be stickier. There its main competitor is <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/07/23/the-dark-horse-of-ai-labs">Anthropic</a>, a rival model-maker whose Claude chatbot has become popular among coders in particular. To catch up, Open<span>AI </span>has built a consulting division that helps big companies deploy its technology, and has developed enterprise tools such as AgentKit, launched in October, which clients can use to automate work. Although consumers still provide the majority of Open<span>AI</span>’s revenue, the share from businesses has been rising.</p><p>Open<span>AI</span>’s strategy also seems to entail vertical integration, drawing inspiration from Google. The search giant’s <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2025/11/25/google-has-pierced-nvidias-aura-of-invulnerability">custom chips</a>, which it began developing more than a decade ago, have become a big advantage in the <span>AI</span> race, costing it between a half and a tenth as much as an equivalent Nvidia chip. The 4bn users of Android devices worldwide also provide a vast distribution channel for Google’s <span>AI</span> products. Over the past year Open<span>AI</span> has signed a deal with Broadcom, a chip designer, to develop its own custom silicon, and hired Sir Jony Ive, the designer behind Apple’s iPhone, to dream up a consumer device.</p><p>The difference is that Open<span>AI</span> does not have the fistfuls of cash provided by Google’s search engine. Already some investors are growing wary. The boss of one venture-capital (<span>VC</span>) firm notes that Open<span>AI</span>’s losses are as big as the deficits of many national governments. He laments that, in fundraising discussions, “you are not even allowed to ask” about the amount of cash it is burning. When questioned in a recent podcast interview by Brad Gerstner, one of Open<span>AI</span>’s most loyal investors, how the company would fund its gargantuan spending commitments, which are equivalent to roughly 100 times its revenue in 2025, Mr Altman snapped: “If you want to sell your shares, I’ll find you a buyer.”</p><p>Open<span>AI</span>’s detractors decry what they see as Mr Altman’s hubris. “This is the WeWork story on steroids,” says another <span>VC</span> boss who backs one of its rivals, referring to the once high-flying office-rental business that collapsed spectacularly under the weight of enormous debts and unrealistic growth projections.</p><p>If Open<span>AI</span>’s enterprise sales disappoint and it is unable to monetise Chat<span>GPT</span> in other ways, the company could quickly unravel. For now, however, Mr Altman still has plenty of believers. “If you’d told me five years ago that Sam was going to pull these deals off, I would never have believed you. He’s better than anyone realised,” says one of his investors. The year ahead will test whether Open<span>AI</span>’s boss is more than just a showman. ■</p><p><span>To track the trends shaping commerce, industry and technology, sign up to “</span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/the-bottom-line"><span>The Bottom Line</span></a><span>”, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter on global business.</span></p>6:["slug","openai-faces-a-make-or-break-year-in-2026-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
