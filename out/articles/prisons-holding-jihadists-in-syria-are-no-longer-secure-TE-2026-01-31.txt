3:Td4f,<p><span><span><span>Breaking down and breaking out </span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Prisons holding jihadists in Syria are no longer secure</span></span></p><p><span><span>The danger of ignoring these camps is becoming more acute</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jan 29, 2026 05:48 AM | Al-Hol</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-31-00141.jpg"/></p><p><span>Suddenly people care about him</span></p><p><span>B</span><span>EHIND A CHAIN-LINK</span> fence in north-east Syria, women clutch rucksacks and glance at their phones. They are Uyghurs—among the thousands of foreign Muslims who travelled to Syria a decade ago to join jihadist groups during its civil war. Since 2018, when Islamic State (<span>IS</span>) lost its last territory, they have been held at al-Hol, a vast detention camp near the Iraqi border. Today many expect to be released.</p><p>Around 35,000 people have been detained in camps run by the Kurdish-led Syrian Democratic Forces (<span>SDF</span>). Al-Hol is divided in two. One section houses Syrians and Iraqis caught up in the war. The other—the “annexe”—holds foreign jihadists, many still loyal to the brutal ideology of <span>IS</span>. </p><p>For nearly a decade they existed in legal limbo. The Kurds could not try them, nor would they free them. Most Western governments, despite American pressure, refused to take their citizens back. But as long as the camps were secure, the problem could be ignored.</p><p>That status quo collapsed last week. As a government offensive retook territory from the <span>SDF</span>, al-Hol and other detention sites changed hands. Kurdish guards fled; fighters loyal to Ahmed al-Sharaa, Syria’s interim president and a former jihadist, moved in. They seem more sympathetic to the inmates.</p><p>Last week your correspondent saw dozens of women climbing out of the foreigners’ annexe. Others were collected by masked men, apparently bound for Idlib, Mr Sharaa’s former fief. “Please, sisters, go back inside,” pleaded one guard as veiled women slipped through a breach in the fence.</p><p>Some governments may now accept that they have a moral and security duty to deal with their nationals, says Devorah Margolin of the Washington Institute for Near East Policy. Others, she warns, will continue to push the problem onto someone else. That is ever riskier.</p><p>America is moving as many as 7,000 of the most dangerous detainees, mostly foreign men, to Iraq. Iraq’s intelligence chief said this week that around 10,000 <span>IS</span> militants are active in Syria (up from 2,000 a year ago). Taking custody of the prisoners is a pre-emptive security move.</p><p>Some of those held in al-Hol have committed no crimes. Some were born there; others arrived as small children. One blue-eyed 15-year-old said he was Bosnian, though he spoke no Bosnian and barely understood what a passport was. After eight years behind wire, he could scarcely remember life outside. The only people who seem eager to claim boys like him are <span>IS</span> recruiters. The danger of ignoring these camps for so long is becoming apparent. ■</p><p><span>Sign up to the </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/middle-east-dispatch"><span>Middle East Dispatch</span></a><span>, a weekly newsletter that keeps you in the loop on a fascinating, complex and consequential part of the world.</span></p>6:["slug","prisons-holding-jihadists-in-syria-are-no-longer-secure-TE-2026-01-31","d"]
