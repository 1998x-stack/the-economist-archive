3:T151d,<p><span><span><span>One step closer</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Israel recognises Somaliland</span></span></p><p><span><span>That may exacerbate regional rivalries</span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 30, 2025 07:43 AM | NAIROBI</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00041.jpg"/></p><p><span>New nation, new merch</span></p><p><span>W</span><span>HEN THE</span> Israeli flag is sighted on the streets of the Muslim world, it is often being set alight or trampled underfoot. Yet in recent days the Star of David has been plastered on buildings and brandished by jubilant crowds in Hargeisa, the capital of Somaliland. On December 26th Israel became the first country to recognise the breakaway republic as a sovereign state. “I am the happiest man in the world,” says an adviser to Somaliland’s president.</p><p>It is a diplomatic coup for Somaliland. Since declaring independence from Somalia in 1991, the statelet has developed a system of government which, though imperfect, has been more stable and democratic than in Somalia, where political dysfunction and civil conflict have prevailed. For this reason many observers, <a href="http://economist.com/leaders/2021/05/08/somaliland-deserves-international-recognition?__cf_chl_rt_tk=7e0HxnMqO0FihyZxej3qgL95pXpeplOI1nO3LSY09SE-1767025528-1.0.1.1-2idjBg.pLlmnHCpuuU0zTaGIQa4Iua6UmEoPCZzVq08">including this newspaper</a>, have argued that Somaliland’s claim to statehood deserves recognition.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00074.jpg"/></p><p>Yet Israel’s gambit carries risks. Borders in Africa have been redrawn before, in Namibia, Eritrea and South Sudan. But multilateral bodies such as the <span>UN</span> and the African Union (<span>AU</span>) signed off on those changes. Israel’s unilateral move could prove destabilising on a continent of weak states and strong ethnic antagonisms. Moreover in recent years rivalries between Middle Eastern countries have created instability across the Horn of Africa, with the United Arab Emirates (<span>UAE</span>) most notably flexing its muscle there. If Israel pushes into the region, rivalries may get nastier still.</p><p>Much will depend on whether other countries follow Israel’s suit. For Somaliland, America is its brightest hope. In the past year American diplomats and generals have made several showy visits. They have long been interested in setting up a military base near Somaliland’s port of Berbera. The statelet already has ties to the <span>UAE</span>, which has invested heavily in commercial and military facilities at Berbera. Other friendly countries include Britain and Ethiopia. Somaliland has “very big expectations”, says the presidential adviser, that more recognitions are in the offing.</p><p>That may be optimistic. Somalia has called Israel’s move “illegal”. The <span>AU</span> and the European Union have both criticised it, as has China. Worse for Somaliland, regional heavyweights such as Egypt, Qatar, Saudi Arabia and Turkey have jointly declared their “condemnation in the strongest terms”. This will make others think twice before recognising Somaliland, says Abel Abate Demissie of Chatham House, a think-tank in London. On December 26th Donald Trump dismissed speculation that America was about to recognise it.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00109.jpg"/></p><p>Somali and Middle Eastern security sources say Israel may already be using Berbera for surveillance and as a potential base for military operations, with covert support from the <span>UAE</span>, though Somaliland’s presidential adviser denies such reports. If Israel already has facilities in Somaliland, it would probably use them to hit the Iranian-backed Houthis who rule much of Yemen and have targeted Israeli cities in recent years and scared most shipping lines from the Red Sea, previously one of the world’s busiest trade routes. An Israeli presence in Somaliland would also challenge Turkey, which has extensive commercial and military interests in Somalia and, to a lesser extent, Sudan.</p><p>Other Muslim powers will be alarmed by signs of deepening collaboration between Israel and the <span>UAE</span>. Egypt, Saudi Arabia and Turkey back the opposite side to the <span>UAE</span> in Sudan’s civil war. And they are aligned with Eritrea in its <a href="https://www.economist.com/middle-east-and-africa/2025/11/13/ethiopia-is-perilously-close-to-another-war">proxy war with Ethiopia</a>, another close ally of the <span>UAE</span>. Israel’s recognition of Somaliland, predicts Mohamed Maareye of <span>TIKLS</span>, a Somalia-based research group, will intensify rivalries across the Horn of Africa. </p><p>In Somalia, meanwhile, al-Shabab, jihadists who control swathes of territory in the south, have denounced Israel as “the biggest enemy of Islamic society”. Widespread hostility to Israel among Somalis may boost their recruitment of fighters. Both al-Shabab and the Houthis, who are said to be forging an alliance, are threatening to attack Somaliland. But none of this dampened the celebrations in Hargeisa. ■</p><p><span>Sign up to the </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/analysing-africa"><span>Analysing Africa</span></a><span>, a weekly newsletter that keeps you in the loop about the world’s youngest—and least understood—continent.</span></p>6:["slug","israel-recognises-somaliland-TE-2026-01-03","d"]
