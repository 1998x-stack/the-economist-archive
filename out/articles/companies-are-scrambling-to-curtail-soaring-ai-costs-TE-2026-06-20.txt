Token reckoning<h1>Companies are scrambling to curtail soaring AI costs  </h1>For the heaviest users, tokenmaxxing is losing its appeal<p>6月 18, 2026 03:17 上午</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-06-20-00035.jpg" title=""/>
<p><b>“I</b><span>t’s going to</span> be an absolute nightmare,” says an executive at a big American tech company. He is talking about an emerging problem for businesses that use artificial intelligence. <span>AI </span>agents—bots that can read, interpret and act—use masses of processing power and have started to run up huge bills. As they proliferate, the problem will grow. Big companies, the executive points out, typically use hundreds of software programs. If each of those offers agents (as they probably will), <span>AI </span>costs could spiral out of control.</p>
<p>Budget management is a new worry for <span>AI </span>adopters. Not long ago employees were encouraged to binge on the technology. Burning through vast numbers of tokens—the chunks of text that models process, which are often used as a unit of pricing—became a badge of honour; techies dubbed it “tokenmaxxing”. Companies showed off staff’s <span>AI </span>use on internal leaderboards. Meta’s display awarded top users titles like “Token Legend”.</p>
<p>Such incentives partly explain the boom in <span>AI </span>spending. So does a change in the way enterprises use the technology. Token-heavy applications, such as reasoning models and agents, are growing more popular. In some cases agents build their own agents, sending costs higher still. Ramp, a corporate-credit-card provider, analyses its clients’ transaction data to shed light on their <span>AI </span>use. It reckons their spending has risen 13-fold in the past year. In April Uber said that it had spent its annual <span>AI</span> budget in four months. Other firms have similar problems. One reportedly spent $500m on <span>AI </span>tokens in a month. Sam Altman, the boss of Open<span>AI</span>, has called mounting customer costs “a huge issue”.</p>
<p>For now, the problem is concentrated. The top spenders tend to be tech firms, because they are early adopters and because <span>AI</span> is particularly good at writing software. Ramp reckons that the 1% of clients that spend the most on <span>AI </span>per employee are racking up average monthly bills of about $7,450 per person; the median client pays $11. And although the big spenders’ <span>AI </span>bills are low compared with the cost of hiring a developer in San Francisco, they are high compared with employing one in Delhi.</p>
<p>Companies are responding in various ways. Although <span>AI</span> laggards are still racing to adopt the technology, for the heaviest users tokenmaxxing is out. Some, including Meta and Amazon, have scrapped their leaderboards. Many are thinking more carefully about model choice. Plenty of tasks do not require pricey, cutting-edge models. Aran Khanna of Archera.ai, which helps companies reduce their cloud costs, points out that in some cases Sonnet, a lagging-edge model from Anthropic, can cost a twentieth of what Opus, a leading-edge one, does. And Kimi, an open-source model from Moonshot <span>AI</span>, a Chinese startup, can cost a twentieth of that.</p>
<p>Another approach is spending caps. Uber has limited its employees to $1,500-worth of tokens each month per coding tool. How firms choose to allocate tokens depends on where they can generate the most value from <span>AI</span>, says Rachel Laycock of Thoughtworks, a consultancy. That often means companies apportion the most tokens to their core business function, such as engineers in a tech firm, she adds.</p>
<p>Some software vendors are experimenting with new pricing plans to ease clients’ concern over costs. Intercom, one such supplier, offers a service whereby the customer pays only for queries that are resolved by its <span>IT</span>-support agent. The big cloud providers have launched cost-management services, too, including budgeting tools and ways to triage queries to the most appropriate model.</p>
<p>For the model-makers, the balancing act is trickier. They want their biggest customers to use as many tokens as possible without balking at the cost. Today the cost of providing<span> AI</span> services is subsidised by the labs. Indeed, Open<span>AI’</span>s plan for winning customers from Anthropic reportedly involves drastic price cuts. But eventually the model-makers will need to turn a profit, which will mean raising prices. Pressure to do so will only intensify later this year if, as expected, Anthropic and Open<span>AI</span> go public. Their customers, then, can look forward to more jaw-dropping <span>AI </span>bills. ■</p>
