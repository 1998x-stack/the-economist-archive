Ready Reader One<h1>Gamified novels—known as LitRPG—are a winning format</h1>Readers are hooked. Authors are reaping the rewards <p>5月 21, 2026 03:18 上午</p><img _="" a="" attends="" book="" by="" carl____="" comic="" con.="" crawler="" dinniman="" dungeon="" matt="" new="" series="" src="/images/articles/TE-2026-05-23-00077.jpg" title="A fan of " york=""/>Winning fans’ hearts
<p><b>A</b><span>LL CARL</span> wants to do is smoke his cigarette, but Princess Donut, his ex-girlfriend’s cat, has escaped. He reluctantly goes after the creature when—“slam”—his building, and the city beyond it, is flattened like “a massive tin can that had been crushed by a giant cosmic boot”. Carl is one of the few survivors left on Earth. Along with Princess Donut, he is forced into a dungeon to fight various monsters; the battles will be televised throughout the universe. As they “level up” through the floors and try to make their escape, they earn <span>XP </span>(experience points).</p>
<p>It sounds like the premise of a <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/10/16/are-video-games-getting-harder">video game</a>. But it is the introduction to “Dungeon Crawler Carl”, a book series by Matt Dinniman, which has sold over 2.7m copies in print. The first instalment has been on the <i>New York Times </i>paperback bestseller list for 19 weeks and counting. The eighth, “A Parade of Horribles”, was released on May 12th. The audiobook is in first place on Audible’s bestseller list in America; all the other books are in the top 20. A <span>TV</span> adaptation and a graphic novel of Carl’s misadventures are in the works.</p>
<p>Gamified novels—known as literary role-playing games, or Lit<span>RPG</span>—are going mainstream. Such novels borrow the tropes of video and tabletop games. (Unlike choose-your-own-adventure tales, however, the reader does not make narrative choices.) Characters face challenges and grow stronger. They go on quests to obtain rewards. In Mr Dinniman’s novels Carl gets tougher as he punches goblins and defeats a “Krakaren”, a monster that is a combination of a “cosmic octopus” and “your average, suburban, anti-vax, let-me-talk-to-your-manager mom”. Throughout the book the reader is updated on his character stats, health bar, <span>XP</span> and special skills.</p>
<p>Video-game vernacular provides a useful shorthand. Minor figures are called <span>NPC</span>s: non-playable characters. By “just saying, ‘He levelled up’, I can communicate a tremendous amount of information that if I was writing traditional fantasy, I would have to demonstrate,” says Seth Ring, who has published nearly 50 Lit<span>RPG</span> titles.</p>
<p>The core readers of Lit<span>RPG</span> are gamers in their 30s who appreciate such elements. But its biggest audience is audiophiles, from truckers to stay-at-home mothers. The novels often have only one perspective, and are usually narrated in the first person, which makes them easy to follow. Fans gush about the voice actors almost as much as they do about the authors.</p>
<p>Though a relatively new player in Western publishing, Lit<span>RPG</span> has been around for a while. (A Russian publisher claims to have coined the term in 2013.) Versions of it have long been popular in Asia. <i>Xuanhuan</i> novels, a type of Chinese fantasy, took off in the early 2000s. Such tales drew on Daoist ideas of “cultivation” and self-improvement and followed heroes as they progressed through different realms. Japanese manga and anime often feature <i>isekai </i>(another world), where characters are transported into video-game-like settings with magic and sword fights. The idea is also a trope in <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/05/08/if-you-havent-heard-of-webtoons-you-will-soon">Korean webtoons</a>.</p>
<p>Western writers have borrowed these ideas and created an “unholy mish-mash” to forge their own version of Lit<span>RPG</span>, says Andrei Nadir, an author who writes under the pen name Actus. Authors including Mr Dinniman argue Lit<span>RPG</span> is less a genre and more a style of writing: stories might be about aliens and post-apocalyptic survival or they might feature steampunk cowboys and orcs who enjoy cooking.</p>
<p>In the early- to mid-20th century pulp fiction such as Raymond Chandler’s “The Big Sleep” and Dashiell Hammett’s “The Maltese Falcon” hooked readers with a punchy style. Lit<span>RPG</span> follows in that tradition, reckons Rhett Bruno, head of Aethon, a publishing house. It is “filling a big void”, he insists, between ponderous fantasy epics such as “Game of Thrones” and smutty <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2024/04/26/romantasy-brings-dragons-and-eroticism-together-at-last">romantasy</a> series like “A Court of Thorns and Roses”. Lit<span>RPG </span>offers “fun, light, rompy-type action fantasy”. Chapters are short, action-packed and usually end with a cliffhanger. The format delivers regular dopamine hits to readers.</p>
<p>Writers of Lit<span>RPG</span> are thinking strategically on and off the page. Many self-publish their work online, chapter by chapter; some are prolific, posting new material daily. Readers often interact with their favourite writers and leave comments on chapters, which then shape the stories. Mr Nadir, for instance, aims for 5,000 words a day and completes a book about every three months. Yet it is not unusual for him to rewrite an already published chapter after receiving feedback from fans.</p>
<p>Authors are gaining more than <span>XP</span>. Loyal followers will pay for early access to chapters. One writer, with the cryptic pen name Zogarth, makes nearly $90,000 every month from subscribers on Patreon, making him one of the most successful authors on the platform. Writers have ample leverage if they choose to negotiate with publishing houses. Mr Dinniman sold the print rights to “Dungeon Crawler Carl” to Ace Books and the audio rights to Audible, but retained the digital ones. “We found something that might change the rules of publishing,” declares Seth Fishman, his agent. (In fact, bespoke rights arrangements are not new, but they are increasingly common among digital-first authors.)</p>
<p>To be sure, Lit<span>RPG </span>is not going to win any <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2023/10/05/the-nobel-prize-in-literature-is-prestigious-lucrative-and-bonkers">prestigious awards</a>. Quantity has been trouncing quality. But readers looking for escapist thrills are often forgiving. “We are trying to write really, really good stories,” Mr Ring says. “We are not trying to create the next great American novel.” ■</p>
