3:T1225,<p><span><span><span>Money and man at Stanford</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Amorality and greed at Silicon Valley’s favourite university</span></span></p><p><span><span>A student journalist examines the culture at Stanford </span></span></p><p><span><span>Jul 02, 2026 06:23 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-07-04-00018.jpg"/></p><p><span>How to Rule the World. </span>By Theo Baker. <span>Penguin Press; 336 pages; $32. Allen Lane; £25</span></p><p><span>T</span><span>HERE ARE</span> good reasons to be sceptical of this book. The author, Theo Baker, is just 21 years old. His subject is his Stanford undergraduate years—a time that feels important while living through it, but is rarely of much interest to anyone else.</p><p>Yet “How to Rule the World” is worth reading. It follows in the tradition of “One L”, <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2024/06/27/why-everyone-should-think-like-a-lawyer">Scott Turow</a>’s account of his first year at Harvard Law School, and <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/06/26/william-f-buckley-the-man-who-put-the-charm-into-conservatism">William F. Buckley</a>’s “God and Man at Yale”, a jeremiad against that university’s leftward drift. Mr Baker offers an inside look at a world-shaping institution. Stanford has “a strong claim…to be the greatest university in the world”, with an annual budget larger than that of 116 national governments. Behemoths including Google and Netflix were founded by its students and alumni.</p><p>The book has three strands: the skimmable bits about parties, friends, how and to whom Mr Baker lost his virginity; a journalistic story about his investigation that compelled Stanford’s president, Marc Tessier-Lavigne, <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2024/02/20/is-running-a-top-university-americas-hardest-job">to resign</a>; and a scathing look at how Stanford’s ties to<a href="https://www.economist.com/the-economist-reads/2022/06/09/the-five-best-books-for-understanding-silicon-valleys-history"> Silicon Valley</a> have hollowed out its moral core.</p><p>Ken Auletta, a journalist at the <span>New Yorker</span>, called the university “a giant tech incubator with a football team”. Many faculty members have grown rich as early investors in their students’ ventures; venture-capital (<span>VC</span>) firms looking for the next big thing have their eyes on undergrads. A student “with sufficiently promising ideas or tech now has no problem raising seven figures on their own”, Mr Baker writes. Some do not even need an idea: “If you have the right pedigree, the <span>VC</span>s will offer you money without so much as the glimmering of a possible startup in mind.”</p><p>Obtaining the right pedigree is essentially a matter of reputation, so the competition to be seen as successful, to get into the “Stanford inside Stanford”, as Mr Baker calls it, is intense. Students cut ethical corners; adults entice these gifted, driven teenagers by telling them how special they are. The traditional goals of a liberal-arts education—self-knowledge, critical thinking, inculcation into a shared culture and history—fall by the wayside.</p><p>Mr Baker, intending to go into computer science, instead finds a home at the student newspaper, the <span>Stanford Daily. </span>Initially he did not believe journalism was for him, but perhaps he had a genetic predisposition towards it: both his parents are superstar journalists and Mr Baker, too, turns out to be a fearless reporter. His investigation into manipulated data in papers co-written by Mr Tessier-Lavigne made him the youngest-ever winner of a George Polk award, a prestigious journalism accolade.</p><p>One reason for Mr Baker’s success at Stanford was a lack of competition: as the <span>Daily</span>’s faculty adviser laments, “very few students care much about journalism.” When everybody wants to get rich—and countenances “a system that embraces ‘just a little bit of fraud’”, as one fellow student quipped—nobody wants to look too hard beneath the shiny surfaces. In decrying Stanford’s thrall to money and power, Mr Baker’s book also testifies to the importance of good local journalism to hold that money and power to account. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","amorality-and-greed-at-silicon-valleys-favourite-university-TE-2026-07-04","d"]
