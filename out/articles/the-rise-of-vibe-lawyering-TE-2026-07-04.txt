3:T1169,<p><span><span><span>Trial and error</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The rise of vibe lawyering</span></span></p><p><span><span>AI is emboldening people to represent themselves in court. The results are mixed</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jul 02, 2026 09:20 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-07-04-00112.jpg"/></p><p><span>W</span><span>HEN BRITAIN’S</span> tax authorities accused Omar Rafique of deliberately underpaying his restaurant’s taxes, he challenged them in court. Alas for Mr Rafique, not only did he fail to submit the correct documents, but his filings were full of fake case law generated by an <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/04/19/why-your-ai-assistant-is-suddenly-selling-to-you"><span>AI</span> chatbot</a>. Though the judge noted sympathetically that <span>AI</span> “might create a more level playing field” for litigants representing themselves, she threw the case out on May 6th.</p><p>Mr Rafique is far from alone in turning to <span>AI </span>rather than to costly lawyers—a trend professionals call “vibe lawyering”. In England and Wales the share of civil cases in county courts with lawyers on both sides fell from 51% in 2022, the year Chat<span>GPT</span> was launched, to 42% last year. In American federal civil courts 17% of people represented themselves in 2025, up from a steady 11% for many years before, according to research by Anand Shah of <span>MIT</span> and Joshua Levy of the University of Southern California. They also find that this year 18% of complaints have probably contained <a href="https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2026/06/16/did-ai-write-this-article"><span>AI</span>-generated text.</a> Courtready, a legal-tech platform, reports that Canadian courts have flagged non-existent cases in 79 rulings so far this year, against seven in all of 2024. Most false citations have been made by people representing themselves.</p><p>As well as inventing cases, <span>AI</span> chatbots often encourage people to litigate, urge them not to settle and overstate their chances of winning. Antony Sendall, a British employment barrister, says chatbots embolden litigants to bring discrimination claims. “A grievance that would have been a few sentences is now more like ten to 12 pages,” he complains. Messrs Shah and Levy found that in cases with self-representing litigants in America, the number of documents filed in the first 180 days is 158% higher than in the pre-<span>AI</span> era. Robert Freund, a commercial lawyer in Los Angeles, says his clients receive correspondence and documents written by <span>AI </span>from opposing parties acting without lawyers.</p><p>And relying on <span>AI</span> can prove expensive. On June 2nd a student was ordered to pay the University of Ottawa C$10,000 ($7,000), partly for using <span>AI</span> improperly to prepare her legal challenge against her removal from a <span>P</span>h<span>D</span> programme. Even lawyers have been caught out. In April Sullivan &amp; Cromwell, a top New York firm, apologised to a court for errors caused by <span>AI </span>hallucinations. On June 8th lawyers on both sides of a contractual dispute in Mississippi were fined for citing fabricated cases. </p><p>Some believe that <span>AI</span> labs should bear responsibility for their models’ inventions. In March Nippon Life, an insurer, sued Open<span>AI</span> in a federal court in Chicago, alleging that Chat<span>GPT</span> had enabled an ex-employee to make what it says is a meritless discrimination claim. Nippon is seeking $10m in punitive damages; Open<span>AI</span> insists that Chat<span>GPT</span> is “not a lawyer”.</p><p>Used with caution, <span>AI</span> can be beneficial. In London on May 14th Tamires Camal Taquidir, a human-resources consultant, recovered £7,000 ($9,300) in fees from a hospitality firm after engaging Garfield <span>AI</span>, which calls itself the world’s first <span>AI</span>-powered law firm approved by regulators. Garfield’s platform did the documentary heavy lifting. But Dominic Li, a barrister, argued the case in court. For now, human lawyers still have their place. ■</p><p><span>To track the trends shaping commerce, industry and technology, sign up to “</span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/the-bottom-line"><span>The Bottom Line</span></a><span>”, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter on global business.</span></p>6:["slug","the-rise-of-vibe-lawyering-TE-2026-07-04","d"]
