3:T1bbd,<p><span><span><span>Schumpeter</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Amazon’s unprecedented gamble on AI redemption might just work</span></span></p><p><span><span>Call it “The Bet-Everything Store”</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:57 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00061.jpg"/></p><p><span>A</span><span>MAZONIANS LIKE</span> to think of their company as frugal—at least compared with many of their tech rivals. Forget the chocolatey treats and massages available to techies in Silicon Valley. At Amazon’s home in Seattle, the most cherished freebies are bananas, distributed from a food truck in the courtyard. This parsimonious culture runs deep. In its heart the e-commerce giant has a shopkeeper’s sense of thriftiness. Except every so often, when it throws caution to the wind and goes on a spending spree. It is on one now. JPMorgan Chase, a bank, calls it “Capexapalooza”.</p><p>It’s a historic gamble. No company has ever matched the $200bn of capital expenditure that Amazon has earmarked for this year, which is partly to be financed by blockbuster bond sales. It is mostly aimed at supporting Amazon Web Services (<span>AWS</span>), its cloud-computing arm, in the race to build artificial-intelligence infrastructure including data centres and the power they rely on. To add to the bill, Amazon has also said it will invest up to $50bn in Open<span>AI</span>, which would be almost quadruple what Microsoft, its arch-rival, has committed to the maker of Chat<span>GPT</span> since 2019.</p><p>Amazon says that strong demand is driving the binge. <span>AWS</span>’s<span>
</span>sales grew at the fastest pace in more than three years last quarter, and despite $250bn of capex in the past three years, Andy Jassy, Amazon’s boss, said it is selling cloud capacity as fast as it is built.</p><p>Yet powerful competitive forces are also at play. Over the past 20 years <span>AWS</span> has been the pioneer in cloud computing, and a big provider of <span>AI</span> services. It is still leader of the pack, but since Open<span>AI</span> launched Chat<span>GPT</span> in 2022, its main cloud rivals, Microsoft’s Azure and Alphabet’s Google Cloud, have grown much faster, chipping away at its lead. By doubling down on <span>AI</span> data centres, and seeking to loosen Microsoft’s grip on Open<span>AI</span>, Amazon looks determined to regain its stride.</p><p>The company has a history of swinging for the fences. Back in 2015 Jeff Bezos, its founder, wrote with his inimitable nerdiness of the “truncated outcome distribution” when attempting to hit home runs. In baseball, the maximum pay-off was four runs. In business, “every once in a while, when you step up to the plate, you can score 1,000 runs.” That letter to shareholders was penned at the start of one spree that scored big time. In 2016-17 Amazon sharply increased spending on logistics and <span>AWS</span>, leading to several years of higher profit margins. But its next capex binge, during the covid-19 pandemic, was initially a bust, as it built far more warehouses than were needed. In 2023 it curtailed spending—just as Microsoft started to ramp it up in anticipation of an <span>AI </span>bonanza. Since then Amazon has been batting more aggressively.</p><p><span>AWS</span> has a few things in its favour. One is its sheer breadth of customers. Its burgeoning relationship with Open<span>AI</span> adds to one with Anthropic, the lab behind Claude. Model-makers are among the biggest sources of cloud demand in the near term, using “gobs and gobs of compute”, as Mr Jassy has put it. But with almost a third of the global market for cloud services, well ahead of Azure and Google Cloud, <span>AWS</span> has much to gain if companies beyond Silicon Valley start to embrace <span>AI</span> more fully. For now, many are still hesitant. But it will take at least 18 months to put this year’s capex to operational use. By then it is possible that<span> AI</span> agents, which can reason in steps, use tools and engage with other bots, will have led to a surge in enterprise spending.</p><p>Working with both Open<span>AI</span> and Anthropic will position Amazon well for that moment. “The Everything Store” remains true to its name, with <span>AWS</span> now able to offer customers ways to build on the two leading families of models as well as numerous others, including its own, Nova. It also supports a variety of chips, including those of Nvidia and its cheaper in-house alternative, Trainium.</p><p>Microsoft and Google offer variety, too. Azure provides models from Open<span>AI</span> and Anthropic, as does Google, which also has its own top-tier model, Gemini, and chips called <span>TPU</span>s. But at a time when all hyperscalers say that demand for their <span>AI</span> services outstrips supply, Amazon’s two rivals may find it harder to allocate scarce computational resources to their cloud customers. That is because their other big businesses—Microsoft’s Office products and Google’s search—are more profitable than their cloud services, making them a more obvious priority for <span>AI </span>chips. With Amazon, things are different. The margins of Amazon.com are paltry compared with those of <span>AWS,</span> and it has a culture of doing a lot with little. Cloud customers may be more likely to come first.</p><p><span>Raising the Rufus</span></p><p>Wall Street has mixed feelings about Amazon’s capex splurge. Although its shares have outperformed Microsoft’s this year, they are still down by 8%, mostly because of doubts about the return on its spending spree. In the short term, depreciation costs stemming from the new investments will rise before revenue from them starts to flow. Brent Thill of Jefferies, a bank, says investors are also worried that the growing costs of <span>AI</span> infrastructure will weaken cloud margins even over the long term.</p><p>Another source of worry is the rise of <span>AI </span>agents embedded in chatbots that can shop on a user’s behalf. They could threaten Amazon’s core business—bypassing <a href="http://amazon.com/">Amazon.com</a> or robbing it of relationships with customers and advertisers. Yet the company is better placed than its sceptics fear. Shortly after it struck its deal with Open<span>AI</span>, the model-maker shelved plans to launch a shopping service called Instant Checkout. Rufus, Amazon’s agentic-<span>AI</span> shopping assistant, helped generate $12bn of incremental annualised sales last year.</p><p>Other model-makers such as Google are muscling into e-commerce, but for now Amazon appears to be fending off the threat. By all means call the spending spree Capexapalooza. But it does not mean Amazon will end up a looza. ■</p><p><span>Subscribers to The Economist can sign up to our </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/opinion"><span>Opinion newsletter</span></a><span>, which brings together the best of our leaders, columns, guest essays and reader correspondence.</span></p>6:["slug","amazons-unprecedented-gamble-on-ai-redemption-might-just-work-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
