3:Td92,<p><span><span><span>Boom clap </span></span></span></p><p><span><span>A Swedish startup wants to reignite Europe’s explosives industry</span></span></p><p><span><span>The war in Ukraine has brought a surge in demand</span></span></p><p><span><span>Dec 30, 2025 05:49 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-01-03-00009.jpg"/></p><p><span>Aiming high</span></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE MENTION</span> of modern warfare may call to mind stealth jets and drones. Yet today’s armed forces still rely on a substance deployed on battlefields since before the first world war. Trinitrotoluene (<span>TNT</span>), an explosive used in artillery shells, missiles and landmines, is stable, easily moulded and much cheaper than newer substitutes. Since Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022, demand for it in Europe has surged. But since the end of the cold war most of the continent’s <span>TNT</span> factories have closed. Joakim Sjoblom, a Swedish entrepreneur, is hoping to reignite the industry.</p><p>Before the war in Ukraine, Poland’s Nitro-Chem, the only <span>TNT</span>-maker left in the <span>EU</span>, could meet the bloc’s needs. But its capacity of around 12,000 tonnes a year, half of which is sold outside Europe, is now insufficient. The <span>EU</span> aims to source 2m 155mm artillery shells at home each year. That alone requires 20,000 tonnes of <span>TNT</span>. Add other munitions and civilian demand, and the total required might exceed 30,000 tonnes.</p><p>The <span>EU</span> could rely more on imports from India, Vietnam and elsewhere, but that would not accord with its efforts to encourage members to buy weapons locally. Enter Mr Sjoblom. After selling his fintech in 2024, the year Sweden joined <span>NATO</span>, he turned his attention to bottlenecks in the defence industry.</p><p>A trajectory from fintech to fireworks may seem odd, but founding Sweden Ballistics (Swebal) was not so different from any other startup, he insists. Both required raising money and finding the right people. For the cash, he has turned to the investment funds of wealthy Swedish families, which will provide the €90m ($106m) needed to build his factory. On December 17th Swebal was granted environmental permits allowing it to break ground. The site will open in 2028 and produce 4,500 tonnes of <span>TNT</span> a year.</p><p>As for the people, it helps that Sweden has a long history of making explosives. Swebal’s plant will be just 3km from Alfred Nobel’s old dynamite factory in the country’s explosives belt. The local government and population are supportive. With three firms nearby making nitroglycerine (which gives dynamite its bang) for civilian use, Swebal will be able to tap trained workers (the chemical process for making <span>TNT</span> is similar). All raw materials can be sourced within 550km.</p><p>Swebal is not alone. Forcit, a Finnish company, plans a €200m investment in a new <span>TNT</span> plant on its country’s west coast, and a Czech-Greek joint venture is spending €83m to restart a factory near Athens. Even so, Europe will be short.
