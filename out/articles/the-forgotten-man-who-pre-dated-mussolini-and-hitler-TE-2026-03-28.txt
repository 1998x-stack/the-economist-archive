Allen Lane; £30</span></p><p><span>F</span><span>AR-RIGHT PARTIES</span> are on the march, causing many to fear the spectre of fascism. Google searches for “fascism” reached a five-year global high in September. Those seeking to understand the history of the term should pick up a new book. The conventional wisdom is that <a href="https://www.economist.com/europe/2025/01/16/a-tv-dramatisation-of-mussolinis-life-inflames-italy">Benito Mussolini</a> was the world’s first fascist. But Sergio Luzzatto, an Italian academic, makes a persuasive case that, in fact, it was the Marquis de Morès, a French aristocrat born in 1858. In his penchant for “hierarchical order, identity politics, conspiracy theories, prejudice against those who are different, incitement to racial hatred” and use of body language and aesthetics to project power, he anticipated the <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/05/22/in-germany-the-nazis-invaded-peoples-dreams">worst actors</a> of the 20th century.</p><p>Morès was a “serial failure”. After a stint in the cavalry, he set off to become a cattle rancher in America. Then he poured energy—and other people’s money—into bold, impractical projects, such as a meat co-operative and a railway along the Indochinese border. Needing someone to blame for his failures, he became a rabid <a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2025/10/09/what-people-talk-about-when-they-talk-about-antisemitism">antisemite</a>. After his return to France in 1889, he found the country in a febrile mood. The collapse of Union Générale, a bank, in 1882, and the stockmarket shock that ensued, was blamed on Jewish financiers. Morès was happy to fan the flames. </p><p>Portraying himself as a revolutionary socialist fighting for the dispossessed, he led violent demonstrations intended to intimidate the authorities. Understanding performative brutality and how to use the media to his advantage, Morès challenged prominent Jews to duels, murdering one. </p><p>After another financial scandal, Morès left for Africa and wrote a polemic, “The Secret of Foreign Exchange”. He used the image of the fasces, a bundle of rods that were a symbol of power in ancient Rome. In the 19th century they had become an expression of “the organic bond between the different components of a national group”. Morès argued that the fasces had to be rebuilt. A year later, he quarrelled over a camel with nomads in the Tunisian desert and was slain in an ambush. His French followers saw him as a martyr. For those whose politics are shaped by hatred and prejudice, Morès is a wretched sort of hero. ■</p><p><span>For more on the latest books, films, </span><span><span>TV </span></span><span>shows, albums and controversies, sign up to </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/culture/2022/11/23/introducing-plot-twist-our-new-culture-newsletter"><span>Plot Twist</span></a><span>, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter</span></p>6:["slug","the-forgotten-man-who-pre-dated-mussolini-and-hitler-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
