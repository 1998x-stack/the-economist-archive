The salesman in the machine <h1>Why your AI assistant is suddenly selling to you</h1>Sponsored chats are changing the way digital advertising works<p>4月 23, 2026 03:39 上午</p><img _turbo="" a="" acan="" all="" can="" feeling="" hand="" has="" holding="" how="" i="" it="" of="" out="" punching="" response="" says="" so="" src="/images/articles/TE-2026-04-25-00055.jpg" stop="" that="" the="" thecaffeine___="" thechatbot_s="" time__="" tired="" title="Illustration of an AI chat where the user is asking " tropic="" unleash=""/>
<p><b>C</b><span>hatbots are</span> <a href="https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/04/13/the-tech-jobs-bust-is-real-dont-blame-ai-yet">employed every day</a> as teachers, counsellors, coders and escorts. Now they are taking on another role: salesmen. Advertisements are popping up ever more frequently in <a href="https://www.economist.com/by-invitation/2026/04/16/welcome-to-the-world-of-machine-audiences">users’ conversations</a> with large language models, punctuating chats with promotions. Consumers’ search queries, editing sessions and even intimate moments are increasingly at risk of interruption by sponsored messages.</p>
<p>As chatbots become adbots, the future of two industries is at stake. For the <a href="https://www.economist.com/leaders/2026/04/16/america-wakes-up-to-ais-dangerous-power">artificial-intelligence business</a>, ads represent a way to monetise a wildly expensive invention that most people currently use free of charge. For the ad industry, adbots are a possible answer to the existential question of how advertising will work if users move away from conventional search engines. Although it is early days, the outlines of a new kind of marketing are emerging.</p>
<p>Many of the <span>AI</span> giants are piling in. In February Open<span>AI</span> said it would begin testing ads in Chat<span>GPT</span>, the most widely used chatbot. Google has been trialling them in its search engine’s “<span>AI</span> Mode” since last year, and Microsoft has woven them into its Copilot. Amazon’s shopping assistant, Rufus, allows brands to sponsor its replies. Meta’s chatbot does not yet show ads, but since December it has been passing on insights from chats to its sister companies, Facebook and Instagram, to help them serve better-targeted plugs.</p>
<p>For Open<span>AI</span>, which reportedly expects to burn $25bn of cash this year and twice as much in 2027, the need for more revenue is acute. Although it sells subscriptions, most of Chat<span>GPT</span>’s more than 900m weekly users are on its free tier. Ads are a way to make money from them. And they may let the company make its best models available to users who do not pay, deepening their engagement, says Benedict Evans, a tech analyst. Even Google, with no immediate cashflow worries, has reason to bring ads to <span>AI</span> chats if these interactions are to replace search for some users. Already it is set to lose its crown as the biggest seller of digital ads: this year Meta will overtake it with ad revenue of $243bn, according to  eMarketer, a research firm.</p>
<p>So far the experiments are modest. Similarweb, a data provider, estimates that Chat<span>GPT</span> is showing ads in only about 1% of conversations, and Google in an even smaller share of <span>AI</span> Mode queries. But they are due to rise fast. Open<span>AI</span> is said to have told investors that it expects Chat<span>GPT</span> to generate $2.5bn in ad revenue this year and $11bn in 2027, with a goal of hitting $100bn by 2030.</p>
<p>The early evidence suggests that Open<span>AI</span> and Google have different approaches to how a chatbot should sell. According to Similarweb, 98.5% of the ads in Google’s <span>AI </span>Mode appear in response to the user’s first query, rather like a conventional search engine. Chat<span>GPT</span>, by contrast, bides its time. Less than half its ads come in the chatbot’s first response, and nearly a third come after the tenth turn in the conversation—like a shop assistant waiting for the customer’s intent to become clear before they make their pitch. “This is not search. This is a new category of advertising,” argues Harel Amir of Similarweb.</p>
<p>So far the results are mixed. Chat<span>GPT</span>’s targeting sometimes seems sophisticated: for instance, advertising job-interview coaching to a user drafting a professional email. At other times it is comically literal. One user asking about cryptographic “private keys” was served an ad for metal safety-boxes from Bed Bath &amp; Beyond. Open<span>AI</span>’s fledgling ad service so far offers advertisers little in the way of performance data or the ability to target specific demographic groups, says Caelean Barnes of Gauge, which helps brands track how chatbots talk about them.</p>
<p>Open<span>AI</span> and Google also seem to be going after different kinds of advertisers. Google’s Direct Offers allow companies to promote a discount code for a product the user is researching in <span>AI </span>Mode. Open<span>AI</span>, meanwhile, is focused on brand-building ads designed to burnish a firm’s image rather than trigger a click. So far 81% of its ads fall into that category, by Similarweb’s analysis. Open<span>AI </span>may reckon that users deep in a coding or editing session are unlikely to break off to pursue an offer.</p>
<p>The good news for chatbot-makers is that users do not seem to be repelled by ads. Some competitors have steered clear of sponsored replies, fearing these will hurt trust: in February Perplexity stopped showing ads and Anthropic skewered its ad-toting rivals with a series of skits showing therapists and professors suddenly launching into sales pitches. Yet Similarweb finds that 72% of ads shown on Chat<span>GPT</span> are not on the final turn of the conversation—in other words, users keep chatting. Conversations incorporating ads last an average of about 20 turns, the same as those without.</p>
<p>For advertisers, <span>AI</span> ads present two challenges. The first is measurement. If users keep chatting rather than clicking through, “post-view attribution”—the murky science of determining whether a user eventually buys the product elsewhere—will become the main way to assess a campaign’s effectiveness. The <a href="https://www.economist.com/science-and-technology/2026/04/15/how-ai-hackers-will-shake-up-cyber-security">second challenge</a> is brand safety. Mark Zuckerberg has painted a picture of a future where advertisers simply tell Meta their objective and let the <span>AI</span> handle the rest. But letting a chatbot write its own creative copy is a gamble. Personalised ads have proved to be more effective than generic ones. Companies may nevertheless be wary of a silicon salesman who could hallucinate a deal too good to be true. ■</p>
