3:Tb25,<p><span><span><span>Damage report</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The war was steadily spiralling in scope and destruction</span></span></p><p><span><span>America, Israel and Iran were striking a widening array of targets across an expanding area</span></span></p><p><span><span>Apr 09, 2026 05:09 AM</span></span></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE DUST</span> has barely settled in the war on Iran. But data from conflict monitors, satellites and independent researchers show that the conflict expanded across a huge area, engulfed a diverse array of targets and affected a huge number of people.</p><p>Since America and Israel started the war on February 28th, Iran has launched at least 4,784 drones and 1,851 missiles at targets from Cyprus to Oman, a greater distance than Paris to Moscow. A conflict between Israel and Hizbullah, a Lebanese militia allied to Iran, has also reignited—an almost separate western front.</p><p>Most of Iran’s munitions have been intercepted—at least 4,513 drones and 1,464 missiles. Most of the bombs aimed at Iran, by contrast, have hit their targets, and so the damage inflicted on it will be far greater. Information from inside the country remains scarce. Even so, using data from <span>ACLED</span> and <span>ISW</span>, two conflict-monitors, we have mapped 3,164 strikes on Iran and a further 2,692 in the rest of the region.</p><p>Satellite radar imagery of Tehran reveals widespread, pinpoint damage, often matching geo-located strike data. And our tally of strikes is likely a massive under-count. America claims to have hit at least 11,000 targets in Iran and Israel 10,000.</p><p>Where targets can be surmised, they hint at a steady expansion in the war’s scope. Early waves of strikes hit military installations and the political leadership, killing Ali Khamenei, the supreme leader, and other senior officials. Later, many strikes appeared to focus on dismantling the regime’s internal security apparatus.</p><p>Recently the intensity of strikes increased, their focus shifting to weapons manufacturers and infrastructure. A spike in attacks in the days before the ceasefire was announced included an Israeli blitz on bridges in the north of the country. That late spike is also corroborated by <span>The Economist</span>’s war-monitoring system, which uses satellite data on abnormal fires near 208 strategic sites across Iran, such as army bases, to detect probable attacks.</p><p>Millions have been exposed to the violence. Roughly 18.5m of Iran’s 93m people live within a kilometre of a reported strike, as do 8.4m people in other countries. On the first day of the conflict, a strike near an <span>IRGC</span> naval base hit a girls’ school, reportedly killing at least 175 people, most of them students.
