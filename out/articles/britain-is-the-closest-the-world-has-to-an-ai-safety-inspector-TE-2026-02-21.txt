3:T276e,<p><span><span><span>AISI does it</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Britain is the closest the world has to an AI safety inspector</span></span></p><p><span><span>But having the first (and best) AI security institute is no excuse for the country to rest on its laurels</span></span></p><p><span><span>Feb 19, 2026 06:23 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-21-00110.jpg"/></p><p><span>G</span><span>OVERNMENTS ARE</span> getting plenty of reminders of the Janus face of artificial intelligence. On January 28th DeepMind, Google’s London-based <span>AI</span> arm, published a paper in <span>Nature</span> on AlphaGenome, a model that could transform understanding of <span>DNA</span>. That same week Dario Amodei, the boss of Anthropic, an <span>AI</span> lab, published an essay urging humanity to “wake up” to <span>AI</span>’s risks. This month his lead safety researcher quit to study poetry, warning that the world was “in peril”.</p><p>Britain was first to grapple with this tension. In 2023 its then prime minister, Rishi Sunak (always a techno-optimist, now an adviser to Anthropic and Microsoft), convened the world’s first major <span>AI</span> safety summit at Bletchley Park. It brought together America and China alongside experts; a successor summit began in India on February 16th. Out of Bletchley came the world’s first <span>AI</span> safety institute, now the <span>AI</span> Security Institute (<span>AISI</span>): a government body designed to understand the capabilities and impacts of advanced <span>AI</span>.</p><p>It became a template for similar outfits in America, Japan, Singapore and elsewhere. William Isaac, a principal scientist at DeepMind, has called Britain’s <span>AISI</span> “the crown jewel of all of the safety institutes”. It reflects the strengths of Britain’s <span>AI</span> strategy: talent, technical skill and diplomacy. It also exposes the country’s limits.</p><p><span>AISI</span> is an example of governance done right. It was modelled on the pandemic-era vaccine task force, which helped Britain become the first country to approve a clinically tested covid-19 jab. Just as that task force was led by an outsider investor, Dame Kate Bingham, <span>AISI</span> brought in a venture capitalist, Ian Hogarth, as its chair, pairing a startup mentality with £100m ($135m) of upfront funding from the government. The institute moved quickly, poaching top talent from DeepMind and from Open<span>AI</span>, the firm behind Chat<span>GPT</span>.</p><p>Today some of the world’s smartest nerds, a mix of ex-tech employees and academics, work at the institute in London. They test what models can do in dangerous domains such as cyber-security, biology and chemistry. Drawing on military war-gaming, they also deploy “red teams” which pose as adversaries to see whether real rogues could bypass safety controls. In 2025 Open<span>AI</span> reported that <span>AISI</span> had uncovered more than a dozen vulnerabilities that could have enabled users to develop biological weapons; these were fixed before the model’s launch. In some ways <span>AISI</span> understands risks better than firms do, one reason why it has secured access agreements with Anthropic, Google and Open<span>AI</span> to test their models before release.</p><p>Some commentators suggest <span>AISI</span> is the closest thing the <span>AI</span> industry has to a weapons inspector. This is an imperfect analogy, not least because this is only part of what it does. The institute instead sees itself as a “technical-research body”, offering insights into fast-evolving systems whose capabilities are poorly understood.</p><p>One recent <span>AISI</span> study found that conversations with an <span>AI</span> chatbot were around 50% more persuasive at shifting political opinions than reading a static, <span>AI</span>-generated message, highlighting the potential risks of “<span>AI</span> propaganda”. Another found that a third of British adults had used <span>AI</span> for emotional needs. The institute’s open-source platform, Inspect, allows firms, academics and governments to run standardised safety tests, making it easier to assess risks against a common yardstick.</p><p>For a modest £66m or so a year, <span>AISI</span> gives the government vital knowledge, most obviously on national security. (Its interim director, Adam Beaumont, comes from <span>GCHQ</span>, Britain’s signals-intelligence agency.) In a speech last October Ken McCallum, the head of <span>MI</span>5, the country’s domestic intelligence agency, praised <span>AISI</span>’s “ground-breaking” work, noting that it was helping the security service plan for the risk that humans could lose control of autonomous <span>AI</span> systems. It also helps project soft power. Britain chairs a global network of <span>AI</span> safety institutes. Other governments come to learn what it got right. By working with labs pursuing artificial general intelligence, the institute helps shape their internal practices.</p><p><span>Safety without sovereignty</span></p><p>Yet if <span>AISI</span> shows how Britain can influence the world on <span>AI</span>, it also risks becoming a totem. It is no substitute for broader <span>AI</span> governance. It was never meant to be a regulator. Its work depends on voluntary access from frontier labs. Because leading <span>AI</span> labs such as Open<span>AI</span> refuse to grant <span>AISI</span> access to their models’ “weights”, the numerical parameters that define them, its teams mostly evaluate models by asking them questions and seeing how they reply.</p><p>But the need to regulate <span>AI</span>’s harms is growing: witness a recent furore over Grok, Elon Musk’s <span>AI</span> chatbot, which spewed out nude deepfakes. Michael Birtwistle of the Ada Lovelace Institute, an <span>AI</span>-research body, argues that the British government has made a “series of retreats” on <span>AI</span> legislation, abandoning a manifesto pledge to enact “binding regulation” to ensure the safe development and use of <span>AI</span> models. Officials point out that risks must be understood before they are constrained; some harms, such as posting sexualised deepfakes of children or building a biological weapon, are covered by existing laws. Still, Britain may have built one of the world’s fanciest smoke alarms: able to detect danger, but powerless to act once it sounds.</p><p>For all its pioneering work, Britain remains a junior partner to America. That was clear at last year’s <span>AI</span> summit, in Paris. America’s vice-president, J.D. Vance, dismissed safety concerns as “hand-wringing”, framing it as focused on “woke” social and ethical issues (like bias, discrimination and privacy) rather than on hard security (like biorisks, cyber-attacks and autonomous weapons).</p><p>Within hours <span>AISI</span> removed references on its website to “societal impacts” and to risks such as inequality and harm to welfare. Within days a minister announced that the institute would be renamed, the “<span>S</span>” no longer denoting “safety” but “security”. The changes were largely cosmetic, argues Professor Seán Ó hÉigeartaigh of the University of Cambridge; <span>AISI</span>’s core focus had always been on security. Even so, the optics were poor. And the reality is that Britain lacks the capital and cheap energy needed to build its own frontier models: its leading lab, DeepMind, had to sell itself to Google in 2014 to fund its research.</p><p>In short, being a leader in<span> AI</span> safety doesn’t make you a leader in <span>AI</span>, says Joshua Tan, an <span>AI</span> researcher at Public <span>AI</span>, a coalition which builds shared <span>AI</span> infrastructure. It’s “like calling yourself a major oil producer without producing any oil”.</p><p>Whatever <span>AISI</span>’s merits, on its own it clearly doesn’t turn Britain into an <span>AI</span> powerhouse. Unable to build the foremost <span>AI</span> models, it is world-leading in testing them, an expertise it still hopes will lead to growth. Now it wants to become among the best at using <span>AI</span> models, too.</p><p>The aim is to be a “country that actually makes <span>AI </span>useful for people”, says Jade Leung, <span>AISI</span>’s chief technical officer and, more pertinently, the government’s <span>AI</span> adviser. This means adopting <span>AI</span> at scale. In a speech on January 28th the secretary of state for science, innovation and technology, Liz Kendall, announced plans to open public datasets to researchers and firms; to pilot more <span>AI</span> tutors in schools; and to put millions of workers through <span>AI </span>training. A new “future of work” unit will monitor whether <span>AI</span> is displacing workers.</p><p>Ms Kendall also spoke of a turbulent world where <span>AI</span> supply chains are weaponised. “We must not be over-reliant on other countries for access to technologies that underpin our future prosperity,” she said. Yet if Britain wants to treat <span>AI</span> capacity as critical infrastructure, the sums behind the government rhetoric are small: so far, £100m for “advanced market commitments” under which the government would act as a first customer for domestic <span>AI</span>-hardware startups, and £500m for a “sovereign <span>AI</span>” unit to invest in Britain-based <span>AI</span> firms. Signals from the market are more promising: this week David Silver, one of Britain’s top <span>AI</span> researchers, announced he was raising $1bn for a new <span>AI</span> lab in London.</p><p><span>AISI</span> is one of the few hard assets Britain has in <span>AI</span>.
