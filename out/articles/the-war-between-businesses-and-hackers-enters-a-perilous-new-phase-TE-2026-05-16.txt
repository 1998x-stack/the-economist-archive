3:T1cc5,<p><span><span><span>Mythos and reality</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>The war between businesses and hackers enters a perilous new phase</span></span></p><p><span><span>AI agents present novel dangers</span></span></p><p><span><span>May 14, 2026 12:54 PM | LOS ANGELES </span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-05-16-00080.jpg"/></p><p><span>C</span><span>YBER-SECURITY IN THE</span> age of <a href="https://www.economist.com/science-and-technology/2026/04/15/how-ai-hackers-will-shake-up-cyber-security">artificial intelligence</a> in some ways resembles modern warfare. It is asymmetric: small bands of attackers armed with the latest technology can penetrate the most powerful defences. And the weaponry is increasingly autonomous. As Nikesh Arora, boss of Palo Alto Networks (<span>PAN</span>), a big cyber-security firm, puts it, “<span>AI</span> has to fight <span>AI.</span>” The good guys do not always win.</p><p>In recent weeks Anthropic and Open<span>AI</span> have unveiled <span>AI</span> models—<a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/04/08/how-dangerous-is-mythos-anthropics-new-ai-model">Mythos</a> Preview and 5.5-Cyber, respectively—so capable of penetrating weak spots in cyber-defences that the <span>AI</span> labs have released them only to trusted firms. But that is no guarantee of safety. New models are emerging all the time and hackers already use earlier varieties. Moreover, as Mr Arora says, it is not a fair fight. With <span>AI</span>, lone wolves can carry out attacks that used to require whole teams, and they have to be right just once to succeed. Defenders, by contrast, have to be right every time.</p><p>That is why cyber-security firms, as well as businesses at large, are scrambling to prepare for a wave of <span>AI</span>-powered cyber-crime. Within the industry, <span>PAN</span> and leading rivals such as CrowdStrike have formed alliances to try to make deployment of the latest models safer. Among their customers, the immediate reaction to models like Mythos and 5.5-Cyber was one of “panic and freak-out”, says Adam Meyers of CrowdStrike. But the models’ appearance was also a wake-up call. Assessments of cyber-readiness are moving from <span>IT</span> departments to <span>C</span>-suites and boardrooms. For the time being, these are more likely to be pessimistic than optimistic.</p><p>The worst news is that however tightly <span>AI</span> labs limit access to their most hazardous creations, criminals will catch up. Soon after Anthropic unveiled Mythos, Open<span>AI</span> produced its own 5.5 series that, according to the British government’s <span>AI</span> Security Institute, may be even stronger. Many expect other model-makers, such as open-source or Chinese outfits, to quickly develop the long-term reasoning and other capabilities that make Mythos and 5.5-Cyber so effective. The genie is out of the bottle.</p><p>Even before the new models were released, older ones were enabling hackers to intrude faster and more frequently. CrowdStrike has said that <span>AI</span>-enhanced attacks rose by 89% in 2025 from the year before. <span>PAN</span> found that, equipped with <span>AI</span>, the fastest quartile of attackers were able to steal data from a software system they had broken into in just over an hour last year, down from almost five in 2024. A typical firm may take days to detect a breach.</p><p>The rise of autonomous <span>AI</span> agents that can handle multiple tasks on their own further increases the risks. As Mr Arora says, hackers can use agentic tools to be even more menacing. And the more firms adopt agents for coding, customer service and so on, the bigger the area for hackers to attack. In anticipation of this, late last month <span>PAN</span> said it would acquire Portkey, a firm that helps manage and protect <span>AI</span> agents. Firms “are building more software than ever, so we are exposing ourselves more”, says Jeremy D’Hoinne of Gartner, an <span>IT</span> consultancy.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-05-16-00125.jpg"/></p><p>Already companies are overstretched as they try to patch the bugs that <span>AI</span> tools are helping to reveal. Security flaws are tagged as “common vulnerabilities and exposures” (<span>CVE</span>s), which are made known either to the firms that make the software or to specialised organisations. The number of <span>CVE</span>s reported has been surging recently (see chart). Mr Meyers of CrowdStrike says some speculate that the annual figure could soon increase ten-fold, to 480,000, as more powerful <span>AI</span> models detect even more bugs. “The assumption is that <span>AI </span>will find vulnerabilities faster than patches can be written,” says Erik Nost of Forrester, another consultancy.</p><p>The good news is that though <span>AI</span> is arming attackers, it is also aiding the defenders. Some businesses considered especially critical to the infrastructure of the internet, such as hyperscalers and cyber-security firms, have gained access to the limited-release Mythos and 5.5-Cyber models of Anthropic and Open<span>AI</span>, respectively, to test their own systems. The two <span>AI </span>labs also have lower tiers, whose members are given access to models that are slightly less permissive than Mythos and 5.5-Cyber but have more cyber-capabilities than those on general release. The recipients are akin to pandemic-era key workers receiving early doses of vaccines.</p><p>Mozilla, creator of Firefox, a web browser, recently offered an optimistic example: an early version of Mythos helped it identify 271 vulnerabilities in a new iteration of Firefox. It said the model was capable of identifying every bug that a human could, which was not possible only a few months ago. This helped level the playing field against attackers. “Defenders finally have a chance to win, decisively,” it said in a blog post.</p><p>But it is not just the enhanced capabilities of the cutting-edge models that are assisting defenders. Cyber-security firms are also developing their own tools, which they call harnesses, to make all models more effective. On May 12th Cisco, an <span>IT </span>giant with access to both Mythos and 5.5-Cyber, said it would make available an open-source, step-by-step guide to creating cyber-security harnesses to help firms use any model to boost their defences.</p><p>There are widespread hopes that the industry can rise to the challenge of <span>AI</span> if it gets its act together. Anthropic and Open<span>AI </span>have won widespread plaudits for leading a collaborative approach. But although Mr Arora gives them an <span>A</span>+ for intent, <span>PAN</span>’s boss reckons neither the labs nor the cyber-security industry deserves more than a <span>B</span>+ for execution. That is partly because <span>AI</span> threats are still so new that everyone is learning as they go. “There’s no magic bullet. There’s no panacea. We don’t know what the right answer is yet.” Music to the ears of hackers, no doubt. ■</p><p><span>To track the trends shaping commerce, industry and technology, sign up to “</span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/the-bottom-line"><span>The Bottom Line</span></a><span>”, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter on global business.</span></p>6:["slug","the-war-between-businesses-and-hackers-enters-a-perilous-new-phase-TE-2026-05-16","d"]
