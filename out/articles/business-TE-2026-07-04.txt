3:T157e,<p><span><span><span>The world this week</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Business</span></span></p><p><span><span>Jul 02, 2026 06:23 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-07-04-00071.jpg"/></p><p>The Trump administration lifted its ban on non-Americans using <span>Anthropic’s</span> latest Fable and Mythos <span>AI</span> models, which it had decreed in mid-June. The ban was imposed over a potential breach of security protocols in Fable; as a result Anthropic closed all access to Fable and Mythos, even internally. The <span>AI</span> firm has implemented new safeguards and worked with the Commerce Department to resolve the concerns, but the issue has highlighted the ease with which the American government can restrict access to frontier <span>AI</span>. Anthropic said it underlined the need for the industry to find a consistent way to assess and fix potential evasions of <span>AI</span> safeguards.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-07-04-00121.jpg"/></p><p>Despite the conflict with Iran and some bouts of volatility, <span>stock markets</span> recorded their best quarter in six years. The <span>S&amp;P</span> 500 rose by 15% from April to June, the <span>NASDAQ</span> Composite by 21% and the pan-European <span>STOXX</span> 600 by 10%. Japan’s Nikkei 225 had its best three months ever, rising by 37%. South Korea’s <span>KOSPI</span> increased by 68%. Even so, the share prices of some big tech giants did not fare so well in June, with Apple, Meta and Nvidia all down. Microsoft’s stock fell by 17%, its worst month since December 2000.</p><p><span>South Korea’s</span>
<span>chipmakers</span> were largely responsible for a 70.9% year-on-year rise in the country’s exports in June, the biggest such leap in 50 years. Driven by demand for memory chips, semiconductor exports were up by 200%. Samsung and <span>SK</span> Hynix, meanwhile, joined the government in announcing a $600bn investment for new chip factories in South Korea’s least-developed areas.</p><p>The <span>yen</span> hit ¥162 to the dollar for the first time in 40 years, increasing the likelihood of Japan’s financial authorities intervening to defend the currency. Investors are betting that Japan will find it hard to curb inflationary pressures caused by higher energy costs.</p><p>The future of the <span>free-trade agreement</span> between America, Canada and Mexico (<span>USMCA</span>) was in some doubt after the Trump administration declined to renew it. The <span>US</span> trade representative, Jamieson Greer, said America would now negotiate with Canada and Mexico about the <span>USMCA</span>’s “shortcomings”.</p><p><span>Accelerating change</span></p><p>Reports emerged that <span>Volkswagen</span> will lay off 100,000 employees worldwide, about a sixth of its workforce, and end production at four factories in Germany. The carmaker has been aiming for 50,000 job cuts by 2030. <span>VW</span>’s powerful unions were not happy; reports suggested they had not been told of the extra redundancies. The company has been trying to catch up with the switch to electric cars and its sales in China are falling. It says American tariffs on <span>EU</span> vehicles and the conflict in the Middle East have also hurt its bottom line. </p><p><span>Rocket Lab, </span>a rockets-and-space firm founded in New Zealand but now based in America, said it would buy <span>Iridium</span>, a satellite operator. Iridium runs a network of internet satellites, but has been eclipsed by the success of SpaceX’s rival Starlink system. </p><p>Another big shake-up to America’s media industry loomed, as <span>Comcast</span> said it would spin off its <span><span>NBC</span></span><span>Universal</span> and <span>Sky</span> subsidiaries into a separate company, leaving it to focus on its cable and tech business. It hopes to complete the spin-off by mid-2027, but noted there were no assurances the transaction would be completed.</p><p><span>Nike</span> announced a quarterly profit of $1.1bn thanks mostly to $986m it received in refunded tariff costs from the government. Its footwear sales rose in America but fell in Europe, and China, where they declined by 13%, year on year. Total revenue of $11bn was the company’s lowest since 2022. Nike’s share price is down by 30% this year.</p><p>In <span>Indonesia</span>, the founder of Gojek, a superapp offering 20 services across South-East Asia, including ride-hailing and food deliveries, was sentenced to at least ten years in prison for corruption. Nadiem Makarim left Gojek to become the government’s education minister in 2019. He was found guilty of procuring overpriced Chromebook laptops running on software made by Google, an investor in Gojek, which the court decided was an abuse of authority. Mr Nadiem is to appeal against the verdict.</p><p><span>Park it</span></p><p>Loved and loathed in equal measure, <span>Lime</span> started selling shares on the Nasdaq exchange in an <span>IPO</span> that raised $167m. The stock rose by 4% on the first day of trading. The company hires out white and pale-green electric bikes in 230 cities around the world and is backed by Uber, which allows users to rent Lime’s bikes through its app. Its global user base rose by 21% last year. In London local councils man hotlines to deal with complaints about rental bikes left strewn across pavements.
