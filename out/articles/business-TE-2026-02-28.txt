The world this week<h1>Business</h1><p>2月 26, 2026 04:27 上午</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-28-00004.jpg" title="hipping containers stand stacked at the Port of Los Angeles"/>
<p>Donald Trump’s new 10% <b><a href="../../feed_14/article_0/index_u53.html">global tariff·</a></b><b> </b>came into force. The president  imposed the levy under a statute from 1974, after the Supreme Court struck down his previous tariffs. In its decision the court did not say whether refunds should be issued for tariffs collected under the disallowed policy. That will be left to lower courts. FedEx has become the first large company to sue the government for a full refund of the duties it has had to pay. The legal process will be long, slow and complicated.</p>
<p>Mr Trump intends to raise his global tariff eventually to 15%. That has angered countries that have struck a <b>trade deal</b> or are negotiating with America. Lawmakers in the European Parliament suspended the ratification process for the <span>EU</span>’s agreement with America, though the <span>EU </span>trade commissioner said respect for the deal was “paramount”. Mr Trump warned countries not to “play games”, or they could face even higher tariffs.</p>
<p>The <b>American economy</b> grew by 1.4% at an annual rate in the fourth quarter of 2025, a big drop from the 4.4% recorded in the third quarter. The Bureau of Economic Analysis estimated that the long government shutdown shaved one percentage point off the quarter’s growth rate. The economy expanded by 2.2% in the whole of 2025, the slowest pace since the pandemic year of 2020.</p>
<p>The yen fell sharply against the dollar, after Takaichi Sanae, Japan’s prime minister, nominated two doveish candidates to the <b>Bank of Japan’s</b> policy board. Asada Toichiro and Sato Ayano support  economic stimulus and lower interest rates, which could put them at odds with Ueda Kazuo, the central bank’s governor, who may want to raise rates.</p>
<p><b>Aston Martin</b> decided to cut its workforce by 20%, as it struggles to generate cash and manage its large pile of debt. The British luxury carmaker blamed tariffs in part, saying they had been “extremely disruptive” to its business.</p><h4>Rewriting the script</h4>
<p><b>Paramount</b> raised its hostile takeover offer for <b><a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/01/22/the-battle-for-warner-bros-is-only-getting-fiercer">Warner Bros Discovery</a></b> from $30 a share to $31. Warner acknowledged that this could constitute a “superior proposal” to the deal it has accepted from Netflix. It said it would engage with Paramount, but that the merger agreement with Netflix remained in effect.</p>
<p>The government of Panama said Moller-Maersk and <span>MSC</span>, two European shipping lines, were now operating two ports on the <b>Panama Canal</b>. In January the country’s Supreme Court annulled the contract of <span>CK </span>Hutchison, a Hong Kong conglomerate, to run the facilities. The Panamanian president said <span>CK</span> Hutchison had established an “autonomous territory” at the vital waterway during its decades-long operations. The company described the takeover as “unlawful”.</p>
<p>Aliko Dangote, Africa’s richest man, said he would list his oil refinery in Lagos on the <b>Nigerian</b> stock exchange in the coming months, with the minority stake currently owned by the state oil company up for grabs. The listing of the refinery, which is valued at some $20bn, may help spur interest in the country’s stockmarket.</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-02-28-00005.png" title=""/>
<p><b><a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/01/29/how-ibm-became-an-ai-darling"><span>IBM</span></a><a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/01/29/how-ibm-became-an-ai-darling">’s</a></b> share price recorded its biggest one-day loss since 2000, after <a href="../../feed_13/article_5/index_u3.html">Anthropic·</a> announced that its <span>AI</span> coding tool could now update an old programming language that is run on <span>IBM</span>’s mainframe computers. Modernising the system “once required armies of consultants spending years mapping workflows”, boasted Anthropic. “<span>AI</span> changes this.”</p><h4>Gulp</h4>
<p><span>IBM</span>’s share price was not the only one to come under pressure, as a fresh sell-off hit the stocks of software firms. A blog post by Citrini Research, a platform for equity research, bore some of the blame. It imagined an <b>economy torn asunder</b> by <span>AI</span> in 2028, when America’s unemployment rate hits 10%. The firm stressed that its analysis was a scenario and not a prediction. “This isn’t bear porn or <span>AI</span>-doomer fan-fiction,” it noted. Still, it then went on to describe an “economic pandemic”, as white-collar jobs once considered tedious disappeared. They were replaced by <span>AI</span> agents that “found nothing tedious”.</p>
<p><b>Meta </b>announced that <b><span>AMD</span></b> will supply it with  graphics-processing units to help power its <span>AI</span> infrastructure. Under the deal Meta could take a stake of up to 10% in the chipmaker. As it seeks ways to feed its voracious appetite for computing power, Meta has also recently reached a chip-supply agreement with Nvidia. It plans to spend up to $135bn this year on <span>AI</span>.</p>
<p><b>Nvidia</b> produced another stellar set of quarterly earnings. Revenue rose by 73%, year on year, to $68.1bn, and net profit came in at $43bn, up by 94%. Computing demand for <span>AI</span> “is growing exponentially”, said Jensen Huang, its <span>CEO</span>.</p>
