Schumpeter<h1>What the heirs to General Electric did next</h1>Their success illustrates the benefits of breaking up—and more<p>3月 05, 2026 04:15 上午</p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-07-00045.jpg" title="Illustration of a businessman plugging in a cable that powers colorful company buildings branching upward in different directions"/>
<p><b>T</b><span>he ideal</span> business school is little more than a well-endowed history department teaching the canon of great American companies. General Electric is one of them. Its rise and fall vividly illustrate the genius of entrepreneurs, the struggles of succession and the allure of financial alchemy. Ostensibly novel questions of business strategy find instructive answers in its archives. How to flog a new technology your patrons cannot afford? <span>GE</span> accepted payment in the form of its customers’ shares a century before <a href="https://www.economist.com/united-states/2025/10/30/led-by-nvidia-the-ai-industry-has-plans-to-reindustrialise-america">Nvidia</a> did. Or profit from one in which the government takes a special interest? <span>GE</span> started the Radio Corporation of America to help dislodge British dominance of the communications industry after the first world war.</p>
<p>The reading list is long, varied and incomplete. Jack Welch, who ran <span>GE</span> between 1981 and 2001 (by which time the sprawling conglomerate was the world’s most valuable firm), was the author of four books and an online <span>MBA</span> course. When Jeffrey Immelt, Welch’s successor, published his memoir in 2021, the firm had fallen out of the top 100. That year, after selling assets for a decade, the company announced that it would break into three separate companies called <span>GE</span> Aerospace, <span>GE</span> Healthcare and, inexplicably, <span>GE</span> Vernova. Two comprehensive histories of <span>GE</span> were published around the same time, doubling as lengthy obituaries. Most predicted a faded and fragmented future for the firm—a corporate equivalent of the Austro-Hungarian empire. Yet the reality of dissolution could not have been grander.</p>
<p>Today <span>GE</span> Aerospace and <span>GE</span> Vernova, a power business, dominate industries whose times have come. The former, which as the senior branch of the family inherited <span>GE</span>’s chief executive and stockmarket ticker symbol, cannot build engines fast enough. But the real money, two-thirds of the firm’s revenue, is made fixing them: <span>GE</span> Aerospace exerts herculean pricing power when selling spare parts for its engines, given the costs <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2026/03/02/airlines-take-a-hit-from-hostilities-in-the-middle-east">airlines</a> incur when their planes are grounded. So does <span>GE</span> Vernova, which is enjoying massive demand for its gas turbines and electrical equipment as tech companies build data centres to run their artificial-intelligence models. Orders are growing about as fast as they did in 2000, at the height of the dotcom bubble.</p>
<p>The pair are everything the <span>GE</span> of a decade ago was not. Most importantly, they are profitable. Their combined annual net income of $14bn last year was more than twice the cumulative profit managed by <span>GE</span> in the decade before its break-up. Their balance-sheets are unburdened by complexity or large debts. Both firms are beloved by the market. Add the much smaller health-care operation and the three heirs are worth $600bn, five times <span>GE</span>’s value before the split was announced. Their size-weighted price-to-earnings ratio is 45—double what it was in 2000.</p>
<p>Even long-disinherited cousins of <span>GE</span>’s heirs are prospering. Baker Hughes, an oil-services firm, is worth nearly three times what it was when <span>GE</span> sold its controlling share in 2019. <span>GE</span>’s trainmaking business, shed the same year, has done even better. The division which made <span>GE</span>’s small, on-site generators is thriving under the ownership of a private-equity fund: sold for $3bn in 2018, it is probably worth $15bn today. Another even managed to grow while owned by a French firm: <span>GE</span>’s water-treatment unit was sold for $3bn to Suez. Last year Veolia, which now owns Suez, bought out a minority investor in the division at a valuation of $6bn.</p>
<p>What about the financiers who almost sank the whole ship in 2008? <span>GE</span> Capital, the firm’s financial arm, had grown to supply nearly half of earnings by 2005, from 8% in 1981. Its assets are now scattered throughout the financial system. AerCap, the owner of its enormous plane-leasing business, is thriving; Synchrony, which lends to individuals, is not. <span>GE</span> Capital is a source of great inspiration in some corners of Wall Street. No firm has made more effort to recreate its operations than Apollo—so far without, thankfully, <span>GE</span>’s reliance on short-term funding markets. The private-equity investor turned life insurer has assembled a similarly eclectic range of asset-based lenders through which to invest its policyholders’ premiums. They include an aircraft-lending business previously owned by <span>GE</span> and a mid-market corporate-lending division staffed with former <span>GE</span> Capital employees.</p><h4>Mass appeal</h4>
<p>One lesson of the <span>GE</span> revival is so obvious that it doesn’t bear repeating in besuited company: businesses perform better when they are not subsumed within giant conglomerates. Honeywell, another big American industrial conglomerate, will complete a similar three-way split later this year. But the revival owes more to a deeper change of mood under way in business and markets.</p>
<p>Everywhere hard hats are triumphing over soft heads. Asset-heavy firms are outperforming ones which investors now fear have no valuable assets at all, like software companies (and the private-markets funds which lend to them). Industrial and defence stocks are soaring. Governments obsess over <a href="https://www.economist.com/leaders/2026/02/26/americas-dangerous-pursuit-of-critical-mineral-dominance">critical minerals</a>. The capex budgets of the big technology firms, which are more likely to be held back by a shortage of power than of capital, exceed those of the energy industry. Venture-capital types crow about the dominance of “atoms” over “bits”. Some bosses struggle to convince the market their ethereal businesses exist at all. “People say that cryptocurrency is intangible. It’s not. It’s very tangible,” said Eric Trump, the president’s son, in a recent video of him strolling round his bitcoin-mining facility.</p>
<p>The reassertion of the physical world is easier to observe than to fully explain. A combination of higher interest rates, the demands of <span>AI</span>, manufacturing nostalgia, war and general paranoia are to blame. A combination of firms which started life as divisions of <span>GE </span>will profit most from it. ■</p>
