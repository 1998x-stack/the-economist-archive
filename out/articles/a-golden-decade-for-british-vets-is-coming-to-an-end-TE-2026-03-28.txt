3:T14bd,<p><span><span><span>Peak dog</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>A golden decade for British vets is coming to an end</span></span></p><p><span><span>An inquiry has chilled investment in a sector already facing declining demand</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00019.jpg"/></p><p><span>Wild rover</span></p><p><span>T</span><span>HE LONDON VET SHOW</span>, Europe’s biggest vet conference, offers a window into Britain’s <a href="https://www.economist.com/business/2024/09/12/people-are-splurging-like-never-before-on-their-pets">pet mania</a>. Stalls advertise probiotic powders and memorial jewellery made from a deceased pup’s fur. A vendor demonstrating a pet-activity monitor insists that “steps matter for dogs, too.”</p><p>Britons are not known for their displays of affection, but have long made an exception for their pets. On veterinary services alone—which includes vet visits, grooming and training—they spend about £6.7bn ($8.9bn) a year. According to the Office for National Statistics (<span>ONS</span>), household spending on such services rose by 2.7% a quarter on average between 2019 and 2023, after adjusting for inflation, as pet ownership boomed during the pandemic. It was a firm favourite for investors, particularly private-equity (<span>PE</span>) firms keen to consolidate the sector.</p><p>But that giddy momentum has stalled. Between 2024 and the third quarter of 2025, household spending fell by around 0.2% per quarter on average. Investors have taken note: shares in <span>CVS </span>Group, one of the country’s largest vet groups, have dipped by more than 33% since the start of 2024, while Pets at Home, a rival, is down by 43%. A golden decade for <a href="https://www.economist.com/britain/2024/10/24/britain-is-a-world-leader-in-pet-health-care">Britain’s vet industry</a> might be coming to an end.</p><p>Three forces are to blame. The first is regulatory scrutiny. A two-year investigation by the Competition and Markets Authority (<span>CMA</span>), a watchdog, prompted by thousands of complaints about high prices and opaque billing, concluded on March 24th. It follows a decade of rapid consolidation in which <span>PE</span> funds rolled hundreds of small clinics into large chains, drawn by the industry’s fragmentation, (supposed) resilience in downturns and Britain’s high pet ownership. Big groups such as <span>IVC</span> Evidensia and <span>CVS </span>now own roughly 60% of Britain’s vet practices, up from about a tenth in 2013; three of the six largest operators are <span>PE</span>-backed. Such concentration could dull competition, worries the <span>CMA</span>.</p><p>The regulator’s proposals—clearer ownership labels, mandatory price lists and a cap on prescription fees—are hardly radical. But the review has cast a shadow. According to Pitchbook, <span>PE</span> deals across Britain’s vet industry averaged £810m a year between 2020 and 2023; by late November the figure for 2025 was just £28m, even as dealmaking accelerated in Europe.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00108.jpg"/></p><p>The idea that corporate vet groups are merely profiteering machines is misleading, argues Charles Hall, an investment banker. Their operating margins, around 20%, are healthy but not exceptional. Large groups are able to buy equipment such as <span>CT</span> and <span>MRI</span> scanners. And as owners increasingly demand that their pets receive treatments similar in standard of care to humans, arguably such investments make this possible.</p><p>The second reason is fewer new pets. The return to the office and the cost-of-living squeeze are making people think twice about adding a pooch to their household. Vet bills have risen nearly twice as fast as inflation since 2020, according to the <span>ONS</span>. The Kennel Club, Britain’s main registry for pedigree dogs, reports that puppy registrations surged by nearly 40% in 2021 to a record 349,000, before falling by 21% in 2022, and then by a further 18% in 2023.</p><p>This thinning pipeline of new puppies and kittens is the main reason behind falling profits in clinics. After a pet’s first birthday, demand for care tends to dwindle to a trickle, rising again only in old age.</p><p>The third force is thriftier owners. Squeezed finances mean owners are being choosier: when an animal is ill or injured, people will find the cash. But the sales of fancy leads, personalised collars and toys—all of which beef up vet finances—have slumped, says Guy Killick, of Palace Vets, a family-run veterinary practice.</p><p>Britain’s fondness for its pets has not dimmed, and demand for treatment will revive as the animals bought during covid-19 snuggle into their silver years. Regulatory clarity may tempt investors back. But for companies hoping for a return to the treats-filled days of high margins and revenue, it’s probably time to accept that Britain has left its Greyhound phase and entered its Basset Hound years. ■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in Britain, </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/blighty"><span>sign up</span></a><span> to Blighty, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","a-golden-decade-for-british-vets-is-coming-to-an-end-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
