3:T1af0,<p><span><span><span>Good or bad ODA?</span></span></span></p><p><span><span>Britain’s foreign aid morphs from open-handed to hard-headed</span></span></p><p><span><span>Overseas assistance has to become smaller and smarter</span></span></p><p><span><span>Mar 26, 2026 05:58 AM</span></span></p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00037.jpg"/></p><p><span>Help used to be on its way</span></p><p><span>“A</span><span>ID IS A</span> peculiar thing,” says Stefan Dercon, former chief economist at the Department for International Development (<span>D</span>f<span>ID</span>). “You take some of your own money and spend it in the interest of people other than your own citizens that pay the taxes.” But at its best it has helped achieve extraordinary progress. Foreign aid, or “official development assistance” (<span>ODA</span>), peaked in 2023 when $240bn was provided globally, and has been a big contributor to the decline of deaths from <span>AIDS</span>, malaria and vaccine-preventable diseases. Britain has often been at the forefront, both in terms of spending levels and expertise—in particular since 1997 when <span>D</span>f<span>ID</span> was born. In 2013 Britain became the first <span>G</span>7 country that met the <span>UN</span>’s aid target of 0.7% of gross national income (<span>GNI</span>).</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00075.jpg"/></p><p>That era is over. In 2020 <span>D</span>f<span>ID</span> was controversially melded back into the Foreign Office. A year later the <span>ODA</span> budget was cut from 0.7% of <span>GNI</span> to 0.5%. Then the Labour government slashed it to 0.3% of <span>GNI</span> by 2027. Even that slim share involves creative accounting that follows “the letter but not the spirit of <span>ODA</span>”, notes Nilima Gulrajani of the Overseas Development Institute, a think-tank. About a quarter of the previous three years’ <span>ODA</span> budgets has been devoured by Britain’s domestic spending on refugees, so the amount going overseas—much of it to Ukraine—will be closer to 0.2% of <span>GNI</span> (see chart). The government’s newly announced allocations for aid programmes mean that bilateral aid to sub-Saharan Africa will have shrunk by half between 2024 and 2028.</p><p>Britain’s paltry growth and its cost-of-living squeeze are part of the explanation. “You don’t feel very generous towards other countries if you think you’re struggling yourself,” says Ian Mitchell, of the Centre for Global Development (<span>CGD</span>), another think-tank. But a deeper reason is a new geopolitical reality.</p><p><span>Tougher love</span></p><p>Many countries are prioritising hard power over soft; and national interest, not altruism, increasingly dictates how aid money is spent. In his second term Donald Trump set about dismantling <span>USAID</span>, America’s main aid agency. Marco Rubio, his secretary of state, has set out three tests for American aid: every dollar spent must either make America safer, stronger or richer. Other big donors, including Germany and France, have announced billions of euros-worth of aid cuts too. In Britain the prime minister, Sir Keir Starmer, has said the country’s aid cuts were to fund higher defence spending.</p><p><img src="/images/articles/TE-2026-03-28-00124.jpg"/></p><p>What impact will this have? Analysis by members of Bond, an <span>NGO</span> group, last year predicted that the announced 24% cut in British funding of Gavi, an international vaccination programme, and a 15% cut to the Global Fund to Fight <span>AIDS</span>, Tuberculosis and Malaria would lead to at least 500,000 fewer lives saved over four years. A 30% reduction in Britain’s aid programmes for sexual health could possibly mean an additional 600,000 unsafe abortions across Africa over three years.</p><p>Britain’s reputation has taken a hit. Aid has always been an instrument of soft power, giving “tremendous access” to governments, notes Nicholas Westcott, a former ambassador to several African countries. Currently, 11 countries receive £100m-plus in aid, down from 21 in 2013, after adjusting for inflation. Countries such as China and Russia can pose as more reliable finance and security partners, without replacing contributions to vital aid sectors such as health and education.</p><p>But the upheaval also presents an opportunity to do things better. Criticism of aid’s shortcomings in lifting countries out of poverty—that it creates dependency and removes incentives for real change—had been mounting. A sharper focus on areas where aid efforts have proved most effective, primarily humanitarian emergencies and health crises, makes sense.</p><p>Jenny Chapman, the development minister, has been crafting a new approach with fewer resources. In future a higher share of <span>ODA</span> will be channelled through fewer multilateral organisations (such as Gavi and the Global Fund) which are generally better at raising the money, tackling cross-border issues and reducing duplication. They are more efficient and transparent too: when <span>CGD</span> created its “quality of <span>ODA</span>’<span>’</span> index in 2021, the top five donors were all multilaterals. One of them, the African Development Fund, stands out for being African-led and able to gain access to regions that bilateral aid cannot.</p><p><span>Smaller but smarter</span></p><p>Lady Chapman has prioritised humanitarian aid in conflict-stricken countries. As for boosting economic growth in poor countries, she has called for a shift from being a “donor” to an “investor” via British International Investment, the government’s development-finance body. This reflects similar thinking in America and France, based on a belief that financing local businesses can spark growth that sustains itself. A new era of shrunken <span>ODA</span> budgets will mean, with luck, that poor countries evolve from being “recipients” to becoming business-led “partners”.</p><p>A report from Chatham House, a think-tank based in London, urges Britain to team up with like-minded donors such as Australia, Canada, the <span>EU</span> and Japan. They could pool resources, cut duplication and co-operate more closely—a <span>NATO</span> for aid, in effect. Schemes such as the Global Clean Power Alliance, a British-led initiative with 12 countries and the African Union, already point the way forward. Britain, which is hosting an international-development conference in May and presiding over the <span>G</span>20 next year, is well placed to facilitate this shift to the next, harder-headed era of aid. ■</p><p><span>For more expert analysis of the biggest stories in Britain, </span><a href="https://www.economist.com/newsletters/blighty"><span>sign up</span></a><span> to Blighty, our weekly subscriber-only newsletter.</span></p>6:["slug","britains-foreign-aid-morphs-from-open-handed-to-hard-headed-TE-2026-03-28","d"]
